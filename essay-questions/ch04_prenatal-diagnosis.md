## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Antenatal Hydronephrosis Grading and Postnatal Protocol [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 28-year-old primigravida is referred to the foetal medicine unit at 20 weeks' gestation following detection of a bright kidney on the right with an anteroposterior renal pelvis diameter (APD) of 12 mm on routine anomaly scan. The left kidney has an APD of 5 mm. Amniotic fluid volume is normal. The parents are anxious and ask what this means for their baby after birth.

**(a)** Describe the SFU (Society for Fetal Urology) grading system for antenatal hydronephrosis, and classify this foetus using the system. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Explain the ESPU/ESPU antenatal hydronephrosis classification, including the role of APD thresholds and calyceal dilatation in determining severity. **[4 marks]**

**(c)** Outline the postnatal surveillance protocol you would recommend for this infant, including the timing and choice of investigations. **[6 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the natural history of antenatally detected hydronephrosis, including the likelihood of spontaneous resolution versus the need for surgical intervention, stratified by grade. **[5 marks]**

**(e)** Justify the role of prophylactic antibiotics in the neonatal period for infants with antenatal hydronephrosis, referencing current evidence and guideline recommendations. **[2 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [5 marks] — Award 1 mark per valid point up to 5:
• SFU Grade I: isolated renal pelvis dilatation (APD <10 mm, no calyceal dilatation) [1]
• SFU Grade II: renal pelvis dilatation + mild calyceal dilatation (APD 10-15 mm) [1]
• SFU Grade III: moderate calyceal dilatation with parenchymal thinning [1]
• SFU Grade IV: severe calyceal dilatation with cortical thinning [1]
• This foetus has right APD 12 mm ± calyceal involvement → SFU Grade II-III [1]

**(b)** [4 marks]
• ESPU classification uses APD thresholds stratified by gestational age [1]
• Second trimester (16-27 weeks): APD ≥7 mm is abnormal; ≥10 mm is significant [1]
• Third trimester (≥28 weeks): APD ≥9 mm is abnormal [1]
• Classification also considers calyceal dilatation (major/minor calyces) and parenchymal thickness [1]

**(c)** [6 marks]
• Postnatal ultrasound at day 3-7 of life (not earlier — relative oliguria in first 48 hours may mask hydronephrosis) [1]
• If US shows SFU ≥III or APD ≥15 mm → MCU and MAG3 renogram at 4-6 weeks [1]
• If US shows SFU I-II with APD 7-15 mm → repeat US at 4-6 weeks [1]
• If persistent or worsening → MCU and MAG3 [1]
• Prophylactic antibiotics (trimethoprim or amoxicillin) until VCUG rules out VUR [1]
• If bilateral high-grade hydronephrosis or single kidney → earlier and more aggressive assessment [1]

**(d)** [5 marks]
• Overall resolution rate: ~80% for mild (SFU I-II); ~50% for moderate (SFU III); ~15% for severe (SFU IV) [1]
• Natural history: many resolve by age 2; most by age 5 [1]
• Need for surgical intervention: SFU I-II ~5%; SFU III ~25-30%; SFU IV ~50-70% [1]
• Resolution depends on underlying aetiology: transient hydronephrosis > PUJ obstruction > VUR > UVJ obstruction [1]
• Worse prognosis if associated with: duplicated system, ureterocele, posterior urethral valves [1]

**(e)** [2 marks]
• NICE recommends prophylactic antibiotics (trimethoprim 2 mg/kg/day) for infants with SFU ≥III or APD ≥15 mm pending VCUG [1]
• ESPU guidelines: consider prophylaxis for moderate-severe hydronephrosis while awaiting postnatal imaging [1]
• Evidence base: limited RCT data; practice based on risk of febrile UTI in obstructed systems [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER (full-mark response):**
The SFU grading system assigns severity from I-IV based on the degree of pelvicalyceal dilatation and parenchymal thinning. This foetus has right APD 12 mm, consistent with SFU Grade II-III depending on calyceal involvement. The ESPU system uses APD thresholds by gestational age — 12 mm at 20 weeks is significant and warrants postnatal follow-up by protocol.

Postnatally, I would plan a renal ultrasound at days 3-7, not before day 2 due to neonatal oliguria. If the ultrasound shows SFU ≥III or APD ≥15 mm, I would proceed to MCU and MAG3 renogram at 4-6 weeks. Prophylactic antibiotics should be started from birth pending these investigations.

The natural history is favourable for mild-moderate cases — ~80% of SFU I-II resolve spontaneously within 2 years. However, this foetus has unilateral SFU Grade II-III, so the risk of needing surgery is around 25-30%, typically for PUJ obstruction if it becomes symptomatic or if differential function deteriorates. The normal left kidney and normal amniotic fluid volume are reassuring for overall renal prognosis.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES OF AN EXCELLENT ANSWER:**
• Distinguishes between SFU and ESPU classification systems rather than treating them as interchangeable
• Specifies the exact postnatal day for US and explains WHY (neonatal oliguria)
• Quotes resolution/surgery rates stratified by grade, not just generic percentages
• Mentions prophylactic antibiotic choice and dosing

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Recommending postnatal US on day 1 of life (before postnatal diuresis has occurred)
• Not knowing the APD thresholds
• Recommending MCU/MAG3 for all cases regardless of severity
• Omitting prophylactic antibiotics entirely

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Counselling in Antenatally Detected Urological Anomalies [Total: 21 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 32-year-old woman at 22 weeks' gestation is referred to the paediatric urology team following detection of a multicystic dysplastic kidney (MCDK) on the left side of her foetus. The right kidney appears normal with adequate amniotic fluid volume. The parents are distressed and are considering termination of pregnancy. They have been told the baby "will have only one kidney."

**(a)** Explain the embryological basis of MCDK, including the genetic and molecular pathways involved. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Outline the typical ultrasound features that distinguish MCDK from severe hydronephrosis and from cystic renal disease. **[4 marks]**

**(c)** Counsel the parents regarding the prognosis for a unilateral MCDK with a normal contralateral kidney, including the expected postnatal management and long-term outcomes. **[6 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the indications for, and controversy surrounding, termination of pregnancy for prenatally diagnosed unilateral renal anomalies. **[4 marks]**

**(e)** State which additional investigations the parents should be offered to exclude associated anomalies, and give your reasons. **[2 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [5 marks]
• MCDK results from atresia of the ureteric bud early in development (~8-10 weeks) [1]
• Failure of ureteric bud branching leads to failure of metanephric mesenchymal induction [1]
• Histology: cysts lined by cuboidal epithelium separated by dysplastic stroma [1]
• Associated with contralateral anomalies in ~30-40% (VUR, PUJ obstruction) [1]
• Familial forms linked to PAX2, UMOD, HNF1B gene mutations [1]

**(b)** [4 marks]
• MCDK: multiple non-communicating cysts of varying size, no identifiable renal pelvis, no parenchyma [1]
• Severe hydronephrosis: single dilated central cystic space (pelvis) with communicating calyces ± cortical rim [1]
• Cystic renal disease (ARPKD): bilaterally enlarged echogenic kidneys with small cysts [1]
• Doppler: MCDK has absent/atrophic renal artery; hydronephrosis has patent renal vessels [1]

**(c)** [6 marks]
• Excellent prognosis if contralateral kidney is normal — most children live normal lives with no dietary restriction [1]
• Contralateral kidney undergoes compensatory hypertrophy (detectable prenatally or in first 6 months) [1]
• Postnatal management: confirm with US ± DMSA at 1-3 months [1]
• MCU recommended to rule out contralateral VUR (present in ~20-25%) [1]
• MCDK involutes spontaneously over years; serial US at 1, 3, 5 years [1]
• Long-term: very low risk of hypertension (~1% in childhood); no increased malignancy risk above general population; annual BP monitoring [1]

**(d)** [4 marks]
• Termination for unilateral MCDK is not medically indicated — survival and quality of life are normal [1]
• Abortion Act (UK) requires risk of "serious handicap" — unilateral MCDK does not meet this threshold [1]
• Parents may still request termination; counselling should be nondirective but factual [1]
• Only exception: if associated with other life-limiting anomalies or bilateral MCDK (incompatible with life) [1]

**(e)** [2 marks]
• Detailed anomaly scan looking for VACTERL-associated anomalies [1]
• Offer amniocentesis for genetic testing (chromosomal microarray/HNF1B deletion) particularly if other anomalies present [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER (full-mark response):**
MCDK results from ureteric bud atresia at ~8-10 weeks, preventing metanephric induction and leading to non-functioning dysplastic tissue. On ultrasound, it appears as multiple non-communicating cysts with no identifiable renal pelvis or parenchyma — distinct from hydronephrosis where there is a single communicating system.

The prognosis is excellent with a normal contralateral kidney. The remaining kidney undergoes compensatory hypertrophy and provides normal renal function. Postnatal management involves confirming the diagnosis with US ± DMSA at 1-3 months, performing an MCU to exclude contralateral VUR (~20% risk), and serial US surveillance as the MCDK involutes. Blood pressure monitoring is recommended, though hypertension risk in childhood is <1%.

I would reassure the parents that unilateral MCDK does not warrant termination — children live completely normal lives with no activity restriction. The parents should be offered a detailed anomaly screen and genetic counselling, but in isolation, this is not a reason to terminate.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES OF AN EXCELLENT ANSWER:**
• Discusses the legal framework (Abortion Act 1967 / serious handicap criterion)
• Mentions specific gene associations (HNF1B, PAX2) rather than generic "genetic factors"
• Gives specific surveillance intervals (1, 3, 5 years for US)

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Telling parents the child will be "disabled" or will need medication
• Confusing MCDK with polycystic kidney disease
• Missing the need for MCU to exclude contralateral VUR
• Not mentioning that MCDK involutes over time

## QUESTION 3 — SAQ: Prenatally Diagnosed Renal Anomalies [Total: 8 marks] [FOUNDATION]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 20-week foetus has bilateral echogenic kidneys with reduced amniotic fluid volume (oligohydramnios).

**(a)** List three likely differential diagnoses for bilateral echogenic kidneys with oligohydramnios. **[3 marks]**

**(b)** Describe the postnatal outcome for each of the diagnoses listed in (a). **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [3 marks — 1 per correct differential]
• Autosomal recessive polycystic kidney disease (ARPKD) [1]
• Bilateral MCDK [1]
• Bilateral renal dysplasia/hypoplasia with chronic kidney disease [1]
• (Also acceptable: obstructive uropathy such as PUV with renal dysplasia, or congenital nephrotic syndrome)

**(b)** [5 marks]
• ARPKD: poor neonatal outcome — respiratory distress from pulmonary hypoplasia, hypertension, progressive CKD; many require dialysis/transplantation in childhood [1-2]
• Bilateral MCDK: incompatible with life — Potter sequence (pulmonary hypoplasia, limb deformities, facies) → death from respiratory failure shortly after birth if severe oligohydramnios before 24 weeks [1-2]
• Bilateral renal dysplasia: spectrum from severe (neonatal death) to moderate (CKD progressing to ESRD in childhood); depends on degree of amniotic fluid volume and pulmonary development [1-2]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Listing unilateral MCDK (not typically associated with oligohydramnios)
• Not recognising Potter sequence as a consequence of severe oligohydramnios
• Overly optimistic prognostication when severe oligohydramnios is present

## QUESTION 4 — SAQ: Antenatal Counselling [Total: 8 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
You are counselling parents at 28 weeks' gestation. The foetus has bilateral SFU Grade III hydronephrosis with a thickened bladder wall and a dilated posterior urethra (keyhole sign).

**(a)** What is the most likely diagnosis, and what additional prenatal imaging finding would you specifically look for to prognosticate? **[2 marks]**

**(b)** State three poor prognostic indicators for long-term renal function in this condition. **[3 marks]**

**(c)** What is the role of prenatal intervention (vesicoamniotic shunting) in this setting, and what evidence supports its use? **[3 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [2 marks]
• Diagnosis: posterior urethral valves (PUV) [1]
• Additional finding: renal cortical echogenicity/cysts on foetal US (indicating renal dysplasia) [1]

**(b)** [3 marks — 1 each]
• Early diagnosis (before 24 weeks) — indicates more severe obstruction with greater impact on nephrogenesis [1]
• Evidence of renal dysplasia on US (echogenicity, cysts) — irreversible damage [1]
• Oligohydramnios / anhydramnios — indicates severe obstruction with poor nephron endowment [1]
• (Also: abnormal urinary biochemistry — Na >100, Ca >1.8, β2-microglobulin >6, osmolality >210)

**(c)** [3 marks]
• Vesicoamniotic shunting (VAS) diverts urine from bladder to amniotic cavity, restoring amniotic fluid volume to prevent pulmonary hypoplasia [1]
• PLUTO trial (2013): RCT comparing VAS to conservative management [1]
• Results: VAS improved perinatal survival (OR 1.88, not statistically significant in intention-to-treat); no difference in long-term renal survival [1]
• VAS does not prevent renal dysplasia (which has already occurred by time of diagnosis) — it primarily addresses pulmonary hypoplasia [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Overstating the benefit of vesicoamniotic shunting in preserving renal function
• Missing the keyhole sign as diagnostic of PUV prenatally
• Not knowing the PLUTO trial by name

## QUESTION 5 — LCV: Prenatal Detection of Bilateral Hydronephrosis [Total: 35 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~40 minutes

─── STAGE 1: PRENATAL DETECTION ───────────────────────────────
A 26-year-old woman (G1P0) has a routine anomaly scan at 20 weeks' gestation. The foetus shows bilateral renal pelvis dilatation (APD 10 mm on the right, 8 mm on the left). The bladder appears normal and the amniotic fluid volume is normal. No other anomalies are detected. The parents are referred to you for counselling.

**Q1a.** What is your differential diagnosis for this finding, and how would you refine it using further imaging? List the key sonographic features that discriminate between the possibilities. **[6 marks]**

**Q1b.** Outline the key points you would include in your prenatal counselling for the parents at this stage. **[4 marks]**

─── STAGE 2: LATER PRENATAL COURSE ──────────────────────────
A repeat US at 30 weeks shows progression: right APD 18 mm with mild calyceal dilatation, left APD 14 mm. The bladder wall is thickened (5 mm). The posterior urethra appears dilated with a keyhole sign. Amniotic fluid volume is at the lower limit of normal.

**Q2a.** What is the likely diagnosis, and what additional prognostic information would you seek from the foetal US? **[4 marks]**

**Q2b.** The parents ask about foetal intervention. Discuss the evidence for and against vesicoamniotic shunting, including the specific findings from the PLUTO trial. **[6 marks]**

─── STAGE 3: POSTNATAL PRESENTATION ──────────────────────────
The infant is born at 39 weeks by normal vaginal delivery. Birth weight 3.4 kg. APGARs 8 and 9. The paediatric team performs a postnatal US on day 3, which confirms bilateral SFU Grade IV hydronephrosis, bilateral hydroureters, and a thickened bladder. A catheter is passed easily and 80 mL of urine drained. Serum creatinine at 48 hours is 68 μmol/L.

**Q3a.** What is your immediate management plan for this neonate? **[4 marks]**

**Q3b.** A MCU is performed and confirms PUV. Describe the technique of primary valve ablation, including cystoscopic approach, energy source, and steps to ensure adequate ablation. **[6 marks]**

─── STAGE 4: FOLLOW-UP AND COMPLICATIONS ─────────────────────
At 6 months, the child has had two febrile UTIs despite prophylactic antibiotics. Serum creatinine is steady at 55 μmol/L. Serial US shows persistent bilateral hydroureteronephrosis. Repeat MCU shows no residual valves. MAG3 renogram shows drainage with mild hold-up bilaterally.

**Q4a.** Discuss the concept of "valve bladder syndrome" and its management options. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**FULL MARKING GUIDE WITH MODEL ANSWERS:**

**Q1a.** [6 marks]
• Transient hydronephrosis (most common — resolves prenatally or postnatally) [1]
• PUJ obstruction (unilateral or bilateral; look for renal pelvis dilatation without ureteric dilatation) [1]
• VUR (look for intermittent pelvicalyceal dilatation, ureteric dilatation, ± normal bladder) [1]
• PUV (male foetus, thickened bladder, keyhole sign, bilateral hydroureteronephrosis) [1]
• UVJ obstruction/megaureter (ureteric dilatation out of proportion to pelvis, normal bladder) [1]
• Duplex system with obstructed upper moiety (upper moiety hydroureteronephrosis ± ureterocele) [1]

**Q1b.** [4 marks]
• Most cases of mild bilateral hydronephrosis resolve spontaneously [1]
• Need for serial US surveillance through pregnancy to detect progression [1]
• Plan for postnatal follow-up: US at days 3-7, possible MCU and MAG3 if persistent [1]
• Reassure: normal amniotic fluid volume is a good prognostic indicator [1]

**Q2a.** [4 marks]
• Diagnosis: posterior urethral valves (PUV) — based on keyhole sign, thickened bladder, progression [1]
• Prognostic features on US: renal cortical echogenicity (dysplasia), presence of cortical cysts, parenchymal thickness [1]
• Amniotic fluid volume (now at lower limit — further reduction would worsen prognosis) [1]
• Foetal urinary biochemistry (if considered): Na >100, Ca >1.8, β2-microglobulin >6 — poor prognosis [1]

**Q2b.** [6 marks]
• VAS rationale: restore amniotic fluid to prevent pulmonary hypoplasia [1]
• PLUTO trial (2013): multicentre RCT, closed early due to poor recruitment [1]
• Intention-to-treat: 50% survival with VAS vs 34% without (OR 1.88, p=0.27 — not statistically significant) [1]
• Per-protocol analysis showed significant survival benefit [1]
• No difference in renal function at 1 year between survivors [1]
• Complications: shunt migration, occlusion, preterm labour, chorioamnionitis [1]
• Therefore: VAS may improve survival but does NOT prevent renal dysplasia — counselling should be realistic [1]

**Q3a.** [4 marks]
• Catheter drainage (5Fr feeding tube) — leave on free drainage [1]
• Monitor urine output and serum creatinine daily [1]
• IV fluids to match urine output plus insensible losses [1]
• Prophylactic antibiotics (co-amoxiclav or trimethoprim) [1]
• Arrange MCU when stable and infection-free [1]

**Q3b.** [6 marks]
• General anaesthesia with caudal/penile block [1]
• Position: lithotomy [1]
• Cystoscope (6-8Fr) with 0° or 30° lens [1]
• Identify valves as leaflets arising from verumontanum extending anterolaterally [1]
• Ablation technique: Bugbee electrode (3Fr) at 15-20W cutting current [1]
• Alternative: cold knife, laser (Ho:YAG) — less tissue trauma [1]
• Ablate at 5, 7, and 12 o'clock positions [1]
• Avoid cautery to verumontanum (preserve antegrade ejaculation) and external sphincter [1]
• Confirm adequate ablation by visualising unobstructed flow during bladder expression [1]

**Q4a.** [5 marks]
• Valve bladder syndrome: persistence of abnormal bladder function after valve ablation [1]
• Causes: polyuria from nephrogenic diabetes insipidus, bladder wall hypertrophy, detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia [1]
• Features: hydroureteronephrosis despite unobstructed urethra, VUR, UTIs, incontinence [1]
• Management: optimized bladder drainage (CIC if incomplete emptying) [1]
• Anticholinergics (oxybutynin) for detrusor overactivity [1]
• α-blockers (tamsulosin) to reduce bladder outlet resistance [1]
• Double voiding or timed voiding regimen [1]
• In refractory cases: bladder augmentation (enterocystoplasty) or Mitrofanoff channel [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES OF AN EXCELLENT ANSWER:**
• Discusses valve bladder syndrome as a separate long-term condition, not just "PUV that wasn't fixed properly"
• Knows the PLUTO trial details and limitations specifically
• Describes actual surgical technique with energy settings and endoscopic approach
• Mentions preservation of verumontanum for future fertility

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Not distinguishing between transient hydronephrosis and pathological obstruction
• Recommending VAS as a definitive treatment for PUV
• Describing valve ablation without specific technical details
• Not anticipating valve bladder syndrome in follow-up

## QUESTION 6 — EMQ: Prenatal Diagnosis of Urinary Tract Anomalies [Total: 10 marks] [BOARD]

**Theme: Evaluation of Antenatal Urological Findings**

**OPTIONS (each option may be used once, more than once, or not at all):**

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| A. Posterior urethral valves | F. Multicystic dysplastic kidney |
| B. PUJ obstruction | G. Autosomal recessive polycystic kidney disease |
| C. UVJ obstruction / primary megaureter | H. Normal variant / transient hydronephrosis |
| D. Duplex kidney with upper moiety obstruction | I. Cloacal anomaly |
| E. Ureterocele | J. Prune belly syndrome (triad syndrome) |

**For each of the following clinical scenarios, select the single most appropriate option from the list above:**

**1.** A 22-week foetus has a left-sided intrarenal cystic mass with multiple non-communicating cysts of varying sizes. The right kidney appears normal. Amniotic fluid volume is normal. The left renal artery cannot be identified on Doppler ultrasound. **[1 mark]**

**2.** A 28-week foetus has bilateral hydroureteronephrosis with a thickened bladder wall and a dilated posterior urethra. The amniotic fluid volume is moderately reduced. **[1 mark]**

**3.** A 24-week foetus has severe oligohydramnios. Both kidneys are symmetrically enlarged (above 95th centile) and markedly echogenic, with poor corticomedullary differentiation but no identifiable cysts at this stage. **[1 mark]**

**4.** A 20-week foetus has right-sided renal pelvis dilatation (APD 14 mm) without ureteric dilatation. The left kidney and bladder are normal. Amniotic fluid is normal. Repeat US at 28 weeks shows improvement (APD 8 mm). **[1 mark]**

**5.** A 32-week foetus has a left-sided duplex system. The upper moiety shows hydroureteronephrosis and there is a thin-walled cystic structure projecting into the bladder. **[1 mark]**

**6.** A 22-week foetus has massive bilateral hydroureteronephrosis with a large, distended, thin-walled bladder. The abdominal musculature appears deficient on US. The infant is male. **[1 mark]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**ANSWERS & RATIONALE:**

**1. F** — MCDK. The multiple non-communicating cysts of varying size, absent renal artery on Doppler, and normal contralateral kidney are classic. Normal amniotic fluid confirms the contralateral kidney is functioning.

**2. A** — Posterior urethral valves. The triad of bilateral hydroureteronephrosis + thickened bladder + dilated posterior urethra (keyhole sign) is diagnostic. Reduced amniotic fluid reflects impaired foetal renal function.

**3. G** — ARPKD. Symmetrically enlarged, echogenic kidneys with oligohydramnios are hallmarks. Cysts may not be visible in utero early on. This is a life-limiting condition when presenting this early with severe oligohydramnios.

**4. H** — Normal variant / transient hydronephrosis. The absence of ureteric dilatation, improvement on follow-up, and normal amniotic fluid indicate a self-limiting finding. The most common prenatal renal anomaly.

**5. E** — Ureterocele. The duplex system with upper moiety hydroureteronephrosis and the thin-walled cystic structure in the bladder are classic for a ureterocele associated with an obstructed upper moiety.

**6. J** — Prune belly syndrome (triad syndrome). The triad of massive bilateral hydroureteronephrosis, distended thin-walled bladder, and deficient abdominal musculature is diagnostic. This is almost exclusively male.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Confusing MCDK with severe hydronephrosis — the non-communicating cysts on US and absent renal artery Doppler are key discriminators
• Missing ureterocele in a duplex with upper moiety obstruction
• Not recognising prune belly syndrome from the classic triad

**EXAMINER'S NOTES — COMMON SCRIPT PATTERNS TO WATCH FOR:**
• Candidates often fail to distinguish between prenatal and postnatal management pathways — many describe postnatal imaging protocols when asked for prenatal counselling
• The role of VAS is frequently overestimated — candidates should understand that it treats pulmonary hypoplasia, not renal dysplasia
• Many candidates do not know the ESPU/SFU grading systems by their full names or the specific APD thresholds
• The PLUTO trial is expected knowledge at this level; omission of trial name and key findings is a discriminator between pass and distinction
