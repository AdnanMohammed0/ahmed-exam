## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Phimosis [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 5-year-old boy is referred by his GP because his foreskin "will not retract." His mother reports he has difficulty aiming his stream, and the foreskin balloons during voiding. He has had two episodes of balanitis treated with topical antibiotics. There is no history of UTI or urinary retention.

**(a)** Distinguish between physiological and pathological phimosis, including the age at which each typically presents and the clinical features that differentiate them. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Describe the clinical features of balanitis xerotica obliterans (BXO)/lichen sclerosus and its management. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Compare preputioplasty with circumcision for the treatment of pathological phimosis, including success rates, complications, and the evidence base for each. **[6 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the non-surgical management options for phimosis, including topical steroid therapy, with specific reference to duration, success rates, and patient selection. **[6 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [5 marks] — 1 per valid point up to 5:
• Physiological: ballooning but can express urine, no scarring, normal for age <3-5 years [1]
• Ballooning alone is not pathological [1]
• Pathological: scarring/phimotic ring, whitish fibrotic band at tip, inability to retract after age 5 [1]
• BXO: characteristic porcelain-white discolouration, firm phimotic ring, meatal involvement [1]
• BXO does NOT resolve spontaneously and requires treatment [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Clinical: white/brown atrophic patches on prepuce/glans, firm phimotic ring, may extend to meatus [1]
• If meatal involved: meatal stenosis (pinpoint stream, difficulty voiding) [1]
• Biopsy: hyperkeratosis, atrophy of stratum spinosum, liquefactive degeneration of basal layer, dermal homogenisation [1]
• Medical: high-potency topical steroids (clobetasol 0.05% BD for 4-8 weeks) [1]
• Surgical: circumcision + meatal dilatation/meatotomy if meatal stenosis [1]

**(c)** [6 marks]
• Preputioplasty: dorsal slit or Y-V plasty — preserves foreskin, short procedure, day surgery [1]
   • Success: ~90-95% for primary phimosis without BXO [1]
   • Recurrence: ~5-10% — higher if underlying BXO [1]
• Circumcision: definitive removal of prepuce — gold standard for BXO, near-zero recurrence [1]
   • Success: 99%+ for phimosis [1]
   • Complications: bleeding, infection, meatal ulcer (5-10%), penile shortening, loss of sensation [1]
• Preputioplasty preferred if parents wish to preserve foreskin; circumcision if BXO or recurrent [1]

**(d)** [6 marks]
• Topical steroids: betamethasone 0.05% or clobetasol 0.05% BD applied to phimotic ring for 4-8 weeks [1]
• Mechanism: anti-inflammatory, reduces fibrosis, allows gradual retraction [1]
• Success: ~70-80% in physiological/phimosis without BXO [1]
• Lower success in BXO (<50%) [1]
• Apply with gentle retraction during bathing; continue for 2-4 weeks after successful retraction [1]
• Contraindicated if active infection [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
At age 5, ballooning alone is physiological. However, scarring and a phimotic ring indicate pathological phimosis. I would examine for BXO (white patches, firm ring). First-line treatment is topical clobetasol 0.05% BD for 6 weeks with gentle retraction during bathing. If this fails, options are preputioplasty (preserves foreskin, 90-95% success) or circumcision (definitive, >99% success). If BXO is present, circumcision is preferred as preputioplasty has higher recurrence.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Distinguishes physiological from pathological phimosis using age and physical findings
• Describes BXO histology — a strong differentiator
• Quotes specific success rates for preputioplasty vs circumcision
• Gives specific steroid regimen with duration

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Balanoposthitis and Meatal Stenosis [Total: 20 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 3-year-old boy presents with his first episode of balanoposthitis (red, swollen prepuce with purulent discharge). He is systemically well and afebrile. He has no previous history.

**(a)** Describe the clinical features, microbiology, and management of acute balanoposthitis in a child. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** A 6-year-old boy who was circumcised in infancy presents with dysuria and a pinpoint urinary stream. What is the diagnosis and how is it managed? **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Discuss the evidence for and against routine neonatal circumcision, including the medical benefits, risks, and ethical considerations. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** Outline the technique of circumcision, including the specific steps to avoid complications. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [5 marks] — 1 per valid point up to 5:
• Balanoposthitis: inflammation of glans (balanitis) + prepuce (posthitis) [1]
• Typically polymicrobial: skin flora, Candida, sometimes GAS [1]
• Management: warm saline soaks, topical antibiotic/antifungal (e.g., clotrimazole + hydrocortisone) [1]
• If febrile/systemic: oral antibiotics (co-amoxiclav) [1]
• Recurrent episodes → consider definitive treatment (circumcision) [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Diagnosis: meatal stenosis — acquired scar at meatus after circumcision [1]
• Typically presents 1-2 years after circumcision [1]
• Clinical: pinpoint stream, dysuria, upward deflection of stream [1]
• Management: meatotomy (dorsal slit or ventral incision) [1]
• Alternatively: meatal dilatation (bougie) for mild cases [1]

**(c)** [5 marks]
• MEDICAL benefits: reduced risk of UTI in infancy (RR 0.2-0.4), reduced penile cancer (RR 0.2), reduced HIV/STI transmission [1]
• Risks: bleeding (0.2%), infection (0.4%), meatal stenosis (5-10%), removal of erogenous tissue, surgical complications [1]
• Ethics: non-therapeutic surgery on a minor who cannot consent; parental vs child rights [1]
• NICE: not routinely recommended; decision should be informed and made by parents after counselling [1]
• AAP (2012): benefits outweigh risks but does not recommend routine; parents should decide [1]

**(d)** [5 marks]
• Assess child's anatomy, ensure adequate light [1]
• Suture traction at 12 and 6 o'clock on prepuce [1]
• Incise dorsal slit to 2-3 mm from corona [1]
• Excise inner and outer prepuce layers separately [1]
• Haemostasis with bipolar diathermy; close mucosa to skin with 5-0 absorbable sutures [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
Acute balanoposthitis is managed conservatively with hygiene, topical antifungals, and oral antibiotics only if febrile. Post-circumcision meatal stenosis requires meatotomy. The circumcision debate balances marginal medical benefit (reduced UTI, penile cancer) against the ethical concern of non-therapeutic surgery. Technique is critical: avoid excessive skin removal, achieve meticulous haemostasis, and ensure mucosal approximation.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Discusses the circumcision debate with reference to official body positions (NICE, AAP)
• Describes specific circumcision technique steps
• Recognises meatal stenosis as a post-circumcision complication
• Quotes specific complication rates

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [4 marks]
• Retract phimotic ring gently as far as it allows [1]
• Apply artery forceps at 12 o'clock on phimotic ring [1]
• Incise between forceps through phimotic ring (dorsal slit) [1]
• Suture transversely or leave to heal — achieves widening of the phimotic band [1]

**(b)** [4 marks — 2 each]
• Parental preference to preserve foreskin (cultural/religious) [2]
• Mild-to-moderate phimosis without BXO [2]

## QUESTION 4 — SAQ: Circumcision Complications [Total: 8 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** List four recognised complications of circumcision. **[4 marks]**

**(b)** State how each can be avoided or managed. **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a) & (b)** [4 marks — 1 each correct pair]
• Bleeding — avoid by careful haemostasis; manage with pressure or suture ligation [1]
• Infection — aseptic technique, prophylactic antibiotics in high-risk patients; antibiotics if infected [1]
• Skin loss/excess removal — measure twice; preserve adequate skin for closure; skin graft if severe [1]
• Meatal stenosis — avoid circumcision too early; meatotomy if develops [1]
• (Also: glans amputation — rare, catastrophic; reconstruction if partial, replantation unlikely)

## QUESTION 5 — LCV: BXO [Total: 35 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~40 minutes

─── STAGE 1: PRESENTATION ───────────────────────────────
A 9-year-old uncircumcised boy presents with difficulty retracting his foreskin. On examination, there is a white, firm phimotic ring. The glans has porcelain-white patches. He has had two episodes of balanitis.

**Q1a.** What is the most likely diagnosis? **[1 mark]**
**Q1b.** What is the natural history of this condition if left untreated? **[3 marks]**

─── STAGE 2: TREATMENT ─────────────────────────────────
Topical clobetasol fails. He undergoes circumcision. At surgery, the meatus appears stenotic.

**Q2a.** Describe the operative steps for circumcision + meatotomy. **[6 marks]**
**Q2b.** What post-operative care and follow-up is required? **[4 marks]**

─── STAGE 3: COMPLICATION ──────────────────────────────
At 6 months post-op, he presents with a pinpoint stream and dysuria. The meatus appears scarred.

**Q3a.** What is the diagnosis and what factors may have contributed? **[3 marks]**
**Q3b.** Compare urethral dilatation, meatotomy, and meatoplasty as treatment options. **[5 marks]**

─── STAGE 4: LONG-TERM ─────────────────────────────────
At 15 years, he is asymptomatic with a good stream. He is concerned about the appearance of his circumcision and feels the scar is unsightly.

**Q4a.** Discuss the psychological impact of genital surgery in childhood. **[3 marks]**
**Q4b.** Outline management options for unsatisfactory circumcision scar revision. **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**FULL MARKING GUIDE:**

**Q1a.** [1 mark]
• Balanitis xerotica obliterans (BXO) / lichen sclerosus et atrophicus

**Q1b.** [3 marks]
• Progressive phimosis → complete non-retractability [1]
• Meatal involvement → stenosis, voiding difficulty, upstream dilatation (hydronephrosis in severe cases) [1]
• Penile carcinoma in situ (penile intraepithelial neoplasia) — rare but recognised long-term association [1]

**Q2a.** [6 marks]
• Dorsal slit of phimotic ring [1]
• Complete circumcision with excision of all affected prepuce [1]
• Inspect meatus — calibrate with 10Fr; if tight → meatotomy [1]
• Meatotomy: incise stenotic meatus at 6 o'clock (ventral) to 12-14Fr [1]
• Approximate meatal edges with 6-0/7-0 absorbable sutures [1]
• Catheter optional — if inserted, remove after 24 hours [1]

**Q2b.** [4 marks]
• Topical antibiotic ointment to suture line [1]
• Analgesia and stool softeners [1]
• Review at 2 weeks for wound check [1]
• Follow-up uroflowmetry at 6 months to monitor for recurrence [1]

**Q3a.** [3 marks]
• Recurrent meatal stenosis [1]
• Contributing factors: BXO is a progressive disease — may recur in meatus [1]
• Inadequate initial meatotomy, poor wound healing, suture reaction [1]

**Q3b.** [5 marks]
• Dilatation: serial bougie dilatation — temporary, high recurrence [1]
• Meatotomy: repeat ventral incision — success ~60-70% [1]
• Meatoplasty: excision of stenotic segment with advancement of urethral mucosa — more definitive [1]
• Urethroplasty (graft): if stricture extends into fossa navicularis [1]
• Recurrence lowest with meatoplasty/urethroplasty [1]

**Q4a.** [3 marks]
• Genital surgery can cause body image concerns, especially in adolescence [1]
• Scar appearance, penile length concerns, sensitivity changes [1]
• Important to address proactively; refer to psychologist if needed [1]

**Q4b.** [4 marks]
• Revision circumcision: excise prominent scar, re-approximate skin [1]
• Release of buried penis if skin bridges/fibrosis [1]
• V-Y plasty/ Z-plasty if scar contracture [1]
• Patience — allow maturation of scar >12 months before revision [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Recognises BXO as a progressive disease that can recur in meatus even after circumcision
• Describes meatotomy with specific calibration size (10Fr → 12-14Fr)
• Differentiates dilatation, meatotomy, and meatoplasty
• Addresses psychological impact of genital surgery in children/adolescents

## QUESTION 6 — EMQ: Prepuce and Foreskin Pathology [Total: 10 marks] [BOARD]

**Theme: Foreskin Pathology in Children**

**OPTIONS (each option may be used once, more than once, or not at all):**

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| A. Physiological phimosis | F. Preputial adhesions |
| B. Pathological phimosis (non-BXO) | G. Paraphimosis |
| C. BXO / lichen sclerosus | H. Balanoposthitis |
| D. Meatal stenosis | I. Penile torsion |
| E. Phimosis with ballooning | J. Smegma retention cyst |

**For each of the following clinical scenarios, select the single most appropriate diagnosis:**

**1.** An 18-month-old boy whose foreskin cannot be retracted. The prepuce is soft with no scarring. Ballooning occurs during voiding but the urine stream is strong. **[1 mark]**

**2.** A 7-year-old who develops a swollen, painful glans after his mother retracted his foreskin during bathing and could not pull it back. **[1 mark]**

**3.** A 4-year-old with recurrent redness and swelling of the prepuce with purulent discharge. Responds to hygiene and topical antibiotics. **[1 mark]**

**4.** A 6-year-old circumcised boy with a pinpoint stream, dysuria, and upward deflection of the urine stream. **[1 mark]**

**5.** A 10-year-old with a white, firm phimotic ring. The glans has porcelain-white patches. He has had recurrent balanitis. **[1 mark]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**ANSWERS & RATIONALE:**

**1.** **A** — Physiological phimosis. The foreskin cannot retract because of physiological adhesions between the prepuce and glans. At 18 months, this is normal. Ballooning is also normal. No scarring = reassurance.

**2.** **G** — Paraphimosis. The retracted foreskin cannot be reduced over the corona of the glans, causing constriction of the glans. This is a medical emergency requiring reduction (typically under anaesthesia or with osmotic reduction).

**3.** **H** — Balanoposthitis. Recurrent episodes of inflammation of the glans and prepuce, responding to conservative measures. Recurrent episodes may indicate need for circumcision if severe.

**4.** **D** — Meatal stenosis. The classic post-circumcision meatal stenosis presenting with pinpoint stream and upward deflection. Meatotomy is the treatment.

**5.** **C** — BXO / lichen sclerosus. The porcelain-white discolouration and firm phimotic ring with recurrent balanitis in a school-age child is classic BXO. Circumcision is definitive treatment.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Treating physiological phimosis as pathological — many candidates want to intervene in toddlers with non-retractile foreskin
• Confusing paraphimosis with phimosis — they are opposite conditions
• Missing BXO when recurrent balanitis and a white phimotic ring are present
• Forgetting that meatal stenosis occurs after circumcision, not before

**EXAMINER'S NOTES — COMMON SCRIPT PATTERNS TO WATCH FOR:**
• Many candidates cannot distinguish physiological from pathological phimosis by age
• BXO is frequently mentioned but the histology is rarely known — it is a strong discriminator
• Preputioplasty is under-utilised in exam answers; candidates often jump straight to circumcision
• Meatal stenosis is a well-known complication but the management (meatotomy) is poorly described
