## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Imaging After Febrile UTI [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
An 18-month-old girl is admitted with her second culture-confirmed febrile urinary tract infection (temperature 39.5°C) within 4 months. She is now afebrile and clinically well after 48 hours of intravenous antibiotics. Urine culture grew E. coli >10⁵ CFU/mL, sensitive to trimethoprim. Renal ultrasound performed during the admission shows no abnormality.

**(a)** Outline the NICE (CG54) imaging guidelines for a child of this age with recurrent febrile UTI, including the rationale for each investigation recommended. **[6 marks]**

**(b)** Explain the technique and timing of DMSA scintigraphy in this context, and describe how you would differentiate acute pyelonephritic changes from established scarring. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** A DMSA performed 6 months later shows a photopenic area in the right upper pole. State the clinical significance of this finding and outline the evidence base for the management of renal scarring in children. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the controversy surrounding the use of prophylactic antibiotics following febrile UTI in children, with specific reference to the RIVUR and PRIVENT trials. **[6 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [6 marks] — Award 1 mark per valid point up to 6:
• NICE CG54 (2007, updated 2022): categorises by age and recurrence [1]
• Age <6 months with atypical/recurrent UTI → DMSA + MCU + US [1]
• Age 6 months–3 years with recurrent UTI → DMSA (4-6 months post-infection) + US [1]
• Age 6 months–3 years with first UTI → US only (DMSA only if atypical features) [1]
• Older children (>3 years): US only unless recurrent/atypical [1]
• MCU reserved for cases with: dilated hydronephrosis on US, poor urinary stream, non-E. coli infection, or family history of VUR [1]
• Rationale: to detect renal scarring (DMSA), anatomical abnormalities (US), and VUR (MCU) [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• DMSA (dimercaptosuccinic acid) binds to proximal renal tubules, giving static image of functional cortex [1]
• Timing: minimum 4-6 months after acute infection to avoid false positive from acute inflammation [1]
• Acute pyelonephritis: area of reduced uptake that may resolve completely on follow-up [1]
• Established scar: persistent photopenic area on delayed imaging (6+ months) with volume loss [1]
• Technique: IV injection of 99mTc-DMSA, imaging at 2-3 hours; posterior and posterior oblique views [1]

**(c)** [5 marks]
• Clinical significance: established renal cortical scar reflecting permanent parenchymal damage [1]
• Represents reflux nephropathy from previous pyelonephritis [1]
• Risk of long-term complications: hypertension (approximately 15-20% risk if bilateral or extensive scarring), proteinuria, CKD [1]
• Evidence base: no high-quality RCTs for scar management; current practice is BP monitoring and annual urine ACR [1]
• ACE inhibitors shown to reduce proteinuria and progression in children with scarring and proteinuria (ESCAPE trial) [1]

**(d)** [6 marks]
• RIVUR trial (2014): RCT of trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole prophylaxis vs placebo in children with VUR grade I-IV [1]
• RIVUR result: 50% reduction in recurrent UTI (RR 0.50, p<0.001) but no reduction in new scar formation [1]
• PRIVENT trial (2009): broader population (children after first UTI, not all with VUR) [1]
• PRIVENT result: modest reduction (6% absolute risk reduction) in symptomatic UTI; number needed to treat = 13 [1]
• Limitations: RIVUR studied selected population (all had VUR); neither study showed scar prevention benefit [1]
• Current practice: prophylaxis considered for high-risk groups (high-grade VUR, recurrent pyelonephritis, renal anomalies); not routine for first UTI [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER (full-mark response):**
NICE CG54 imaging guidance stratifies by age and recurrence. For this 18-month-old girl with recurrent febrile UTI, I would recommend a DMSA scan performed at least 4-6 months after the acute infection to assess for established scarring, and a renal ultrasound. An MCU is not required per NICE unless the ultrasound shows dilated hydronephrosis, there is a non-E. coli organism, poor urinary stream, or a family history of VUR.

The DMSA shows a photopenic area in the right upper pole at 6 months, representing an established scar. This is permanent and carries implications for long-term renal health including hypertension risk. Evidence does not support antibiotic prophylaxis for preventing new scars — the RIVUR trial showed prophylaxis reduces recurrent UTI by 50% in children with VUR but did not reduce scarring. The PRIVENT trial showed a more modest benefit in a broader population. I would therefore focus on bladder bowel dysfunction management as a modifiable risk factor and monitor blood pressure and urinalysis annually.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES OF AN EXCELLENT ANSWER:**
• Quotes the NICE age/recurrence stratification accurately rather than giving a generic "imaging after UTI" answer
• Cites RIVUR and PRIVENT by name with specific outcomes (RR 0.50, NNT 13)
• Distinguishes scar prevention from UTI prevention as separate endpoints
• Addresses the NICE updated guidance (2022) vs original 2007

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Recommending MCU for all children after first febrile UTI without regard to NICE criteria
• Performing DMSA during the acute infection
• Quoting prophylaxis as "proven to prevent scarring"
• Not knowing the distinction between RIVUR and PRIVENT trial populations

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Complicated UTI and Acute Pyelonephritis [Total: 20 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 5-year-old girl presents to the emergency department with a 3-day history of fever (39.8°C), vomiting, and right-sided abdominal pain. She appears unwell and dehydrated. Urine dipstick is strongly positive for leucocytes and nitrites. She has had one previous UTI treated as an outpatient at age 3. She has no known urinary tract abnormalities.

**(a)** List the clinical features that distinguish acute pyelonephritis from cystitis, and justify your admission decision in this case. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Describe your immediate management plan, including choice of antibiotics, fluid management, and the indications for switching from intravenous to oral therapy. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Outline the spectrum of organisms responsible for paediatric UTI according to age group, and discuss the emerging problem of antimicrobial resistance in this context. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** State how you would follow up this child after discharge, including investigations, prophylaxis decisions, and parental counselling regarding recurrence risk. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [5 marks] — 1 per valid point up to 5:
• Pyelonephritis features: fever ≥39°C, rigors, flank/loin pain, systemic upset, vomiting, dehydration [1]
• Cystitis: lower urinary tract symptoms (dysuria, frequency, urgency, suprapubic pain), usually afebrile or low-grade fever [1]
• This case: high fever, vomiting, systemic upset, right-sided abdominal pain → consistent with acute pyelonephritis [1]
• Admission justified due to: dehydration, inability to tolerate oral intake, systemic symptoms, age <6 and clinical severity [1]
• Risk of bacteraemia/sepsis in untreated pyelonephritis [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• IV fluids: 0.9% NaCl bolus 10-20 mL/kg if dehydrated, then maintenance IV fluids [1]
• IV antibiotics: co-amoxiclav 30 mg/kg TDS or cefuroxime 50 mg/kg TDS; alternative: gentamicin 7 mg/kg once daily (check levels) [1]
• Send blood cultures before antibiotics [1]
• Switch to oral when: afebrile for 24-48 hours, tolerating oral intake, clinically improving, and sensitivities available [1]
• Total course: 10-14 days antibiotic therapy (IV + oral combined) [1]

**(c)** [5 marks]
• Neonates (0-3 months): E. coli (~50%), Klebsiella, Enterococcus, Group B Streptococcus [1]
• Infants and children (3 months–12 years): E. coli (75-85%), Klebsiella, Proteus, Enterococcus [1]
• Adolescents: E. coli dominant, also Staphylococcus saprophyticus [1]
• Emerging resistance: ESBL-producing E. coli increasingly common (10-20% in some centres) [1]
• Co-amoxiclav resistance rates rising; local antibiogram should guide empirical choice [1]

**(d)** [5 marks]
• Renal ultrasound to exclude anatomical abnormality [1]
• DMSA scan at 4-6 months to assess for scarring [1]
• MCU only if: dilated ureters on US, recurrent pyelonephritis, non-E. coli UTIs, family history of VUR [1]
• Prophylaxis not routinely indicated after first episode; consider if recurrent or high-grade VUR [1]
• Counselling: adequate fluid intake, complete bladder emptying, avoid constipation, perineal hygiene [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This child has acute pyelonephritis based on high fever, systemic upset, and flank pain — distinct from lower UTI. Immediate management requires IV fluids and IV antibiotics (co-amoxiclav or third-generation cephalosporin, adjusted by local sensitivity patterns). She should be switched to oral therapy once afebrile and tolerating feeds, completing a 10-14-day course.

The microbiology of paediatric UTI is age-dependent, with E. coli dominant across all groups but emerging ESBL resistance requiring vigilance. Follow-up should include renal ultrasound and DMSA at 4-6 months. I would not routinely give prophylaxis after a single episode but would educate the family on recurrence risk and the importance of bladder bowel dysfunction management.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Mentions local antibiogram/local resistance patterns
• Distinguishes between empirical and directed antibiotic therapy
• Specifies DMSA timing (4-6 months post-infection)
• Discusses ESBL as a specific emerging resistance pattern

## QUESTION 3 — SAQ: Prophylaxis and Bladder Bowel Dysfunction [Total: 8 marks] [FOUNDATION]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** List two antibiotics commonly used for UTI prophylaxis in children and their doses. **[2 marks]**

**(b)** State three modifiable risk factors for recurrent UTI in children beyond anatomical abnormalities. **[3 marks]**

**(c)** Outline the relationship between bladder bowel dysfunction (BBD) and recurrent UTI, and name two validated assessment tools for BBD. **[3 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [2 marks — 1 each]
• Trimethoprim 2 mg/kg once daily (max 50 mg) [1]
• Nitrofurantoin 1-2 mg/kg once daily (contraindicated <3 months of age and in GFR <30) [1]
• (Also acceptable: cefalexin 10 mg/kg once daily)

**(b)** [3 marks — 1 each]
• Constipation / bowel dysfunction [1]
• Incomplete bladder emptying [1]
• Dysfunctional voiding / infrequent voiding [1]
• (Also: perineal hygiene, wiping technique in girls, bubble baths, tight clothing)

**(c)** [3 marks]
• BBD creates a reservoir of periurethral bacteria; incomplete voiding promotes bacterial growth [1]
• Constipation impairs bladder emptying and increases residual urine [1]
• Validated tools: DVSS (Dysfunctional Voiding Symptom Score) [1]
• BBD score from the RIVUR trial (based on Rome III criteria for constipation and dysfunctional voiding) [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Missing the age/GFR contraindication for nitrofurantoin
• Not recognising BBD as the single most important modifiable risk factor
• Forgetting validated assessment tools exist

## QUESTION 4 — SAQ: Atypical UTI [Total: 8 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 6-week-old term male infant presents with fever (38.5°C), poor feeding, and lethargy. Urine culture grows Klebsiella pneumoniae >10⁵ CFU/mL. Ultrasound shows bilateral hydroureteronephrosis with a thickened bladder wall.

**(a)** List three features that make this an "atypical" UTI as defined by NICE CG54. **[3 marks]**

**(b)** State the imaging investigations you would arrange and the recommended timing for each. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [3 marks]
• Age <3 months (neonate/infant) [1]
• Non-E. coli organism (Klebsiella) [1]
• Abnormal renal ultrasound (hydroureteronephrosis, thickened bladder wall) [1]
• (Also: family history of VUR, poor urinary stream, septicaemia, failure to respond to treatment within 48 hours — but 3 of the above are sufficient)

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Renal ultrasound (already done) — if not, repeat to assess renal parenchyma and bladder [1]
• MCU — after infection clears, under antibiotic cover; to assess for VUR and PUV [1]
• DMSA — at 4-6 months post-infection to assess for scarring [1]
• Given bilateral hydroureteronephrosis and thickened bladder in a male infant → MCU is urgent to exclude PUV [1]
• If MCU shows PUV → immediate referral for valve ablation [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Not recognising PUV as the underlying cause in a male infant with bilateral hydroureteronephrosis
• Delaying MCU in this scenario
• Not classifying Klebsiella as a non-E. coli organism

## QUESTION 5 — LCV: Recurrent Pyelonephritis [Total: 35 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~40 minutes

─── STAGE 1: PRESENTATION ───────────────────────────────
A 4-year-old girl is referred to paediatric urology clinic. She has had three culture-confirmed febrile UTIs in the past 12 months. Each episode required hospital admission for IV antibiotics. She is otherwise well between episodes but has chronic constipation (Bristol Stool Chart Type 1-2, bowel opening every 3-4 days) and is described as a "infrequent voider" by her mother.

**Q1a.** List the differential diagnoses you would consider and the key features of her history that narrow the differential. **[5 marks]**

**Q1b.** What questions would you ask to assess bladder bowel dysfunction (BBD) and how would you quantify it? **[5 marks]**

─── STAGE 2: INVESTIGATION RESULTS ──────────────────────
Renal ultrasound shows mild prominence of the right renal pelvis (APD 12 mm) without ureteric dilatation. Post-void bladder residual volume is 35 mL (estimated bladder capacity 250 mL). DMSA scan shows a photopenic area in the right upper pole with reduced differential function to 38%. MCU demonstrates right-sided grade III VUR.

**Q2a.** Interpret these findings and state how they change your management strategy. **[6 marks]**

**Q2b.** Discuss the role of endoscopic treatment for VUR in this child, including success rates, procedural technique, and factors influencing outcome. **[6 marks]**

─── STAGE 3: MANAGEMENT DECISION ────────────────────────
The parents are concerned about repeated infections and request "something definitive." They have heard about the Deflux injection. You discuss the options of conservative management, endoscopic injection, and open ureteric reimplantation.

**Q3a.** Outline the evidence for each option in this clinical scenario, including success rates, risks, and benefits. **[6 marks]**

**Q3b.** Justify your final recommendation and describe how you would counsel the family to reach a shared decision. **[3 marks]**

─── STAGE 4: FOLLOW-UP AND OUTCOME ──────────────────────
The family opts for endoscopic treatment. The child undergoes a successful Deflux injection (HIT technique). At 6-month follow-up, she has had no further UTIs. The residual VUR is grade I. She continues to struggle with constipation and infrequent voiding.

**Q4a.** Outline your ongoing management plan for this child, including the role of urotherapy, bowel management, and surveillance. **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**FULL MARKING GUIDE:**

**Q1a.** [5 marks]
• Primary VUR (most likely given recurrent pyelonephritis and gender) [1]
• Bladder bowel dysfunction contributing to UTI recurrence [1]
• PUJ obstruction (less likely without a palpable mass or pain) [1]
• Duplex system/ureterocele (USS will clarify) [1]
• Voiding dysfunction (infrequent voiding, constipation suggest underlying BBD) [1]

**Q1b.** [5 marks]
• Bladder diary: voiding frequency, urgency, incontinence episodes, daytime wetting [1]
• Bowel history: stool frequency, consistency (Bristol chart), soiling, pain on defecation [1]
• Fluid intake: volume and type of drinks [1]
• DVSS (Dysfunctional Voiding Symptom Score) questionnaire [1]
• Rome III/IV criteria for functional constipation [1]

**Q2a.** [6 marks]
• Right upper pole scar on DMSA with reduced function to 38% = established reflux nephropathy [1]
• Grade III VUR on right = may be contributing to recurrent pyelonephritis [1]
• Post-void residual 35 mL (14% of capacity) = abnormal (>10-20 mL is significant in a 4-year-old) [1]
• BBD is the major modifiable cofactor — must be addressed before or alongside VUR treatment [1]
• Management: aggressive BBD treatment (laxatives, timed voiding, urotherapy) + discuss VUR intervention [1]
• Prophylactic antibiotics while awaiting definitive management [1]

**Q2b.** [6 marks]
• Endoscopic treatment: subureteric injection of Deflux (dextranomer/hyaluronic acid) [1]
• HIT (hydrodistention implantation technique): inject into intramural ureter at 6 o'clock [1]
• Double HIT: additional injection more proximally [1]
• Success rates: grade III ~75-85% (complete resolution); grade IV ~65-70% [1]
• Factors improving success: lower VUR grade, good coaptation mound appearance, BBD managed [1]
• Complications: ureteric obstruction (~1-2%), failure, de novo contralateral VUR [1]

**Q3a.** [6 marks]
• Medical management: prophylaxis + BBD treatment — reduces UTI by ~50% (RIVUR trial), but does not address scarring; ~25% annual resolution rate for grade III [1-2]
• Endoscopic treatment: single session, day surgery, ~75-85% success, low morbidity, avoids open surgery [1-2]
• Open reimplantation: 95%+ success, general anaesthesia, 48-hour stay, requires catheter/stent, longer recovery; may be considered after failed endoscopic treatment [1-2]

**Q3b.** [3 marks]
• My recommendation: endoscopic treatment given the frequency of breakthrough infections despite what appears to be modifiable BBD [1]
• However, BBD must be aggressively managed first or simultaneously — failure to do so reduces endoscopic success [1]
• Open reimplantation reserved if endoscopic fails or if further infections occur [1]

**Q4a.** [4 marks]
• Urotherapy: timed voiding (every 3 hours), double voiding, optimised fluid intake [1]
• Bowel management: high-dose polyethylene glycol (Movicol) aiming for daily soft stool [1]
• Repeat MCU at 1 year to confirm VUR resolution [1]
• DMSA at 2 years to reassess scarring progression [1]
• Discharge plan: if asymptomatic, no further US/DMSA needed after 2 years; BP check annually [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Treats BBD as a primary intervention, not an afterthought
• Quotes specific success rates for endoscopic vs open treatment by grade
• Describes the HIT technique by name
• Recognises that endoscopic success is reduced if BBD is untreated

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Skipping BBD management and going straight to surgery
• Quoting open reimplantation as first-line for grade III VUR
• Not quantifying post-void residual or bladder capacity
• Not offering urotherapy as a core treatment

## QUESTION 6 — EMQ: Imaging Strategy After UTI [Total: 10 marks] [BOARD]

**Theme: Post-UTI Imaging According to NICE CG54**

**OPTIONS (each option may be used once, more than once, or not at all):**

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| A. US only | F. DMSA + US + MCU |
| B. US + DMSA | G. No imaging required |
| C. US + MCU | H. MAG3 renogram + US |
| D. DMSA only | I. MCU only |
| E. US + DMSA + MCU | J. CT urogram |

**For each of the following clinical scenarios, select the single most appropriate imaging strategy:**

**1.** A 5-month-old girl with a first febrile UTI, responds well to treatment. No atypical features. **[1 mark]**

**2.** A 2-year-old boy with recurrent febrile UTIs (2 episodes in 6 months). Renal US shows no abnormality. No atypical features. **[1 mark]**

**3.** A 4-week-old term male with a febrile UTI. US shows bilateral SFU grade III hydronephrosis with thickened bladder wall. **[1 mark]**

**4.** A 6-year-old girl with recurrent cystitis (afebrile episodes, 3 in the past year). She has no abnormal findings on examination. **[1 mark]**

**5.** An 8-year-old boy with a first febrile UTI. US shows a solitary left kidney with mild pelvicalyceal dilatation. **[1 mark]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**ANSWERS & RATIONALE:**

**1. A** — US only. Per NICE CG54, a first febrile UTI in a child 6 months–3 years with no atypical features requires only renal US. DMSA and MCU are not indicated.

**2. B** — US + DMSA. Recurrent febrile UTI in a child aged 6 months–3 years requires US and DMSA (at 4-6 months post-infection). MCU is not indicated unless ultrasound shows dilatation or atypical features.

**3. E** — US + DMSA + MCU. This is a neonate with atypical features (age <3 months, abnormal US). Full imaging is indicated: US to evaluate anatomy, MCU to exclude PUV/VUR, DMSA at 4-6 months for scarring.

**4. G** — No imaging required. Recurrent cystitis (afebrile lower UTI) in a school-age child without atypical features does not require imaging per NICE. The focus should be on BBD, constipation, and hygiene measures.

**5. E** — US + DMSA + MCU. A solitary kidney with febrile UTI warrants full imaging. Any damage to a solitary kidney carries greater consequence. MCU is needed to exclude VUR, DMSA to assess baseline function.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Over-investigating lower UTI (cystitis) — imaging is for upper tract infection (pyelonephritis)
• Forgetting that age stratification changes the imaging algorithm
• Not recognising the solitary kidney as a special case requiring more aggressive investigation

**EXAMINER'S NOTES — COMMON SCRIPT PATTERNS TO WATCH FOR:**
• Candidates frequently fail to distinguish between first and recurrent UTI when recommending imaging
• The RIVUR trial findings are often remembered as "prophylaxis prevents scarring" — this is incorrect; it prevents UTI recurrence but not new scar formation
• BBD is consistently under-recognised as a risk factor — strong candidates prioritise it
• NICE CG54 age categories are poorly recalled; many candidates give generic "USS and MCU" answers without stratification
