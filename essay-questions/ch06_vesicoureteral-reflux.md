## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Diagnosis and Natural History of VUR [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 2-year-old girl is referred following her second culture-confirmed febrile UTI. Renal ultrasound shows no abnormality. DMSA performed 6 months after the second infection shows a photopenic area in the right upper pole. MCU demonstrates right-sided grade IV VUR and left-sided grade II VUR.

**(a)** Describe the International Grading System for VUR on MCU, and classify this child's reflux using this system. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Explain the timing and rationale for performing DMSA scintigraphy in this clinical context, including how you distinguish between acute pyelonephritic changes and established scarring. **[4 marks]**

**(c)** Discuss the natural history of VUR of different grades, including spontaneous resolution rates and factors that favour resolution. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** Outline the factors that predict renal scarring in children with VUR, and describe the long-term sequelae of established reflux nephropathy. **[4 marks]**

**(e)** The parents ask whether they should investigate their 4-week-old son. Discuss the evidence for sibling screening in VUR and your recommendation. **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [5 marks] — Award 1 mark per valid point up to 5:
• Grade I: reflux into ureter only (not reaching pelvis) [1]
• Grade II: reflux to renal pelvis, no calyceal dilatation [1]
• Grade III: mild-moderate dilatation of ureter and pelvis ± blunting of calyces [1]
• Grade IV: moderate dilatation and tortuosity of ureter, blunted calyces, but papillary impressions still visible [1]
• Grade V: gross dilatation and tortuous ureter, loss of papillary impressions, intraparenchymal reflux [1]
• This child: right grade IV (moderate-severe), left grade II (mild) [1]

**(b)** [4 marks]
• DMSA static scan: quantifies functioning renal cortex [1]
• Timing: minimum 4-6 months after acute infection — acute inflammation causes transient reduced uptake that resolves [1]
• Acute changes: photopenic area with preserved renal contour — usually resolves [1]
• Established scar: photopenic area with volume loss and cortical thinning — persists on repeat imaging [1]

**(c)** [5 marks]
• Overall natural history: tendency toward spontaneous resolution with age [1]
• Resolution rates at 5 years: grade I-II ~80%, grade III ~50%, grade IV ~30%, grade V ~10% [1]
• Factors favouring resolution: lower grade, younger age, unilateral reflux, absence of renal scarring, resolution of BBD [1]
• Annual resolution rate declines with age — most spontaneous resolution occurs before age 5 [1]
• BBD delays resolution — treating BBD improves resolution rate by ~2x [1]

**(d)** [4 marks]
• Predictors of scarring: high-grade reflux (IV-V), recurrent febrile UTI/pyelonephritis, delay in treatment, presence of intrarenal reflux on MCU [1]
• Breakthrough infections despite prophylaxis, male gender in infancy [1]
• Long-term sequelae of reflux nephropathy: hypertension (~15-20% in bilateral scarring), proteinuria, CKD progression [1]
• Pregnancy complications: increased risk of UTI, pre-eclampsia, and reduced renal function in pregnancy [1]

**(e)** [4 marks]
• VUR is familial — autosomal dominant with variable penetrance [1]
• Sibling risk: ~27-35% for first-degree relatives [1]
• ESPU recommends: sibling screening with US; MCU only if US abnormal or sibling symptomatic [1]
• NICE: does not recommend routine sibling screening [1]
• My recommendation: take history (any UTIs?); if asymptomatic, US at minimum; if US abnormal or any febrile illness, MCU [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This child has right grade IV and left grade II VUR by the International Classification. The right upper pole DMSA defect represents established scarring given the 6-month interval from infection. Grade II VUR has ~80% resolution rate over 5 years, while grade IV has ~30%, with BBD a major modifiable factor.

The right-sided scar carries long-term implications: hypertension risk (~15% if unilateral, higher if bilateral), and pregnancy-related risks. I would recommend aggressive BBD management, prophylactic antibiotics (per RIVUR trial evidence), and discuss endoscopic treatment given the grade IV reflux with existing scar. Sibling screening for the 4-week-old son: I'd take a history and offer a screening US. Per ESPU, I would not routinely do an MCU unless symptomatic.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Describes all 5 grades with the specific papillary impression feature that distinguishes grade IV from V
• Quotes specific resolution percentages by grade
• Distinguishes ESPU from NICE sibling screening recommendations
• Identifies intrarenal reflux as a predictor of scarring

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Management of High-Grade VUR [Total: 20 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 3-year-old boy is referred with recurrent breakthrough febrile UTIs despite trimethoprim prophylaxis. He has grade IV left-sided VUR on MCU with a DMSA showing reduced left kidney function to 32% and evidence of scarring in the lower pole. His parents are keen to avoid open surgery if possible.

**(a)** List the evidence for and against continuing antibiotic prophylaxis in this child, with reference to the RIVUR trial. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Describe the technique of endoscopic subureteric injection (HIT/STING procedure) including success rates for grade IV VUR. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Discuss the role of open ureteric reimplantation in this child, comparing the Cohen (cross-trigonal) and Lich-Gregoir (extravesical) techniques. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** Justify your management recommendation for this case and outline the counselling points you would discuss with the family. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [5 marks] — 1 per valid point up to 5:
• RIVUR trial: prophylaxis with TMP-SMX reduced UTI recurrence by 50% (RR 0.50, p<0.001) [1]
• However, no reduction in new renal scarring was demonstrated [1]
• This child has breakthrough infections despite prophylaxis — this is a key indication to move beyond medical management [1]
• Continued prophylaxis after VUR treatment reduces post-procedure infection risk [1]
• Benefits weighed against: antibiotic resistance, side effects, compliance [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Cystoscopy under general anaesthesia, 8-10 Fr cystoscope [1]
• STING (Subureteric Teflon/Deflux Injection): needle inserted 2-3 mm below ureteric orifice, inject Deflux to create mound [1]
• HIT (Hydrodistention Implantation Technique): hydrodistend ureteric orifice, inject into intramural ureter at 6 o'clock [1]
• Double HIT: combines STING and HIT techniques [1]
• Success rate for grade IV: ~65-75% (single session), ~85% after two sessions [1]

**(c)** [5 marks]
• Cohen cross-trigonal: ureter mobilised transvesically, reimplanted into contralateral side; high success (>95%), but makes future ureteric access difficult [1]
• Lich-Gregoir extravesical: ureter reimplanted extravesically with detrusor tunnel; no incision of bladder mucosa; no risk of ureteric access problems [1]
• Lich-Gregoir bilateral: risk of post-operative urinary retention (~5% if bilateral) from denervation [1]
• Both have >95% success for primary VUR [1]
• Post-operative: urethral catheter 48 hours, ureteric stent optional, admission 2-4 days [1]

**(d)** [5 marks]
• This child has grade IV VUR with breakthrough infections and established scarring — active treatment indicated [1]
• Endoscopic treatment is reasonable first step given parent preference, but success is lower for grade IV [1]
• If endoscopic fails → open reimplantation (Lich-Gregoir single side — low morbidity, >95% success) [1]
• If significant BBD → must be treated aggressively before or alongside intervention [1]
• Counselling: discuss success rates, risks (obstruction 1-2%, failure, de novo contralateral VUR), post-op course [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This child has failed medical management — breakthrough infections despite prophylaxis warrants escalation. I would recommend proceeding to endoscopic treatment (HIT technique) as first-line given parent preference. Success for grade IV is ~65-75% per session. If this fails, open Lich-Gregoir extravesical reimplantation offers >95% success. BBD must be addressed concurrently to optimise outcomes.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Quotes specific success rates for endoscopic vs open by grade
• Describes both HIT and STING by name with technical detail
• Acknowledges the specific complication of urinary retention after bilateral Lich-Gregoir
• Recommends treating BBD before intervention

## QUESTION 3 — SAQ: Imaging in VUR [Total: 8 marks] [FOUNDATION]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** Compare and contrast the roles of DMSA scintigraphy and MCU in the evaluation of VUR. **[4 marks]**

**(b)** State the radiation dose implications of MCU in children and name two strategies to minimise radiation exposure during the procedure. **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [4 marks]
• DMSA: static functional imaging — identifies functioning renal cortex and scarring [1]
• DMSA: quantifies differential renal function [1]
• MCU: anatomical imaging — diagnoses VUR and grades severity by International Classification [1]
• MCU: also images urethra (diagnoses PUV in males) and bladder contour [1]

**(b)** [4 marks]
• MCU radiation dose: ~0.1-1 mSv depending on fluoroscopy time and technique [1]
• Strategies: pulsed fluoroscopy (rather than continuous) reduces dose by 50-70% [1]
• Cyclic voiding (fill and void 2-3 times in one session) to maximise diagnostic yield per exposure [1]
• Digital fluoroscopy with last-image-hold; limit fluoroscopy time to <1-2 minutes [1]

## QUESTION 4 — SAQ: VUR and Renal Scarring [Total: 8 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** List three risk factors for the development of renal scarring in children with VUR. **[3 marks]**

**(b)** Outline the long-term follow-up recommended for a child with established bilateral reflux nephropathy, including the specific investigations and their frequency. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [3 marks — 1 each]
• High-grade VUR (IV-V) [1]
• Recurrent febrile UTIs / pyelonephritis [1]
• Intrarenal reflux on MCU [1]
• (Also: delayed presentation/treatment, male gender in infancy, presence of BBD)

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Annual blood pressure measurement (starting at diagnosis) [1]
• Annual urinalysis for proteinuria (first morning urine protein:creatinine ratio) [1]
• Urea and creatinine / eGFR assessment [1]
• Renal US every 1-3 years to assess growth and compensatory hypertrophy [1]
• If proteinuria develops: start ACE inhibitor (ESCAPE trial evidence) [1]
• Transition to adult nephrology services at 16-18 years [1]

## QUESTION 5 — LCV: The Child with VUR and BBD [Total: 35 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~40 minutes

─── STAGE 1: PRESENTATION ───────────────────────────────
A 6-year-old girl is referred with a history of recurrent UTIs (3 episodes in 18 months, one febrile). She also has daytime wetting, urgency, and squats to hold on (Vincent's curtsy sign). She opens her bowels every 3-4 days with hard stools requiring straining.

**Q1a.** What is the most likely cause of her recurrent UTIs, and what specific assessments would you perform in clinic? **[6 marks]**

**Q1b.** How would you quantify her bladder bowel dysfunction and what initial non-pharmacological treatments would you recommend? **[5 marks]**

─── STAGE 2: INVESTIGATION RESULTS ──────────────────────
Renal US is normal with normal bladder wall thickness. Post-void residual is 45 mL (estimated bladder capacity 300 mL). MCU shows left grade II VUR. DMSA shows no scarring.

**Q2a.** Interpret these results. Does the VUR need treating, and why or why not? **[5 marks]**

**Q2b.** What is the link between BBD and VUR, and how does treating BBD affect VUR resolution rates? **[5 marks]**

─── STAGE 3: MANAGEMENT ─────────────────────────────────
The child is started on a structured urotherapy programme including timed voiding, optimised fluid intake, and laxatives. She misses her 3-month follow-up. She returns at 8 months with another febrile UTI. The parents are frustrated and request "surgery to fix the reflux."

**Q3a.** Discuss how you would approach this consultation, including the importance of compliance with medical therapy. **[5 marks]**

**Q3b.** Describe the role of anticholinergic therapy in this child and the evidence for its use. **[5 marks]**

─── STAGE 4: OUTCOME ────────────────────────────────────
The family agrees to recommit to BBD management. After 6 months of compliant treatment, her voiding symptoms improve. She has no further UTIs. Repeat US shows normal residual volumes. You decide not to intervene on the grade II VUR.

**Q4a.** Justify this decision and outline the long-term follow-up plan. **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**FULL MARKING GUIDE:**

**Q1a.** [6 marks]
• BBD as primary driver of recurrent UTIs (dysfunctional voiding + constipation) [1]
• Assessment: bladder diary (3 days) documenting voiding frequency, volumes, episodes of incontinence [1]
• Bowel history: Bristol Stool Chart, frequency, soiling [1]
• Post-void residual measurement by US or bladder scan [1]
• DVSS questionnaire [1]
• Urinalysis and culture [1]

**Q1b.** [5 marks]
• Urotherapy: timed voiding every 2-3 hours [1]
• Optimised fluid intake (6-8 glasses of water/day) [1]
• Proper toileting position (feet supported, knees above hips) [1]
• Bowel management: increased dietary fibre, adequate fluids, osmotic laxatives if needed [1]
• Double voiding if residual urine is high [1]

**Q2a.** [5 marks]
• Normal US and DMSA = no renal damage from VUR to date [1]
• Left grade II VUR has ~80% spontaneous resolution rate [1]
• Does not require active surgical intervention — BBD management is the priority [1]
• Grade II VUR without breakthrough infections or scarring is managed conservatively [1]
• Treating BBD improves VUR resolution rate [1]

**Q2b.** [5 marks]
• BBD causes: incomplete bladder emptying → residual urine → bacterial reservoir [1]
• Detrusor overactivity → high intravesical pressure → facilitates VUR [1]
• Constipation → rectal distension → bladder outlet compression → incomplete voiding [1]
• Treatment of BBD doubles spontaneous VUR resolution rate [1]
• Untreated BBD reduces success of endoscopic/surgical VUR treatment [1]

**Q3a.** [5 marks]
• Acknowledge parental frustration and thank them for re-engaging [1]
• Explain that BBD was the primary driver — VUR grade II was incidental [1]
• The recurrence occurred during period of non-compliance with BBD therapy [1]
• Recommend recommitment to full BBD programme before considering VUR intervention [1]
• If breakthrough infections continue after 6-12 months of compliant BBD treatment, then consider endoscopic treatment [1]

**Q3b.** [5 marks]
• Anticholinergics (oxybutynin 0.2-0.4 mg/kg TDS) reduce detrusor overactivity [1]
• Indications: persistent urgency, frequency, incontinence despite urotherapy [1]
• Evidence: reduces daytime wetting episodes, improves bladder capacity [1]
• Side effects: constipation, dry mouth, flushing, blurred vision [1]
• Alternative: solifenacin or tolterodine if oxybutynin not tolerated [1]

**Q4a.** [4 marks]
• Grade II VUR managed conservatively — no indication for intervention [1]
• VUR may still resolve spontaneously given her age [1]
• Annual follow-up: monitor BP, urinalysis, US every 1-2 years [1]
• Reinforce BBD maintenance; re-refer if further UTIs occur [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Prioritises BBD treatment over VUR intervention throughout
• Recognises that grade II VUR is likely an incidental finding
• Discusses pharmacotherapy (oxybutynin) as second-line after failed urotherapy
• Understands the bidirectional relationship between VUR and BBD

## QUESTION 6 — EMQ: Matching VUR Grade to Management [Total: 10 marks] [BOARD]

**Theme: Management of Vesicoureteral Reflux by Clinical Scenario**

**OPTIONS (each option may be used once, more than once, or not at all):**

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| A. No treatment or follow-up needed | F. Endoscopic injection |
| B. Antibiotic prophylaxis only | G. Open ureteric reimplantation |
| C. Antibiotic prophylaxis + BBD management | H. Nephrectomy |
| D. BBD management only | I. Anticholinergic therapy |
| E. Urotherapy only | J. Laparoscopic reimplantation |

**For each of the following clinical scenarios, select the single most appropriate management option:**

**1.** A 4-year-old asymptomatic girl with left grade I VUR found on sibling screening. Normal US and DMSA. No history of UTI. **[1 mark]**

**2.** A 2-year-old boy with right grade III VUR and two breakthrough febrile UTIs despite prophylaxis. He has BBD. DMSA shows a small upper pole scar. **[1 mark]**

**3.** A 6-month-old girl with left grade IV VUR, first febrile UTI. Normal DMSA. No BBD. **[1 mark]**

**4.** A 7-year-old girl with recurrent UTIs, daytime wetting, constipation, and left grade II VUR on MCU. Normal DMSA. **[1 mark]**

**5.** An 8-year-old boy with left grade V VUR, function on that side <10% on DMSA and recurrent pyelonephritis. The contralateral kidney is normal. **[1 mark]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**ANSWERS & RATIONALE:**

**1. A** — No treatment or follow-up. Grade I VUR in an asymptomatic child with normal imaging and no UTI history requires no intervention. The risk of spontaneous resolution is very high and the risk of UTI is low.

**2. F** — Endoscopic injection. This child has breakthrough infections (failed medical management) and grade III VUR with early scarring. Endoscopic treatment (HIT technique) is appropriate first-line — ~80% success for grade III.

**3. B** — Antibiotic prophylaxis only. Grade IV VUR in an infant with a first UTI and normal DMSA warrants prophylaxis and observation. Many will improve with age. Surgery is reserved for breakthrough infections or new scarring.

**4. C** — Antibiotic prophylaxis + BBD management. Grade II VUR has high spontaneous resolution. BBD is the primary driver. Prophylaxis reduces UTI risk while BBD treatment improves VUR resolution.

**5. G** — Open ureteric reimplantation. Grade V is unlikely to resolve. With <10% function and recurrent pyelonephritis, a non-functioning kidney may be driving morbidity. However, if the kidney has any function or potential, reimplantation should be considered before nephrectomy. Nephrectomy is appropriate if function is negligible and the kidney is the source of infections.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Treating grade I-II VUR as a surgical condition
• Not distinguishing between asymptomatic VUR (no treatment) and VUR with breakthrough infections (intervention)
• Removing a kidney with any salvageable function without attempting reimplantation

**EXAMINER'S NOTES — COMMON SCRIPT PATTERNS TO WATCH FOR:**
• Candidates often recommend intervention for all grades of VUR without considering natural history
• The RIVUR trial is frequently cited inaccurately — many say "prophylaxis prevents scarring" when the trial showed no scar reduction
• BBD is frequently mentioned but rarely prioritised as the primary intervention
• Surgical technique descriptions (particularly Cohen vs Lich-Gregoir) are often vague or confused
