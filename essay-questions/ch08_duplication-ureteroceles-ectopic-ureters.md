## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Urinary Duplication Anomalies [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 3-year-old girl is referred with continuous wetting since birth. She passes urine normally in the toilet but is constantly damp. Her mother reports "she's never been dry for more than 30 minutes." Examination reveals moisture at the introitus separate from the urethral meatus. Renal ultrasound shows a left duplex kidney with a thin upper moiety and a dilated, tortuous ureter coursing distally.

**(a)** Explain the embryological basis of ureteric duplication and state the Weigert-Meyer rule. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Give the most likely diagnosis and describe the anatomical basis for this child's symptom of continuous wetting. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Outline the imaging investigations you would arrange to confirm the diagnosis and aid surgical planning. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** Describe the surgical options for managing a non-functioning upper moiety with an ectopic ureter, including the advantages and disadvantages of each approach. **[7 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [5 marks]
• Duplication arises from premature or accessory ureteric bud from mesonephric duct [1]
• Weigert-Meyer rule: upper moiety ureter inserts inferomedially in the bladder (ectopic) and "tends to obstruct"; lower moiety ureter inserts superolaterally and "tends to reflux" [2]
• Upper moiety ureter crosses behind lower moiety ureter [1]
• The ureter that inserts lower on the trigone drains the upper pole [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Diagnosis: left duplex kidney with upper moiety ectopic ureter [1]
• In girls, the ectopic ureter may insert into the vagina, vestibule, or distal urethra (distal to the external sphincter) [1]
• Because the ureteric orifice is distal to the external urethral sphincter, urine bypasses the normal continence mechanism → continuous wetting [1]
• This occurs alongside normal voiding if the lower moiety ureter is normally placed [1]
• Distinguish from true incontinence (always wet) vs urge incontinence (urgency-driven) [1]

**(c)** [5 marks]
• Renal US + bladder US: assess upper moiety parenchyma thickness, ureteric dilatation, and look for ureterocele [1]
• DMSA scintigraphy: assess differential function of upper vs lower moiety; upper moiety often non-functioning [1]
• MCU: assess for VUR into the lower moiety (present in ~50%) [1]
• MR urogram: defines anatomy — course of ectopic ureter, site of insertion, relationship to other structures [1]
• Cystoscopy/vaginoscopy: direct visualisation of ectopic ureteric orifice [1]

**(d)** [7 marks]
• Upper pole heminephrectomy + ureterectomy: remove non-functioning upper moiety and its ureter [2]
   • Advantages: removes source of infection, cures wetting
   • Disadvantages: risk of damage to lower moiety blood supply (5-10%), requires two incisions flank+groin
• Upper pole heminephrectomy + ureteric stump drainage: remove upper moiety, leave distal ureter open [1]
   • Less dissection; risk of stump complications (UTI, empyema)
• Common sheath ureteric reimplantation: if both moieties have function worth preserving [1]
• Ureteropyelostomy/ureteroureterostomy: anastomose obstructed upper moiety ureter to lower moiety pelvis [1]
• Endoscopic puncture: if ureterocele present — incise ureterocele transurethrally [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This girl has continuous wetting from a left duplex kidney with an ectopic upper moiety ureter draining distal to the external sphincter — likely into the vestibule. The Weigert-Meyer rule explains why the upper moiety obstructs (ectopic, often dysplastic) while the lower moiety refluxes. Imaging requires US, DMSA (to confirm non-functional upper moiety), MCU (to assess lower moiety VUR), and MR urogram for anatomical planning. The treatment of choice is upper pole heminephrectomy with excision of the dilated ureter down to the pelvis, taking care to preserve the lower moiety blood supply.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Accurately applies Weigert-Meyer rule to the specific clinical scenario
• Explains the exact mechanism of continuous wetting (insertion distal to external sphincter)
• Discusses surgical options with specific success rates and risks
• Mentions MR urogram as the optimal anatomical imaging modality

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Ureterocele Management [Total: 20 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 2-week-old female infant is found to have a palpable abdominal mass on routine neonatal examination. She is otherwise well, feeding normally, and passing urine. Renal ultrasound reveals a duplex right kidney with marked dilatation of the upper moiety and a thin-walled cystic structure projecting into the bladder lumen. The left kidney is normal.

**(a)** What is the most likely diagnosis, and how does this condition present in neonates versus older children? **[4 marks]**

**(b)** Classify ureteroceles and state the type most likely in this case. **[4 marks]**

**(c)** Discuss the management options for a neonatal ureterocele, including the role of endoscopic puncture, upper pole heminephrectomy, and total reconstruction. **[6 marks]**

**(d)** Outline the potential complications of endoscopic ureterocele puncture and describe how you would manage each. **[6 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [4 marks]
• Diagnosis: duplex kidney with obstructed upper moiety and ureterocele [1]
• Neonates: detected on antenatal US (cystic structure in bladder) or as abdominal mass from hydronephrotic upper pole [1]
• Older children: UTI, urinary retention (ureterocele prolapses into bladder neck), or incontinence [1]
• Adults: incidental finding or recurrent UTIs [1]

**(b)** [4 marks]
• Intravesical: ureterocele entirely within bladder [1]
• Extravesical (ectopic): ureterocele extends into bladder neck/proximal urethra [1]
• SVPU (Stephens) classification: stenotic, sphincteric, sphincterostenotic, cecoureterocele [1]
• This case: intravesical ureterocele (contained within bladder, classic "cobra head" appearance) [1]

**(c)** [6 marks]
• Observation: if asymptomatic, no UTI, no obstruction to bladder neck — US surveillance [1]
• Endoscopic puncture: first-line for intravesical ureterocele — incise with Bugbee electrode or laser at low point of ureterocele [1]
   • Advantages: minimally invasive, preserves upper moiety function temporarily
   • Success: ~70-90% for intravesical; lower for ectopic
• Upper pole heminephrectomy + partial ureterectomy: if upper moiety non-functioning and endoscopic fails [1]
• Total reconstruction: heminephrectomy + ureterocele excision + ureteric reimplantation — definitive, but major surgery in neonate [1]
• Usually staged: endoscopic first, then definitive surgery if needed [1]

**(d)** [6 marks]
• De novo VUR into upper moiety (most common, ~30-50%) — manage with prophylaxis; may resolve; reimplantation if persistent [1]
• Inadequate decompression — needs repeat or higher puncture [1]
• Ureterocele prolapse/inversion — reduce manually; if recurrent, excision [1]
• UTI from inadequate drainage — antibiotics [1]
• Bladder neck injury (in ectatic ureteroceles) — careful placement away from bladder neck [1]
• Retained ureterocele wall causing flap-valve obstruction — endoscopic resection [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This neonate has a duplex right kidney with an intravesical ureterocele causing upper moiety obstruction. In neonates, these present as abdominal masses from the hydronephrotic upper pole. The intravesical type (contained within the bladder) is more favourable than the ectopic type. First-line management is endoscopic puncture — I would incise the ureterocele at its inferomedial aspect using a 3Fr Bugbee electrode. This decompresses the upper moiety and relieves obstruction. Prophylactic antibiotics are given post-puncture. The child will need long-term follow-up for de novo VUR (the most common complication, ~30-50%).

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Distinguishes intravesical from ectopic ureterocele prognostically
• Quotes specific success and complication rates for endoscopic puncture
• Describes staged approach rather than upfront total reconstruction
• Mentions the SVPU classification

## QUESTION 3 — SAQ: Ectopic Ureter in Boys [Total: 8 marks] [FOUNDATION]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** Compare the presentation of an ectopic ureter in boys versus girls, explaining the anatomical reason for the difference. **[4 marks]**

**(b)** List three possible insertion sites for an ectopic ureter in a male child. **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [4 marks]
• Girls: continuous wetting (ectopic insertion distal to external urethral sphincter) — classic presentation [1]
• Boys: no continuous wetting — ectopic ureter always inserts proximal to external sphincter [1]
• In boys, Wolfian duct derivatives: ectopic ureter inserts into posterior urethra, seminal vesicle, vas deferens, or ejaculatory duct [1]
• Boys present with: recurrent epididymitis, UTI, or incidental finding [1]

**(b)** [4 marks — 1 per site]
• Posterior urethra [1]
• Seminal vesicle [1]
• Vas deferens [1]
• Ejaculatory duct [1]
• (Also: prostatic utricle)

## QUESTION 4 — SAQ: Heminephrectomy Technique [Total: 8 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** Describe the operative steps of a partial nephrectomy (heminephrectomy) for a non-functioning upper moiety in a 2-year-old child, including how you preserve the lower moiety blood supply. **[6 marks]**

**(b)** State two specific complications of this procedure and how you would minimise them. **[2 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [6 marks]
• Position: lateral decubitus, flank approach [1]
• Identify renal hilum: dissect upper and lower moiety vessels separately [1]
• Identify tissue plane between upper and lower moieties (often a subtle groove) [1]
• Clamp upper moiety polar vessels; inject methylene blue to confirm lower moiety perfusion [1]
• Resect upper moiety sharply along the parenchymal plane, leaving a rim of tissue to oversew [1]
• Haemostasis: bipolar diathermy, topical haemostats (Surgicel, Floseal), ± PDS suture repair of collecting system [1]

**(b)** [2 marks]
• Lower moiety devascularisation — minimise by individual upper moiety vessel ligation and methylene blue testing [1]
• Urinary leak from cut surface — minimise by watertight closure of collecting system and drain placement [1]

## QUESTION 5 — LCV: Ectopic Ureter with Complications [Total: 35 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~40 minutes

─── STAGE 1: PRESENTATION ───────────────────────────────
A 5-year-old girl presents with a 3-month history of recurrent left loin pain and febrile UTIs. She has also had persistent issues with dampness since toilet training but her mother "thought she'd grow out of it." US shows a left duplex kidney with a thin, hydronephrotic upper moiety and a dilated tortuous upper moiety ureter seen behind the bladder.

**Q1a.** What specific finding on examination would you seek to distinguish ectopic ureter from other causes of incontinence? **[2 marks]**
**Q1b.** List the investigations you would order and what each would contribute to surgical planning. **[6 marks]**

─── STAGE 2: INVESTIGATION RESULTS ──────────────────────
DMSA confirms 10% function in the upper moiety, 40% in the lower moiety, 50% on the right. MCU shows grade II VUR into the left lower moiety. MR urogram confirms the upper moiety ureter inserts into the vaginal vestibule.

**Q2a.** Interpret these results. What is the relative contribution of each anomaly to her symptoms? **[4 marks]**
**Q2b.** What is the rationale for performing MCU in addition to DMSA and MRU? **[3 marks]**

─── STAGE 3: MANAGEMENT DECISION ────────────────────────
You recommend left upper pole heminephrectomy with proximal ureterectomy.

**Q3a.** Describe the procedure in detail, including the approach, key steps, and how you avoid damaging the lower moiety. **[8 marks]**
**Q3b.** Discuss the management of the distal ureteric stump — should it be removed or left in situ? Justify your choice. **[5 marks]**

─── STAGE 4: COMPLICATION ──────────────────────────────
Post-operatively, the child develops increasing left flank pain, fever, and a rising CRP. US shows a fluid collection adjacent to the lower pole. Drain fluid creatinine is 600 μmol/L (serum 40 μmol/L).

**Q4a.** What is the likely diagnosis and how would you manage it? **[3 marks]**
**Q4b.** If this complication persists, what are your longer-term options? **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**FULL MARKING GUIDE:**

**Q1a.** [2 marks]
• Examine perineum for a separate ectopic ureteric orifice in the vestibule (between urethral meatus and vagina) [1]
• Continuous dampness with normal voiding in between — key diagnostic clue [1]

**Q1b.** [6 marks]
• US: assess upper moiety thickness, degree of hydronephrosis, ureteric dilatation [1]
• DMSA: quantify function of upper vs lower moiety [1]
• MCU: detect VUR into lower moiety (present in ~50%) [1]
• MR urogram: anatomical roadmap — course of ectopic ureter, insertion site, relationship to lower moiety ureter [1]
• Cystoscopy/vaginoscopy: direct visualisation of ectopic orifice [1]
• Dimercaptosuccinic acid (DMSA) SPECT for precise functional assessment of each moiety [1]

**Q2a.** [4 marks]
• Upper moiety at 10% function = non-functioning, causing recurrent UTIs from obstructed system [1]
• Lower moiety grade II VUR contributes to UTIs but is low grade and likely self-resolving [1]
• Continuous wetting from ectopic insertion distal to sphincter [1]
• Pain from upper moiety distension during UTIs [1]

**Q2b.** [3 marks]
• Lower moiety VUR grade II needs to be identified — may resolve after heminephrectomy [1]
• If persistent high-grade VUR, may need reimplantation at same time [1]
• MCU also assesses bladder function and capacity [1]

**Q3a.** [8 marks]
• Position: lateral decubitus, flank approach through 11th intercostal space [1]
• Identify renal hilum — dissect upper pole vessels individually [1]
• Upper moiety often has separate arterial supply (lower pole branch of main renal artery) [1]
• Mark lower moiety with methylene blue after clamping upper pole vessels [1]
• Resect upper pole along demarcation line [1]
• Oversew collecting system with 5-0 PDS [1]
• Haemostasis with bipolar + topical agent [1]
• Place drain in resection bed [1]

**Q3b.** [5 marks]
• Proximal ureterectomy: excise as much as possible, down to pelvic brim or lower [1]
• Removal of distal stump: pros — eliminates risk of stump empyema/reflux, cons — requires separate groin incision, risks damage to lower moiety ureter/blood supply [1]
• Stump management depends: if distal ureter is refluxing or infected, remove; if non-refluxing, leave [1]
• My choice: remove as much as possible through flank, leave distal stump only if non-refluxing; monitor with US [1]
• Leaving the stump: 5-10% risk of stump syndrome (UTI, pain, empyema) [1]

**Q4a.** [3 marks]
• Urine leak from lower pole calyceal suture line [1]
• Management: ensure drain output continues, IV antibiotics, US-guided aspiration if collection [1]
• If persistent: consider insertion of ureteric stent or nephrostomy [1]

**Q4b.** [4 marks]
• If leak persists >7-10 days: retrograde stent placement [1]
• Percutaneous nephrostomy to divert [1]
• Surgical re-exploration and repair if conservative fails [1]
• Nephrectomy of the remaining lower moiety only as last resort [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Describes specific perineal examination finding to distinguish ectopic ureter
• Discusses distal ureteric stump management controversy
• Provides methylene blue testing as an intraoperative technique to preserve lower moiety
• Plans for the complication of lower pole urine leak

## QUESTION 6 — EMQ: Duplication Anomalies [Total: 10 marks] [BOARD]

**Theme: Duplication Anomaly Presentations**

**OPTIONS (each option may be used once, more than once, or not at all):**

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| A. Ectopic ureter (upper moiety) | F. Inverted Y duplication |
| B. Ectopic ureter (lower moiety) | G. Triplication of ureter |
| C. Intravesical ureterocele | H. Ureteric diverticulum |
| D. Ectopic ureterocele | I. Single system ectopic ureter |
| E. VUR into lower moiety of duplex | J. Single system VUR |

**For each of the following clinical scenarios, select the single most appropriate option:**

**1.** A 6-month-old girl with a duplex kidney on US. The cystic structure in the bladder collapses and refills during voiding. There is associated upper moiety hydronephrosis. **[1 mark]**

**2.** A 4-year-old girl with continuous wetting since birth. She voids normally. MR urogram shows a duplex kidney with a ureter that inserts into the vaginal vestibule. **[1 mark]**

**3.** A 2-year-old boy with recurrent left epididymitis. US shows a left duplex kidney with a dilated ureter that terminates at the seminal vesicle. **[1 mark]**

**4.** A 3-year-old with recurrent febrile UTIs. MCU shows contrast entering the left lower moiety intrarenally during voiding. The upper moiety ureter inserts normally. **[1 mark]**

**5.** A 1-year-old with a duplex kidney. MCU shows contrast filling the upper moiety ureter after endoscopic puncture of a ureterocele. **[1 mark]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**ANSWERS & RATIONALE:**

**1.** **C** — Intravesical ureterocele. The collapsing cystic structure within the bladder that refills during voiding is pathognomonic for a ureterocele associated with the obstructed upper moiety of a duplex kidney.

**2.** **A** — Ectopic ureter (upper moiety). Duplex kidney with upper moiety ectopic ureter inserting distal to the external sphincter causes continuous wetting with normal voiding. The vaginal vestibule is the classic site in girls.

**3.** **A** — Ectopic ureter (upper moiety). In boys, the ectopic ureter inserts into Wolfian duct structures (seminal vesicle in this case). The presentation is recurrent epididymitis rather than incontinence.

**4.** **E** — VUR into lower moiety of duplex. The lower moiety ureter inserts superolaterally in the trigone — a short intramural tunnel predisposes to VUR. Intrarenal reflux indicates high-grade VUR.

**5.** **A** — Ectopic ureter (upper moiety). Endoscopic decompression of a ureterocele can create de novo VUR into the previously obstructed upper moiety ureter. This is the most common complication.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Confusing ectopic ureter in boys vs girls — boys do NOT get continuous wetting
• Not recognising the collapsed/refilling cystic structure as a ureterocele
• Thinking that post-puncture VUR is a failure of treatment rather than an expected complication in ~30-50%

**EXAMINER'S NOTES — COMMON SCRIPT PATTERNS TO WATCH FOR:**
• The Weigert-Meyer rule is frequently stated but then applied incorrectly to clinical scenarios
• Many candidates cannot explain WHY upper moiety ureters obstruct and lower moiety ureters reflux — they simply memorise the rule without understanding the embryology
• Distal ureteric stump management is a polarising topic — strong candidates present both sides
• Endoscopic puncture is often presented as "easy and definitive" without acknowledging the de novo VUR rate
