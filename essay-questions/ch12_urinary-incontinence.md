## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Dysfunctional Voiding [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 7-year-old girl is referred with daytime wetting, urgency, and recurrent UTIs (3 in the past year). She squats and crosses her legs to prevent wetting (Vincent's curtsy sign). She opens her bowels every 3-4 days with hard, pellet-like stools. Her mother reports she "waits until the last minute" to use the toilet.

**(a)** Explain the pathophysiology of dysfunctional voiding and its relationship with constipation and UTIs. **[6 marks]**

**(b)** Outline your clinical assessment, including the history, bladder diary, and validated questionnaires you would use. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Describe a structured urotherapy programme for this child, including specific behavioural modifications, timed voiding, and toilet positioning. **[6 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the role of pharmacotherapy in this case, including the indications, mechanism of action, and side effects of anticholinergic therapy. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [6 marks] — 1 per valid point up to 6:
• Dysfunctional voiding: learned behaviour where the external urethral sphincter contracts during voiding despite detrusor contraction — "staccato voiding" [1]
• Detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia: incomplete bladder emptying → residual urine → bacterial colonisation → UTIs [1]
• Constipation → faecal loading in rectum → compression of bladder neck → incomplete emptying [1]
• High intravesical pressure from holding → detrusor overactivity → urgency and urge incontinence [1]
• Vincent's curtsy: voluntary contraction of pelvic floor to suppress urgency — further exacerbates dysfunctional voiding [1]
• BBD (Bladder Bowel Dysfunction) is the unifying concept — treatment must address both bladder and bowel [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• History: voiding frequency, urgency, incontinence pattern, stream assessment, straining [1]
• Bladder diary: 3-7 day record of voiding times, volumes, wetting episodes, fluid intake [1]
• Bowel assessment: Bristol Stool Chart, frequency, soiling [1]
• DVSS (Dysfunctional Voiding Symptom Score) questionnaire [1]
• Post-void residual by ultrasound [1]

**(c)** [6 marks]
• Timed voiding: void every 2-3 hours by the clock, even if no urge [1]
• Optimised toileting: feet supported, knees above hips, lean forward, relax pelvic floor [1]
• Adequate fluid intake: 6-8 glasses of water/day; avoid bladder irritants (caffeine, fizzy drinks) [1]
• Double voiding: after initial void, wait 30 seconds and attempt to void again [1]
• Bowel management: osmotic laxatives (Movicol/PEG 3350) aiming for daily soft stool [1]
• Reward chart: positive reinforcement for timed voiding compliance and dry intervals [1]

**(d)** [5 marks]
• Indications: persistent overactive bladder symptoms despite 3-6 months of urotherapy [1]
• First-line: oxybutynin 0.2-0.4 mg/kg TDS [1]
• Mechanism: antimuscarinic — blocks M3 receptors in detrusor → reduces detrusor overactivity, increases functional bladder capacity [1]
• Side effects: constipation (worsens BBD), dry mouth, flushing, blurred vision, overheating in hot weather [1]
• Second-line: solifenacin or tolterodine if oxybutynin not tolerated (fewer CNS side effects) [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This girl has dysfunctional voiding with BBD — a learned pattern of pelvic floor contraction during voiding causing incomplete emptying, urgency, and recurrent UTIs. Vincent's curtsy is characteristic. Urotherapy is the cornerstone of treatment: timed voiding, optimised toilet positioning, adequate hydration, and aggressive bowel management with laxatives. If overactive bladder symptoms persist after 3-6 months, I would add oxybutynin, titrated to response. Both bladder and bowel must be treated together — treating one without the other frequently leads to treatment failure.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Explains the functional relationship between constipation and voiding dysfunction
• Describes specific toilet positioning rather than generic "good toileting"
• Quotes DVSS as a validated tool
• Presents pharmacotherapy as second-line after failed urotherapy

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Nocturnal Enuresis [Total: 20 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
An 8-year-old boy is referred with primary nocturnal enuresis (bedwetting 5-6 nights/week). He has never been dry at night for more than 2 consecutive weeks. He has no daytime symptoms, no UTIs, and no history of snoring or constipation. His parents are frustrated and his mother was a lifelong bedwetter who only became dry at age 14.

**(a)** Distinguish between monosymptomatic and non-monosymptomatic nocturnal enuresis, and classify this case. **[4 marks]**

**(b)** Explain the three pathophysiological mechanisms underlying monosymptomatic nocturnal enuresis (the "three systems model"). **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Outline the management options for this child, including the evidence base for desmopressin and the enuresis alarm. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the concept of "full bladder capacity" vs "functional bladder capacity" and the role of urotherapy (including the bellows manoeuvre) in enuresis. **[3 marks]**

**(e)** At what age would you refer this child to specialist services, and what are the red flag features requiring immediate referral? **[3 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [4 marks]
• Monosymptomatic: isolated nocturnal wetting with NO daytime symptoms [1]
• Non-monosymptomatic: nocturnal enuresis WITH daytime symptoms (urgency, frequency, incontinence, UTIs) [1]
• This case: monosymptomatic — no daytime symptoms, no UTIs, no constipation [1]
• Primary: never achieved 6 months of consecutive night dryness [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Nocturnal polyuria: insufficient nocturnal ADH secretion → excessive overnight urine production [1]
• Detrusor overactivity: nocturnal uninhibited contractions → reduced functional bladder capacity [1]
• Failure to arousal: high arousal threshold, unable to wake to full bladder sensation [1]
• Treatment targets these mechanisms: desmopressin (polyuria), anticholinergics (overactivity), alarm (arousal) [1]
• Most children have combined mechanisms [1]

**(c)** [5 marks]
• Enuresis alarm: first-line for motivated families; conditions an arousal response to bladder fullness [1]
   • Evidence: ~60-70% success; 50% relapse rate [1]
• Desmopressin (DDAVP): synthetic ADH analogue — reduces overnight urine production [1]
   • Starting dose: 120-240 μg sublingual tablet [1]
   • Evidence: ~60-70% response while on treatment; higher relapse after stopping [1]
• Combination alarm + desmopressin for resistant cases [1]

**(d)** [3 marks]
• Full (maximal) bladder capacity: volume at natural voiding desire [1]
• Functional bladder capacity: volume at which child wets — often reduced in enuresis [1]
• Bellows manoeuvre: timed voiding with progressive stretching — increase intervals by 15-30 min weekly to improve functional capacity [1]

**(e)** [3 marks]
• Refer to specialist if: alarm + desmopressin fail after 6 months of combined treatment [1]
• Red flags: daytime wetting, urinary stream abnormalities, UTIs, neurological symptoms, polydipsia/polyuria [1]
• Also: severe family dysfunction or emotional distress [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This boy has primary monosymptomatic nocturnal enuresis. The three systems model explains the pathophysiology: nocturnal polyuria (inadequate ADH), detrusor overactivity (reduced functional capacity), and high arousal threshold. First-line treatment should be an enuresis alarm combined with desmopressin for rapid dryness. The strong family history (mother until age 14) supports a genetic component. If combined therapy fails after 6 months, referral to paediatric urology is indicated.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Articulates the three systems model clearly
• Distinguishes primary from secondary; monosymptomatic from non-monosymptomatic
• Provides specific desmopressin dose and formulation
• Recognises red flags requiring specialist referral

## QUESTION 3 — SAQ: Biofeedback and Urotherapy [Total: 8 marks] [FOUNDATION]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** Define biofeedback in the context of paediatric voiding dysfunction and describe how it is performed. **[4 marks]**

**(b)** List three different modalities of biofeedback and state the indications for each. **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [4 marks]
• Biofeedback: real-time visual/auditory feedback of pelvic floor muscle activity to retrain voiding [1]
• Electrodes placed on perineum/peri-anal region [1]
• Child watches a computer screen display of muscle activity [1]
• Goal: learn to relax pelvic floor during voiding (reverses the learned dyssynergia) [1]

**(b)** [4 marks]
• Surface EMG biofeedback — for dysfunctional voiding with pelvic floor overactivity [1]
• Uroflowmetry biofeedback — real-time flow curve feedback for staccato/interrupted voiding [1]
• Anorectal biofeedback — for constipation/dyssynergia; teaches relaxation during defecation [1]
• (Also: bladder scan biofeedback — visualising post-void residual to motivate complete emptying)

## QUESTION 4 — SAQ: Daytime Incontinence [Total: 8 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** Compare and contrast urgency incontinence and voiding postponement (lazy bladder syndrome). **[4 marks]**

**(b)** State the urodynamic findings expected in each condition. **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [4 marks]
• Urgency incontinence: sudden strong urge to void with inability to hold → wetting; frequent voids, small volumes [1]
• Voiding postponement: child ignores bladder signals, holds for long periods → infrequent voids, large volumes → overflow or urge incontinence [1]
• Urgency incontinence: child tries to get to toilet but doesn't make it [1]
• Postponement: child avoids using toilet outside home → wetting on way home or after arriving [1]

**(b)** [4 marks]
• Urgency incontinence: urodynamics shows detrusor overactivity (involuntary contractions during filling) ± reduced cystometric capacity [1-2]
• Postponement: large cystometric capacity, normal/low-pressure filling, no detrusor overactivity [1-2]
• Both may show high post-void residual [1]

## QUESTION 5 — LCV: Complex Incontinence [Total: 35 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~40 minutes

─── STAGE 1: PRESENTATION ───────────────────────────────
A 9-year-old girl with spina bifida occulta (known L4 level) is referred with persistent daytime incontinence despite CIC. She has had multiple UTIs. She is on oxybutynin. Renal US shows mild bilateral hydronephrosis.

**Q1a.** What is the most likely cause of her incontinence and hydronephrosis? **[3 marks]**
**Q1b.** What investigation is essential to guide further management? **[3 marks]**

─── STAGE 2: INVESTIGATION ──────────────────────────────
Urodynamics show a small capacity bladder (120 mL, expected 300 mL), poor compliance (filling pressure 35 cmH₂O at capacity), and detrusor overactivity with leak point pressure of 40 cmH₂O.

**Q2a.** Interpret these findings in the context of her spina bifida. What is the risk of these pressures to her renal function? **[5 marks]**
**Q2b.** Discuss the medical management options beyond oral oxybutynin. **[5 marks]**

─── STAGE 3: MANAGEMENT DECISION ────────────────────────
She continues to have high-pressure incontinence and recurrent UTIs despite maximum medical therapy.

**Q3a.** What surgical options are available for a child with neuropathic bladder refractory to medical management? **[5 marks]**
**Q3b.** Describe the technique of enterocystoplasty (bladder augmentation), including bowel segment choice and surgical steps. **[6 marks]**

─── STAGE 4: COMPLICATION AND FOLLOW-UP ────────────────
Post-augmentation, she does well for 2 years then develops recurrent UTIs with mucus, bladder stones, and metabolic acidosis.

**Q4a.** Explain the metabolic complications of enterocystoplasty and their management. **[5 marks]**
**Q4b.** Discuss the long-term malignancy risk associated with enterocystoplasty and the recommended surveillance. **[3 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**FULL MARKING GUIDE:**

**Q1a.** [3 marks]
• Neurogenic bladder with high-pressure storage (low compliance, detrusor overactivity) despite CIC and oxybutynin [2]
• High bladder pressures → back-pressure → hydronephrosis [1]

**Q1b.** [3 marks]
• Video-urodynamics (fill MCU with pressure monitoring) — gold standard [2]
• Assesses bladder capacity, compliance, filling pressures, leak point pressure, and presence of VUR [1]

**Q2a.** [5 marks]
• Small capacity (<50% expected), poor compliance (filling pressure >20 cmH₂O at capacity), detrusor overactivity [1]
• Leak point pressure 40 cmH₂O > threshold of 30-40 cmH₂O — "hostile bladder" [1]
• Elevated storage pressures → impaired ureteric drainage → progressive hydronephrosis → renal scarring → CKD [1]
• Risk of ESRD if pressures not reduced [1]
• Upper tract deterioration can be silent — no pain, no symptoms until CKD advanced [1]

**Q2b.** [5 marks]
• Oxybutynin dose optimisation (max 5 mg TDS in older children) [1]
• CIC regimen optimisation: frequency 4-5 times/day [1]
• If oxybutynin ineffective: change to solifenacin or tolterodine [1]
• Intradetrusor botulinum toxin A (Botox) — 100-300 units divided across detrusor [1]
• α-blockers (tamsulosin) to reduce bladder outlet resistance [1]

**Q3a.** [5 marks]
• Enterocystoplasty (augmentation ileocystoplasty or sigmoid cystoplasty) — gold standard [1]
• Mitrofanoff channel (appendix conduit to skin) if urethral CIC difficult [1]
• Bladder neck reconstruction ± sling if sphincteric incontinence [1]
• Urinary diversion (ileal conduit) — rarely in children; last resort [1]
• Artificial urinary sphincter (AUS) — possible but high revision rate in children [1]

**Q3b.** [6 marks]
• Bowel preparation pre-operatively [1]
• Harvest 15-20 cm of terminal ileum (20 cm from ileocaecal valve), preserving vascular arcade [1]
• Open and detubularise bowel segment along antimesenteric border [1]
• Configuration: "cup patch" or "clam" — U-shaped or S-shaped [1]
• Anastomose: bivalve bladder in coronal plane, anastomose opened bowel to bladder plate with running 4-0 absorbable suture [1]
• Mitrofanoff channel: appendix mobilised with mesentery, implanted into augmented bladder, opened to stoma at umbilicus [1]

**Q4a.** [5 marks]
• Metabolic: hyperchloraemic metabolic acidosis (bowel mucosa absorbs NH₄Cl and HCO₃ is lost) — correct with NaHCO₃ or chlorpromazine [1]
• Mucus production — regular bladder washouts with saline ± acetylcysteine [1]
• Bladder stones — form on mucus/pus nidi; require endoscopic or open removal [1]
• UTIs — intermittent catheterisation introduces bacteria; treat symptomatic only; chronic suppression sometimes used [1]
• Vitamin B12 deficiency — if >30 cm ileum resected; monitor annually after 5 years [1]

**Q4b.** [3 marks]
• Malignancy risk: adenocarcinoma, transitional cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma [1]
• Latency: average 10-20 years post-augmentation [1]
• Surveillance: annual US ± cystoscopy and urine cytology starting 10 years after augmentation [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Quotes specific urodynamic thresholds (leak point pressure >30-40 cmH₂O = hostile)
• Describes full enterocystoplasty procedure with surgical detail
• Discusses all related complications (metabolic, mucus, stones, B12, malignancy)
• Mentions Mitrofanoff channel's role in adolescent independence

## QUESTION 6 — EMQ: Incontinence by Type [Total: 10 marks] [BOARD]

**Theme: Classification of Paediatric Incontinence**

**OPTIONS (each option may be used once, more than once, or not at all):**

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| A. Monosymptomatic nocturnal enuresis | F. Neurogenic bladder |
| B. Non-monosymptomatic nocturnal enuresis | G. Ectopic ureter |
| C. Urgency incontinence (overactive bladder) | H. Giggle incontinence |
| D. Voiding postponement | I. Stress incontinence |
| E. Dysfunctional voiding | J. Continuous incontinence |

**For each of the following clinical scenarios, select the single most appropriate type of incontinence:**

**1.** A 7-year-old girl is always wet between voids. She voids normally in the toilet but is constantly damp. Examination reveals moisture at the introitus separate from the urethral meatus. **[1 mark]**

**2.** A 10-year-old boy who is dry at night but wets his underwear several times a day. He feels a sudden strong urge and cannot reach the toilet in time. He voids 12-15 times/day. **[1 mark]**

**3.** A 6-year-old girl with night wetting (6/7 nights) and occasional daytime urgency. She has constipation and recurrent UTIs. She has never been dry at night for more than 2 weeks. **[1 mark]**

**4.** An 8-year-old boy who wets only when he laughs. He has no other urinary symptoms and is dry at night. **[1 mark]**

**5.** A 5-year-old girl who holds her urine for 6-8 hours during the day, crossing her legs and dancing. She has infrequent voiding (3-4 times/day) with large volume voids and occasional wetting on the way to the toilet. **[1 mark]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**ANSWERS & RATIONALE:**

**1.** **J** — Continuous incontinence. The finding of moisture at the introitus separate from the normal urethral meatus suggests an ectopic ureter inserting distal to the sphincter mechanism — this causes constant, continuous wetting regardless of voiding.

**2.** **C** — Urgency incontinence (overactive bladder). The sudden urge với inability to reach the toilet, high voiding frequency, and daytime wetting with normal night dryness is classic overactive bladder. OAB is common in school-age children.

**3.** **B** — Non-monosymptomatic nocturnal enuresis. Night wetting + daytime symptoms (urgency) + BBD (constipation, UTIs) makes this non-monosymptomatic. The management differs from monosymptomatic — urotherapy and BBD treatment are needed first.

**4.** **H** — Giggle incontinence. Urinary incontinence triggered exclusively by laughter is a recognised entity. It is distinct from stress incontinence (which is rare in children) and is thought to involve a central nervous system mechanism.

**5.** **D** — Voiding postponement. The pattern of infrequent voiding, holding behaviour (dancing, crossing legs), large volume voids, and wetting on the way to the toilet is classic for voiding postponement or lazy bladder syndrome.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Confusing continuous incontinence (always wet ± ectopic ureter) with urgency incontinence (wet only when urge overwhelms)
• Not recognising the significance of daytime symptoms in nocturnal enuresis — it changes classification and management
• Missing the distinction between monosymptomatic and non-monosymptomatic enuresis
• Giggle incontinence is often misclassified as stress incontinence

**EXAMINER'S NOTES — COMMON SCRIPT PATTERNS TO WATCH FOR:**
• Many candidates focus exclusively on pharmacotherapy and neglect urotherapy, which is the cornerstone of BBD management
• The distinction between monosymptomatic and non-monosymptomatic enuresis is frequently lost — candidates should always check for daytime symptoms
• Biofeedback is under-utilised in practice and under-mentioned in exams
• Long-term complications of enterocystoplasty (stones, acidosis, B12 deficiency, malignancy) are a strong discriminator
