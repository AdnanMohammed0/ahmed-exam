## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: The Newborn with Ambiguous Genitalia [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A term newborn is noted to have atypical genitalia: a 1.5 cm phallic structure, fused labioscrotal folds with rugae, and a single perineal opening at the base of the phallus. No gonads are palpable. The baby is otherwise well and feeding. A 20-week antenatal scan suggested a female karyotype.

**(a)** List the initial investigations you would arrange in the first 48 hours and explain the rationale for each. **[6 marks]**

**(b)** The karyotype returns 46XX and serum 17-hydroxyprogesterone (17-OHP) is markedly elevated (850 nmol/L; normal <30). What is the most likely diagnosis, and describe the underlying biochemical pathway defect? **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Outline the immediate medical management of this condition and the rationale for treatment. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the long-term management of a 46XX CAH patient raised female, including genital surgery, fertility, and the psychological aspects of DSD care. **[6 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [6 marks] — 1 per valid point up to 6:
• Karyotype (G-banded chromosomes or FISH for SRY) — first-line; determines chromosomal sex [1]
• Serum 17-OHP — screening test for 21-hydroxylase deficiency CAH [1]
• Electrolytes, glucose — salt-wasting CAH can cause hyponatraemia, hyperkalaemia within days [1]
• Pelvic US — identify Müllerian structures (uterus, vagina) and gonads [1]
• hCG stimulation test — assess androgen production (Leydig cell function) [1]
• Baseline adrenal androgens — androstenedione, testosterone [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (CAH) due to 21-hydroxylase deficiency [1]
• 21-hydroxylase converts 17-hydroxyprogesterone to 11-deoxycortisol [1]
• Defect → cortisol deficiency → ACTH drive → adrenal androgen excess [1]
• Also: aldosterone deficiency (salt-wasting in ~75%) [1]
• Prader V virilisation with 17-OHP 850 nmol/L is classic salt-wasting CAH [1]

**(c)** [5 marks]
• Hydrocortisone replacement: 10-15 mg/m²/day in 3 divided doses — replaces cortisol, suppresses ACTH → reduces androgen excess [1]
• Fludrocortisone (mineralocorticoid): 100-200 μg/day — for salt-wasting [1]
• Sodium chloride supplement: 1-3 g/day in divided doses [1]
• Stress dose steroids: triple dose for illness, fever, surgery [1]
• Monitor: electrolytes, 17-OHP, renin, growth [1]

**(d)** [6 marks]
• Genital surgery: controversial and individualised [1]
• Current approach: defer feminising genitoplasty (clitoral reduction, vaginoplasty) until adolescence or patient consent [1]
• Earlier approach (Chicago consensus 2005): early surgery (6-12 months) for severe virilisation [1]
• Fertility: women with CAH can conceive but may need glucocorticoid optimisation [1]
• Gender identity: 95%+ of 46XX CAH raised female identify as female [1]
• Psychological support: multidisciplinary team — endocrinologist, psychologist, urologist, gynaecologist [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This newborn has 46XX CAH (21-hydroxylase deficiency) — the most common cause of ambiguous genitalia in a 46XX infant. The biochemical deficit prevents cortisol synthesis, driving ACTH-dependent adrenal androgen excess → virilisation. Immediate management requires hydrocortisone and fludrocortisone replacement plus salt supplementation. The multidisciplinary DSD team must counsel the family. Long-term management involves medical optimisation, careful consideration of any feminising genitoplasty (ideally deferred to adolescence), fertility counselling, and psychological support. With optimal management, these individuals live healthy, fertile lives as women.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Gives specific 17-OHP threshold values
• Presents both early and deferred surgery approaches with the controversy
• Discusses fertility outcomes in CAH
• Emphasises multidisciplinary DSD team approach

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: 46XY DSD [Total: 20 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 12-month-old child, previously raised female, is referred with a right inguinal hernia. During hernia repair, a testis is found in the hernia sac. The child has no palpable gonads. External genitalia are female with a normal clitoris and separate urethral and vaginal openings. Pelvic US suggests a short blind-ending vagina and no uterus.

**(a)** What is the most likely diagnosis, and what investigation would confirm it? **[4 marks]**

**(b)** Discuss the differential diagnosis of a 46XY DSD with female phenotype and its management. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** The parents are devastated by the news. Discuss the ethical considerations and the process of gender assignment in this child. **[6 marks]**

**(d)** Outline the surgical management of this child, including gonadectomy timing and vaginal reconstruction. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [4 marks] — 1 per valid point up to 4:
• Complete androgen insensitivity syndrome (CAIS) [1]
• Defect: AR gene mutation (X-linked) → complete resistance to androgens [1]
• 46XY karyotype with testes producing normal testosterone and AMH [1]
• Confirm: genetic testing for AR mutation [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• CAIS — AR mutation; female phenotype, no uterus, testis present [1]
• 5α-reductase deficiency — will virilise at puberty, has Wolffian structures, testosterone:DHT ratio elevated [1]
• 17β-HSD deficiency — similar to 5α-reductase, androstenedione:testosterone ratio elevated [1]
• Leydig cell hypoplasia — LH receptor mutation, low testosterone, hCG stimulation test abnormal [1]
• Gonadal dysgenesis (partial/complete) — may have streak gonads, Turner stigmata [1]

**(c)** [6 marks]
• Disclosure: child already raised female for 12 months — this is her gender identity [1]
• CAIS females have female gender identity and normal female sexual function [1]
• Do not change gender assignment based solely on karyotype [1]
• Ethical: sex assignment should be based on gender identity potential, not chromosomal sex [1]
• Psychosocial: immediate referral to child psychologist and DSD support groups [1]
• Honest, age-appropriate disclosure to child at appropriate developmental stage [1]

**(d)** [5 marks]
• Gonadectomy: deferred until after puberty (testes produce hormones that help feminisation) [1]
• If performed earlier: indicated if inguinal/testicular pain or malignancy concern [1]
• Malignancy risk in CAIS: 2-5% after puberty — lower than pure gonadal dysgenesis (30-50%) [1]
• Vaginal reconstruction: if vaginal hypoplasia, sequential dilation under oestrogen therapy [1]
• Vaginoplasty (McIndoe or sigmoid): if dilation fails or not tolerated [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This child has CAIS — a 46XY individual with complete androgen insensitivity. She has been raised female, has female gender identity, and should remain female. The finding of a testis in a hernia sac is the classic presentation. She will need gonadectomy, but this can be deferred until after puberty to allow natural feminisation. Vaginal dilation or reconstruction may be needed. The key is multidisciplinary care: the parents need compassionate counselling and the child needs age-appropriate disclosure. Female gender assignment is appropriate.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Distinguishes CAIS from 5α-reductase deficiency, 17β-HSD deficiency, and gonadal dysgenesis
• Provides specific malignancy risk percentages
• Discusses deferred gonadectomy (preserves natural puberty)
• Addresses the ethics of gender assignment based on karyotype vs identity

## QUESTION 3 — SAQ: Prader Classification [Total: 8 marks] [FOUNDATION]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** Describe the Prader staging system for genital virilisation in 46XX CAH. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** What is the clinical relevance of the Prader stage for surgical planning? **[3 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [5 marks]
• Prader I: clitoromegaly alone, normal vaginal and urethral openings [1]
• Prader II: clitoromegaly + posterior labial fusion with funnel-shaped introitus [1]
• Prader III: prominent clitoromegaly with urogenital sinus opening on the perineum [1]
• Prader IV: phallic structure with labial fusion; single opening at base [1]
• Prader V: phallic urethra (penile appearance) — indistinguishable from male with cryptorchidism [1]

**(b)** [3 marks]
• Higher Prader stages (III-V) require more complex reconstructive surgery (vaginoplasty, clitoroplasty) [1]
• Urogenital sinus length predicts surgical approach [1]
• Guides counselling of parents about expected surgical complexity [1]

## QUESTION 4 — SAQ: Long-term Outcomes in DSD [Total: 8 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** Discuss the long-term fertility outcomes for a woman with 46XX CAH who had early feminising genitoplasty. **[4 marks]**

**(b)** What are the recommendations for transition of care from paediatric to adult DSD services? **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [4 marks]
• Fertility is reduced but possible: 50-70% of women with CAH attempt pregnancy [1]
• Factors: vaginoplasty adequacy, compliance with glucocorticoids, anovulation from androgen excess [1]
• Preconception: optimise glucocorticoids [1]
• Obstetric: higher C-section rate, third-trimester steroid stress dosing [1]

**(b)** [4 marks]
• Transition clinic: joint paediatric-adult DSD team [1]
• Education: condition understanding, medication adherence [1]
• Psychological support for emerging sexuality and body image [1]
• Gynaecology: assessment of vaginal patency and fertility [1]

## QUESTION 5 — LCV: DSD Team Decision [Total: 35 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~40 minutes

─── STAGE 1: PRESENTATION ───────────────────────────────
A newborn is born at term with ambiguous genitalia: a 2 cm phallus, single perineal opening, and no palpable gonads. The baby is clinically well.

**Q1a.** What are the first three investigations you order? **[3 marks]**
**Q1b.** Who should be involved in the immediate counselling of the family? **[3 marks]**

─── STAGE 2: RESULTS ────────────────────────────────────
Karyotype: 46XY. 17-OHP normal. Electrolytes normal. hCG stimulation test: baseline T low, stimulated T low, DHT low.

**Q2a.** What is the likely differential diagnosis? **[3 marks]**
**Q2b.** What additional tests would help narrow the differential (AMH, androstenedione, urinary steroid profile)? **[4 marks]**

─── STAGE 3: GENDER ASSIGNMENT ──────────────────────────
The family is from a conservative background and wants the baby raised male. The phallus is 1.5 cm (below the 2.5 cm threshold for male assignment). No Müllerian structures are seen on US.

**Q3a.** How do you counsel the family about the feasibility of male assignment? **[5 marks]**
**Q3b.** Discuss the ethical framework for gender assignment in 46XY DSD. **[5 marks]**

─── STAGE 4: SURGERY AND FOLLOW-UP ──────────────────────
The family decides on female assignment. A diagnosis of partial androgen insensitivity syndrome (PAIS) is confirmed.

**Q4a.** Outline the surgical plan (gonadectomy, genitoplasty). **[5 marks]**
**Q4b.** Discuss the importance of multidisciplinary long-term follow-up and the role of peer support groups. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**FULL MARKING GUIDE:**

**Q1a.** [3 marks]
• Karyotype + SRY FISH [1]
• Serum 17-OHP + electrolytes [1]
• Pelvic US [1]

**Q1b.** [3 marks]
• DSD multidisciplinary team: neonatologist, endocrinologist, paediatric urologist, psychologist [1]
• Named DSD nurse specialist [1]
• Avoid "sex assignment" language initially — explain "diagnosis in progress" [1]

**Q2a.** [3 marks]
• Partial androgen insensitivity (PAIS) — most likely [1]
• 5α-reductase deficiency [1]
• 17β-HSD deficiency [1]

**Q2b.** [4 marks]
• AMH: high in CAIS/PAIS (testes produce AMH) [1]
• Androstenedione:testosterone ratio (elevated in 17β-HSD deficiency) [1]
• Testosterone:DHT ratio (elevated in 5α-reductase deficiency) [1]
• Urinary steroid profile: abnormal in some forms of CAH [1]

**Q3a.** [5 marks]
• Phallic length of 1.5 cm is significantly below the 2.5 cm threshold for male assignment [1]
• Inability to achieve functional male phallus in adulthood [1]
• Likely need for multiple masculinising surgeries with poor outcome [1]
• Spontaneous virilisation at puberty is possible (especially if 5α-reductase deficiency) [1]
• Honest discussion: quality of life, sexual function, and fertility [1]

**Q3b.** [5 marks]
• Principle of best interest of the child [1]
• Delay non-essential surgery until child can participate in decision (Chicago consensus) [1]
• Respect cultural background but base decision on evidence [1]
• Avoid irreversible surgery that may conflict with later gender identity [1]
• The child's future wellbeing, not parental or societal pressure, is paramount [1]

**Q4a.** [5 marks]
• Gonadectomy: before puberty or deferred depending on malignancy risk [1]
• Malignancy risk in PAIS: moderate (5-10%) [1]
• Vaginoplasty: if needed; may use graduated dilation [1]
• Clitoroplasty: if significant clitoromegaly causing discomfort [1]
• Consider deferring any irreversible surgery to adolescence [1]

**Q4b.** [5 marks]
• Paediatric endocrinology: lifelong glucocorticoid management (if CAH) [1]
• Psychology: gender identity, body image, sexual health [1]
• Transition programme: to adult gynaecology, endocrinology, urology [1]
• Peer support groups: connect family with others who have navigated similar decisions [1]
• Fertility counselling: if potential for egg/sperm preservation exists [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Provides specific phallic length thresholds (2.5 cm) for male assignment
• Discusses biochemical differentiation between PAIS, 5α-reductase deficiency, and 17β-HSD deficiency
• Articulates the ethical framework for gender assignment (best interests, delay non-essential surgery)
• Includes peer support groups as part of management

## QUESTION 6 — EMQ: DSD Classification [Total: 10 marks] [BOARD]

**Theme: Classification of Disorders of Sex Development**

**OPTIONS (each option may be used once, more than once, or not at all):**

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| A. 46XX CAH (21-hydroxylase deficiency) | F. Ovotesticular DSD |
| B. Complete androgen insensitivity | G. 45X (Turner syndrome) |
| C. Partial androgen insensitivity | H. Mixed gonadal dysgenesis (45X/46XY) |
| D. 5α-reductase deficiency | I. Leydig cell hypoplasia |
| E. 17β-HSD deficiency | J. Isolated hypospadias (not DSD) |

**For each of the following clinical scenarios, select the single most appropriate diagnosis:**

**1.** A newborn with female external genitalia, bilateral inguinal masses (testes), and 46XY karyotype. US shows no uterus. At puberty, breast development occurs but no menses. **[1 mark]**

**2.** A 16-year-old with clitoromegaly and primary amenorrhoea. She has severe acne and hirsutism. She is 46XX. She is normotensive. 17-OHP is 650 nmol/L. **[1 mark]**

**3.** A prepubertal boy with small phallus, perineal hypospadias, and no palpable gonads. Family history: two maternal uncles had similar anomalies. 46XY. **[1 mark]**

**4.** A newborn with ambiguous genitalia, a gonad on the right, and a streak gonad on the left. Karyotype 45X/46XY. **[1 mark]**

**5.** A 14-year-old raised female who virilises at puberty (voice deepens, clitoral growth, facial hair). No breast development. Labial gonads palpable. 46XY. Testosterone:DHT ratio is markedly elevated. **[1 mark]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**ANSWERS & RATIONALE:**

**1.** **B** — Complete androgen insensitivity syndrome (CAIS). The classic triad: female external genitalia, bilateral testes (in inguinal canals or labia), 46XY karyotype. No uterus because testes produce AMH in utero. At puberty, testosterone is aromatised to oestrogen → breast development. No menses because no uterus.

**2.** **A** — 46XX CAH (21-hydroxylase deficiency). The markedly elevated 17-OHP (650 nmol/L) is diagnostic for 21-hydroxylase deficiency. Virilisation from excess adrenal androgens causes clitoromegaly, hirsutism, and amenorrhoea. This is a non-salt-wasting form (normotensive).

**3.** **C** — Partial androgen insensitivity (PAIS). X-linked inheritance pattern (maternal uncles), 46XY with under-masculinisation (small phallus, perineal hypospadias, impalpable gonads). The phenotype is variable; PAIS lies on the spectrum between complete AIS and normal male.

**4.** **H** — Mixed gonadal dysgenesis (45X/46XY). The mosaic karyotype with asymmetric gonads (testis on one side, streak on the other) is diagnostic. This is one of the most common causes of ambiguous genitalia in a 46XY infant.

**5.** **D** — 5α-reductase deficiency. Virilisation at puberty (through testosterone effect) suggests this condition. The elevated testosterone:DHT ratio is diagnostic. These individuals have male gender identity (most raised male or reassign at puberty) and can produce sperm via TESE.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Confusing CAIS with PAIS — the presence of ambiguous rather than female genitalia distinguishes
• Missing 5α-reductase deficiency in a "female" who virilises at puberty
• Assuming all 46XY DSD is AIS — consider gonadal dysgenesis and enzyme deficiencies
• Not recognising the asymmetric gonads of mixed gonadal dysgenesis as a diagnostic clue

**EXAMINER'S NOTES — COMMON SCRIPT PATTERNS TO WATCH FOR:**
• DSD management has evolved rapidly — many candidates still describe outdated approaches (early feminising surgery without consent, concealment of diagnosis)
• The Chicago consensus (2005) and more recent DSD guidelines (2016) are key references — knowledge of these is discriminating
• 5α-reductase deficiency is a favourite viva topic because of the gender reversal at puberty
• Many candidates cannot give the specific diagnostic hormone ratios (T:DHT, A:T) — these are strong discriminators
