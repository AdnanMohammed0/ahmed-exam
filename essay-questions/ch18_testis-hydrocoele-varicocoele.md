## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Cryptorchidism [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 6-month-old term male is referred with bilateral cryptorchidism. On examination, neither testis is palpable in the scrotum, inguinal canal, or pubic region. The scrotum is underdeveloped.

**(a)** Outline the normal descent of the testis and state the timing at which spontaneous descent is no longer expected. **[4 marks]**

**(b)** Describe the evaluation of the impalpable testis, including imaging, laboratory tests, and the role of examination under anaesthesia. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Discuss the management options for bilateral impalpable testes, including the timing of surgery and the choice between one-stage and two-stage Fowler-Stephens orchidopexy. **[7 marks]**

**(d)** Explain the rationale for early orchidopexy (recommended at 6-12 months) in terms of germ cell preservation, hormonal function, and malignancy risk. **[6 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [4 marks] — 1 per valid point up to 4:
• Testicular descent: two phases — transabdominal (8-15 weeks, gubernacular migration) and inguinoscrotal (24-35 weeks, androgen-mediated) [1]
• Spontaneous descent: expected by 3 months in most; very rare after 6 months [1]
• Orchiopexy recommended at 6-12 months per EAU/ESPU guidelines [1]
• After 12-18 months, germ cell loss accelerates [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Examination under anaesthesia: may palpate a testis that was impalpable awake [1]
• US: limited sensitivity for impalpable testis; not reliable for localisation [1]
• MRI: better soft tissue contrast, can localise intra-abdominal testis [1]
• Laparoscopy: gold standard for impalpable testis — identifies testis, determines viability, guides approach [1]
• Hormones: FSH/LH, AMH, inhibin B — assess functional testicular tissue [1]

**(c)** [7 marks]
• Laparoscopy first: identify testis location [1]
• If testis is intra-abdominal and viable: options include [1]
• One-stage Fowler-Stephens: ligate testicular vessels, mobilise testis on vas deferens and collateral supply (cremasteric, deferential) — for low intra-abdominal testis [1]
• Two-stage Fowler-Stephens: clip vessels at stage 1; wait 6 months for collateralisation; mobilise testis at stage 2 — for high intra-abdominal testis [1]
• Success: two-stage better than one-stage (85-90% vs 65-75%) [1]
• Microvascular autotransplantation: if Fowler-Stephens not possible [1]
• Orchiectomy: if testis is atrophic/hypoplastic or non-viable after contralateral testis is normal [1]

**(d)** [6 marks]
• Germ cell loss: begins after 12 months; progressive loss of spermatogonia [1]
• Fertility: bilateral cryptorchidism → reduced sperm count even after successful orchidopexy (paternity rate ~50-65%) [1]
• Hormonal function: Leydig cell function preserved even if testis not in scrotum; but early surgery may improve endocrine profile [1]
• Malignancy risk: 2-8x increased vs general population; risk reduced by scrotal placement (allows self-examination) [1]
• Tumour risk higher in intra-abdominal vs inguinal testis [1]
• Orchiopexy does NOT reduce malignancy risk but enables examination and early detection [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This boy has bilateral impalpable testes — urgent referral is indicated for evaluation within 6 months. Laparoscopy is the gold standard for localisation. If both testes are intra-abdominal and viable, I would recommend a staged Fowler-Stephens orchidopexy (clip vessels, wait 6 months, then mobilise). Early surgery (6-12 months) is critical: germ cell preservation is time-sensitive, and progressive spermatogonial loss begins after 12 months. Fertility is reduced even with optimal treatment, especially in bilateral cases. Malignancy risk remains elevated lifelong — parents should be counselled about testicular self-examination in adolescence.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Articulates the two phases of testicular descent with specific gestational ages
• Discusses one-stage vs two-stage Fowler-Stephens with specific success rates
• Explains WHY early surgery matters — germ cell loss after 12 months
• Discusses malignancy risk accurately (orchiopexy does not reduce risk, enables detection)

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Varicocoele in Adolescence [Total: 20 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 14-year-old boy presents with a "bag of worms" feeling in his left scrotum which has been present for 6 months. It is painless but he is self-conscious about the appearance. He is Tanner stage 4. Both testes are in the scrotum; the left testis appears smaller than the right (volume 12 mL vs 18 mL). He has no other symptoms.

**(a)** Classify this varicocoele by grade and state the mechanism of left-sided predominance. **[4 marks]**

**(b)** Discuss the effect of varicocoele on testicular function and the evidence linking varicocoele to infertility. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** What are the indications for varicocoele treatment in an adolescent? **[4 marks]**

**(d)** Compare the surgical options for varicocoele repair in an adolescent — including Palomo (laparoscopic), subinguinal microscopic, and radiological embolisation — with success and complication rates. **[7 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [4 marks] — 1 per valid point up to 4:
• Grade II varicocoele: palpable without Valsalva, visible [1]
• Left-sided predominance (90%): left spermatic vein drains into left renal vein at right angle (higher pressure); right drains into IVC obliquely [1]
• Absent valves in left spermatic vein → reflux of blood → venous dilatation [1]
• Nutcracker phenomenon: compression of left renal vein between SMA and aorta → increased pressure [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Varicocoele → increased scrotal temperature (1-2°C) → impaired spermatogenesis [1]
• Stasis of warm blood → reactive oxygen species → germ cell apoptosis [1]
• Effect on semen analysis: reduced sperm count, motility, and increased abnormal morphology [1]
• Catch-up growth: after repair, testicular volume may normalise in adolescents [1]
• Evidence: varicocoeles are found in 35-40% of infertile men vs 15% of general population [1]

**(c)** [4 marks]
• Testicular size discrepancy >2 mL or >20% volume difference [1]
• Bilateral varicocoele [1]
• Grade III varicocoele or symptomatic (pain, cosmetic) [1]
• Abnormal semen analysis (if age ≥18 and requesting fertility) [1]

**(d)** [7 marks]
• Palomo (laparoscopic high ligation): ligates spermatic vessels at internal ring ± artery preservation [1]
   • Success: ~85-90%; recurrence ~5-10% [1]
   • Complications: hydrocoele (5-15% — from ligated lymphatic), testicular atrophy (rare) [1]
• Subinguinal microsurgical varicocoelectomy (gold standard): uses microscope — identifies and preserves artery, lymphatics, vas [1]
   • Success: ~95%; recurrence ~1-2%; hydrocoele ~1% [1]
   • Advantages: lowest recurrence and complication [1]
• Radiological embolisation: sclerotherapy/coil occlusion via femoral vein [1]
   • Success: ~80-85%; recurrence ~10-15% [1]
   • Advantages: no incision; disadvantages: radiation, failure if anatomy unfavourable [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This boy has a left grade II varicocoele with testicular growth asymmetry (12 vs 18 mL — 33% difference). Indications for treatment are present: size discrepancy >20%. The subinguinal microsurgical approach offers the lowest recurrence (1-2%) and hydrocoele (1%) rates. Laparoscopic Palomo is also effective but has higher hydrocoele risk. Post-operatively, I would monitor with scrotal US at 6 months to confirm catch-up growth.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Discusses Nutcracker phenomenon as mechanism for left-sided varicocoele
• Quotes specific volume discrepancy threshold (20% or 2 mL) for intervention
• Compares techniques with specific recurrence/hydrocoele rates
• Recommends subinguinal microsurgical as gold standard

## QUESTION 3 — SAQ: Communicating Hydrocoele [Total: 8 marks] [FOUNDATION]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** Explain the difference between a communicating and a non-communicating hydrocoele in a child, including the anatomical basis and natural history. **[4 marks]**

**(b)** At what age would you recommend surgical repair of a communicating hydrocoele and why? **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [4 marks]
• Communicating: processus vaginalis remains patent → peritoneal fluid enters tunica vaginalis → fluctuates in size (larger in evening) [1-2]
• Non-communicating: processus vaginalis closed; fluid localised to tunica → no size variation [1]
• Both present as scrotal swelling that transilluminates [1]

**(b)** [4 marks]
• Non-communicating in infant <12-18 months: observe — spontaneous resolution [1]
• Communicating hydrocoele beyond 12-18 months: repair recommended [1]
• Small risk of strangulation if associated with hernia [1]
• Repair: ligation of processus vaginalis via groin incision (same as hernia repair) [1]

## QUESTION 4 — SAQ: Atrophic Testis [Total: 8 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 12-year-old boy has a left testicular volume of 3 mL (right 15 mL) after a failed orchidopexy at age 3.

**(a)** State two causes of testicular atrophy after orchidopexy. **[2 marks]**

**(b)** Discuss the management options for a severely atrophic testis in an adolescent. **[6 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [2 marks — 1 each]
• Torsion of the spermatic cord during mobilisation [1]
• Ischaemic injury from excessive tension or vessel damage [1]
• (Also: compartment syndrome from tight closure)

**(b)** [6 marks]
• US: confirm atrophy, exclude tumour [1]
• Hormones: AMH/inhibin B assess residual function [1]
• Orchiectomy: if testis is non-viable, non-palpable, or at high malignancy risk [1]
• Testicular prosthesis insertion: discuss with patient — cosmetic and psychological [1]
• If contralateral testis is normal: orchiectomy preserves detection by self-examination of contralateral [1]
• If contralateral abnormal: consider biopsy and possible preservation [1]

## QUESTION 5 — LCV: Impalpable Testis [Total: 35 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~40 minutes

─── STAGE 1: PRESENTATION ───────────────────────────────
A 9-month-old boy with a left impalpable testis. The right testis is descended and normal. Examination under anaesthesia cannot palpate a left testis.

**Q1a.** What is the next step in management? **[2 marks]**
**Q1b.** List the possible findings at laparoscopy and the management of each. **[6 marks]**

─── STAGE 2: LAPAROSCOPY ──────────────────────────────
Laparoscopy reveals an intra-abdominal left testis approximately 2 cm from the internal ring. Vessels and vas are normal. The testis appears viable.

**Q2a.** What are the surgical options? **[3 marks]**
**Q2b.** Describe the one-stage laparoscopic Fowler-Stephens orchidopexy. **[6 marks]**

─── STAGE 3: OUTCOME ──────────────────────────────────
Post-operatively, the testis is in the scrotum. At 1 year, the testis is palpable and of normal consistency. At 15 years, the right testis is 20 mL, left is 12 mL.

**Q3a.** Does this represent normal growth or growth failure? **[2 marks]**
**Q3b.** What advice would you give regarding fertility and malignancy risk? **[6 marks]**

─── STAGE 4: LATE EFFECTS ──────────────────────────────
At 30, he has oligospermia and difficulty conceiving.

**Q4a.** Discuss the contribution of unilateral cryptorchidism to infertility. **[4 marks]**
**Q4b.** What fertility options are available? **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**FULL MARKING GUIDE:**

**Q1a.** [2 marks]
• Laparoscopy (gold standard) — diagnostic and potentially therapeutic in one setting [2]

**Q1b.** [6 marks]
• Normal vessels passing through internal ring → testis in inguinal canal → open inguinal exploration [1]
• Intra-abdominal testis with adequate vessel length → one-stage orchidopexy or Fowler-Stephens [1]
• High intra-abdominal testis with short vessels → two-stage Fowler-Stephens [1]
• Blind-ending vessels (vanishing testis) → no further exploration; diagnosis: testicular agenesis [1]
• Atrophic testis/nubbin → remove [1]
• Testis in a normal scrotal position → no further treatment [1]

**Q2a.** [3 marks]
• Laparoscopic orchidopexy (one-stage with vessel preservation) — if vessels reach [1]
• Laparoscopic Fowler-Stephens (one-stage division) — if vessels short but collateral supply good [1]
• Two-stage Fowler-Stephens — if vessels very short [1]

**Q2b.** [6 marks]
• Mobilise testis via laparoscopy; assess vessel length [1]
• Divide testicular vessels between clips [1]
• Develop window in peritoneum medially to create tension-free path [1]
• Pass testis through medial canal into scrotum [1]
• Create dartos pouch in scrotum; secure testis [1]
• Confirm testicular position and Doppler pulse [1]

**Q3a.** [2 marks]
• Left testicular volume 12 mL vs right 20 mL — growth failure [1]
• Relative volume deficit of 40% suggests impaired growth from subtotal ischaemia or reduced function [1]

**Q3b.** [6 marks]
• Fertility: even unilateral cryptorchidism reduces paternity rates (70-80% vs 90% general) [1]
• Malignancy: 2-3x relative risk of testicular cancer; risk persists lifelong [1]
• Self-examination monthly from adolescence [1]
• Fertility evaluation: semen analysis, FSH, inhibin B [1]
• Cryopreservation if oligospermia detected [1]
• Testicular prosthesis discussion if orchiectomy considered [1]

**Q4a.** [4 marks]
• Unilateral cryptorchidism reduces paternity rate to ~70-80% [1]
• Proposed mechanisms: contralateral testis affected by autoimmune response to abnormal testis [1]
• or bilateral embryological defect affecting both testes [1]
• Semen analysis shows oligospermia in ~30-50% of unilateral cases [1]

**Q4b.** [4 marks]
• Lifestyle: healthy weight, avoid smoking/alcohol, loose underwear [1]
• Medical: clomiphene citrate (off-label) — may improve spermatogenesis [1]
• ART: IUI or IVF/ICSI depending on severity [1]
• Testicular sperm extraction (TESE) if azoospermia [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Provides complete laparoscopic algorithm for impalpable testis
• Discusses fertility outcomes for unilateral cryptorchidism (often overlooked vs bilateral)
• Describes testicular prosthesis counselling
• Includes long-term malignancy risk and self-examination

## QUESTION 6 — EMQ: Scrotal Swellings [Total: 10 marks] [BOARD]

**Theme: Scrotal Swellings in Children**

**OPTIONS (each option may be used once, more than once, or not at all):**

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| A. Communicating hydrocoele | F. Spermatocoele |
| B. Non-communicating hydrocoele | G. Varicocoele |
| C. Inguinoscrotal hernia | H. Testicular torsion |
| D. Epididymal cyst | I. Torsion of appendix testis |
| E. Orchitis/epididymitis | J. Testicular tumour |

**For each of the following clinical scenarios, select the single most appropriate diagnosis:**

**1.** An 8-month-old with a swelling that appears and disappears, is reducible with a clicking sensation, and transilluminates. **[1 mark]**

**2.** A 12-year-old with a "bag of worms" in the left scrotum that disappears when supine. **[1 mark]**

**3.** A 6-week-old with a tense, non-tender right scrotal swelling that transilluminates brightly. The testis cannot be palpated separately. It has been present since birth. **[1 mark]**

**4.** A 16-year-old with a firm, non-tender lump on the right testis. The testis feels heavy. Transillumination is negative. **[1 mark]**

**5.** An 8-year-old with an isolated, cystic, transilluminable swelling in the head of the epididymis. It does not extend into the inguinal canal. **[1 mark]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**ANSWERS & RATIONALE:**

**1.** **C** — Inguinoscrotal hernia. The reducible swelling that appears and disappears with a clicking sensation is a hernia. In infants, the processus vaginalis is patent, allowing bowel to enter the scrotum. Transillumination may be positive if fluid-containing bowel.

**2.** **G** — Varicocoele. The "bag of worms" feel and disappearance when supine (drains into renal vein) is classic. Left-sided predominance. This is a grade III varicocoele.

**3.** **A** — Communicating hydrocoele. A tense scrotal swelling present since birth that transilluminates is a hydrocoele. In neonates, the processus vaginalis is open. It usually resolves by 12-18 months.

**4.** **J** — Testicular tumour. The firm, non-tender lump with negative transillumination and heavy feel is suspicious for malignancy. Any solid intratesticular mass is considered malignant until proven otherwise. Urgent scrotal US and tumour markers are indicated.

**5.** **D** — Epididymal cyst. A cystic swelling in the head of the epididymis is an epididymal cyst/spermatocoele. It is separate from the testis and does not extend into the inguinal canal. Benign — no treatment needed.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Confusing communicating hydrocoele with hernia — both present with reducible inguinoscrotal swellings that transilluminate; history of "clicking" suggests hernia
• Missing testicular tumour in a teenager — any firm testicular mass is cancer until proven otherwise
• Assuming all scrotal swellings in newborns are hydrocoeles — hernia must be excluded
• Believing varicocoele is always benign — it may cause testicular growth failure

**EXAMINER'S NOTES — COMMON SCRIPT PATTERNS TO WATCH FOR:**
• Many candidates don't know the specific fertility outcomes for cryptorchidism (70-80% paternity for unilateral, 50-65% for bilateral)
• Laparoscopic management of impalpable testis is well-known, but the specific steps of Fowler-Stephens orchidopexy are poorly described
• Varicocoele indications are often too aggressive (many still recommend treating all adolescent varicocoeles)
• Testicular prosthesis counselling is almost never mentioned — it should be
