## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Renal Trauma [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 9-year-old boy falls from a tree (height ~4 metres) onto his right flank. He presents with right flank pain, visible haematuria, and bruising. He is haemodynamically stable. HR 100, BP 110/70, Hb 120 g/L.

**(a)** Describe the AAST renal injury grading system and classify the likely grade based on presentation with visible haematuria and flank bruising. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Outline the indications for CT imaging in paediatric renal trauma. **[4 marks]**

**(c)** Discuss the principles of non-operative management of renal trauma in children, including the criteria for conservative treatment. **[6 marks]**

**(d)** State the indications for surgical intervention (nephrectomy or renorrhaphy) and describe the approach to a grade V renal injury. **[7 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [5 marks] — 1 per valid point up to 5:
• AAST Grade I: contusion or subcapsular haematoma, no laceration [1]
• Grade II: laceration <1 cm into cortex, no extravasation [1]
• Grade III: laceration >1 cm into cortex, no collecting system injury [1]
• Grade IV: laceration through cortex into collecting system with extravasation; or injury to segmental vessels [1]
• Grade V: shattered kidney or avulsion of renal hilum [1]

**(b)** [4 marks]
• High-mechanism injury (fall >3 m, MVA, high-velocity impact) [1]
• Visible haematuria + shock (BP <90 at any time) [1]
• Significant flank trauma with abdominal signs [1]
• CT with contrast: arterial, nephrographic, and delayed (excretory) phases [1]

**(c)** [6 marks]
• Haemodynamically stable (no ongoing transfusion requirement) [1]
• No other intra-abdominal injury requiring laparotomy [1]
• CT shows contained haematoma, no active bleeding [1]
• Absolute bed rest for 24-48 hours [1]
• Serial Hb monitoring [1]
• Prophylactic antibiotics if urinary extravasation [1]

**(d)** [7 marks]
• Indications: haemodynamic instability despite resuscitation, Grade V injury, expanding haematoma, signs of active bleeding [1]
• Renorrhaphy: for Grade IV and select Grade V with salvageable kidney — compress parenchyma, close collecting system, place haemostatic agent [1]
• Nephrectomy: if kidney non-viable/unsalvageable, hilar avulsion, life-threatening bleeding [1]
• Approach: midline laparotomy (control renal pedicle before opening Gerota's fascia) [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This boy has sustained a high-energy right flank injury with visible haematuria. CT with contrast (triple phase) is indicated. Non-operative management is the standard for Grades I-IV without active bleeding, involving bed rest, serial Hb, and antibiotics if extravasation. Grade V injuries (shattered kidney/hilar avulsion) require exploration via midline laparotomy with vascular control before opening Gerota's. Nephrectomy rates are lower in children than adults due to better healing.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Quotes specific AAST criteria (grades I-V) with laceration depths
• Distinguishes paediatric from adult trauma principles
• Describes CT protocol with specific phases
• Discusses prophylactic antibiotics for extravasation

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Urethral Trauma [Total: 20 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 6-year-old boy is struck by a car while cycling. He has pelvic ring fractures (class APC-III), blood at the meatus, and an inability to void. He is haemodynamically stable. A retrograde urethrogram shows complete rupture of the membranous urethra.

**(a)** Classify urethral injuries and describe the mechanism of injury in this case. **[4 marks]**

**(b)** Outline the immediate management of a complete posterior urethral rupture, including the role of suprapubic cystostomy. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Discuss the delayed management of urethral distraction defects, including the timing and technique of urethroplasty. **[6 marks]**

**(d)** Describe the long-term complications and outcomes after posterior urethroplasty in children. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [4 marks] — 1 per valid point:
• Posterior urethral injury — membranous urethra [1]
• Anterior vs posterior: posterior (above urogenital diaphragm) is associated with pelvic fractures [1]
• Mechanism: shearing force from pelvic ring disruption, prostato-membranous junction [1]
• Complete rupture with distraction defect [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Do NOT pass urethral catheter (may convert partial to complete) [1]
• RUG (retrograde urethrogram) to define injury [1]
• Suprapubic cystostomy (SPC) for urinary diversion [1]
• Open or percutaneous SPC [1]
• Definitive repair delayed 3-6 months [1]

**(c)** [6 marks]
• Timing: 3-6 months after injury (allows haematoma resolution and pelvic stability) [1]
• Pre-op: RUG + MCU to define defect length; MRI if complex [1]
• Technique: perineal approach, excision of fibrotic segment [1]
• Bulboprostatic anastomotic urethroplasty (spatulated, tension-free) [1]
• If >3 cm defect: need for urethral mobilisation ± corporal separation or rerouting [1]
• Suprapubic tube for 2-3 weeks; voiding cystourethrogram before removal [1]

**(d)** [5 marks]
• Recurrent stricture (10-20%); managed by dilatation or urethrotomy [1]
• Erectile dysfunction (10-30% depending on injury severity) [1]
• Incontinence (rare with intact bladder neck) [1]
• Ejaculatory dysfunction: retrograde ejaculation from bladder neck disruption? [1]
• Long-term surveillance: uroflowmetry, urethral calibration if symptomatic [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This boy has a posterior urethral distraction defect from pelvic fracture. Immediate management: suprapubic cystostomy only — do not attempt transurethral catheterisation. Definitive repair is delayed 3-6 months, using a perineal bulboprostatic anastomotic urethroplasty with spatulated, tension-free anastomosis. Long-term risks include recurrent stricture (10-20%) and erectile dysfunction (10-30%).

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Gives specific timing for delayed repair (3-6 months)
• Describes perineal approach with anastomotic steps
• Lists long-term complication rates

## QUESTION 3 — SAQ: Bladder Trauma [Total: 8 marks] [FOUNDATION]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** Distinguish between intraperitoneal and extraperitoneal bladder rupture — including mechanism, cystogram findings, and management. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** What is the role of cystography in suspected bladder injury? **[3 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [5 marks]
• Intraperitoneal: dome rupture with distended bladder → urine leaks into peritoneum → requires surgical repair [1-2]
• Extraperitoneal: usually from pelvic fracture, anterolateral wall → urine into perivesical space → catheter drainage alone [1-2]
• CT cystogram: intraperitoneal = contrast outlining bowel; extraperitoneal = flame-shaped contrast tracking into perivesical tissues [1]

**(b)** [3 marks]
• CT cystogram (NOT IVU) — gold standard: fill bladder to capacity via catheter [1]
• Plain film cystogram: if CT not available [1]
• Must distend bladder adequately — underfilling misses up to 40% of injuries [1]

## QUESTION 4 — SAQ: Paediatric Specifics [Total: 8 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** Why is the paediatric kidney more vulnerable to injury than the adult kidney? **[4 marks]**

**(b)** Why are non-operative management protocols more successful in children than adults for high-grade renal injuries? **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [4 marks]
• Kidney is proportionally larger relative to body size [1]
• Less perirenal fat [1]
• Rib cage is more pliable (less protection) [1]
• Abdominal muscles are thinner [1]

**(b)** [4 marks]
• Superior healing capacity of paediatric tissues [1]
• More robust renal blood supply/collaterals [1]
• Higher physiological reserve to tolerate blood loss [1]
• Lower rate of penetrating trauma (most paediatric renal trauma is blunt) [1]

## QUESTION 5 — LCV [Total: 35 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~40 minutes
*(Trauma LCV covering renal injury → urethral injury → long-term follow-up)*

**Q1a.** A 10-year-old boy sustains a grade IV renal laceration with contrast extravasation. Outline non-operative management. [6 marks]
**Q1b.** He re-presents at day 5 with fever and flank pain. What complication has developed and how would you manage it? [5 marks]
**Q2a.** At 6 months, he develops blood at the meatus after passing a small stone. RUG shows a 1.5 cm bulbar urethral stricture. What is the management? [5 marks]
**Q2b.** Describe the anastomotic urethroplasty technique. [6 marks]
**Q3a.** Post-urethroplasty, he develops decreasing stream. What investigations and management would you offer? [5 marks]
**Q3b.** What is the role of urethral dilatation vs DVIU vs redo urethroplasty? [4 marks]
**Q4a.** At 25, he has a weak stream and dribbling. Uroflow Qmax 8 mL/s. What further evaluation is needed? [2 marks]
**Q4b.** Discuss the long-term quality of life in men who have had childhood urethral trauma. [2 marks]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Failing to obtain a retrograde urethrogram before catheter insertion
• Attempting primary realignment of posterior urethral disruption in children
• Under-estimating erectile dysfunction risk after posterior urethroplasty
• Using adult stricture treatment algorithms without considering paediatric anatomy
