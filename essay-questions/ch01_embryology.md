## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Embryology of the Upper Urinary Tract

**Total: 22 marks | BOARD | Time allowed: ~25 minutes**

### CLINICAL SCENARIO

A 3-day-old term male infant is found to have a non-functioning right kidney on postnatal DMSA following antenatal detection of a multicystic dysplastic kidney (MCDK). The contralateral kidney shows compensatory hypertrophy. The parents ask why the kidney failed to develop normally.

### Question Parts

**(a)** Describe the embryological origin of the ureteric bud and metanephric mesenchyme, and explain the process of reciprocal induction that leads to nephrogenesis. **[6 marks]**

**(b)** List the key molecular signals involved in ureteric bud branching and nephrogenesis, naming at least three genes and their roles. **[4 marks]**

**(c)** Explain the embryological basis for the association between ureteric bud anomalies and renal dysplasia, including the histological features characteristic of a dysplastic kidney. **[6 marks]**

**(d)** Outline the embryological events underlying abnormalities of renal ascent and fusion, giving three examples with their approximate timing of origin. **[6 marks]**

---

### EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE

**(a) [6 marks] — Award 1 mark per valid point up to 6:**

- Ureteric bud arises from mesonephric duct at ~28 days gestation [1]
- Metanephros (metanephric mesenchyme) develops in caudal region of intermediate mesoderm [1]
- Ureteric bud penetrates metanephric mesenchyme at ~32 days [1]
- Reciprocal induction: bud induces mesenchyme to form nephrons; mesenchyme induces bud to branch [1]
- Sequential branching gives rise to pelvis, major/minor calyces, collecting ducts [1]
- Glomeruli, convoluted tubules, loop of Henle derived from metanephric mesenchyme [1]
- Nephrogenesis complete by 36 weeks; fixed nephron count thereafter (~1 million/kidney) [1]
- Max 6 marks

**(b) [4 marks]**

- GDNF (glial cell line-derived neurotrophic factor) — promotes ureteric bud outgrowth [1]
- RET proto-oncogene — receptor for GDNF, essential for bud branching [1]
- WT1 (Wilms tumour suppressor gene) — required for metanephric mesenchyme survival [1]
- Other: PAX2, SIX1, SIX2, BMP4, FGF20 — any three with brief role description [1 each, max 4]

**(c) [6 marks]**

- Faulty interaction between ureteric bud and metanephros leads to dysplastic development [1]
- Histological features: disordered renal architecture [1], primitive undifferentiated tubules [1], small cysts [1], metaplastic cartilage and fibromuscular tissue [1]
- Can arise from severe obstructive uropathy in utero (e.g. PUV) causing secondary dysplasia [1]
- Hypoplastic kidney = reduced size but normal architecture vs dysplasia = abnormal architecture [1]

**(d) [6 marks]**

- Renal ascent occurs weeks 6–10 of gestation [1]
- Horseshoe kidney: fusion of lower poles, trapped under IMA origin at L4 [1]
- Pelvic kidney: failure of ascent, accounts for 60% of simple ectopia [1]
- Crossed fused renal ectopia: both kidneys on same side of midline, usually fused [1]
- Associated with VACTERL, Turner syndrome [1]
- Often asymptomatic; present with obstruction or reflux [1]

### MODEL ANSWER

The ureteric bud arises from the mesonephric duct at approximately 28 days of gestation. It penetrates the metanephric mesenchyme at day 32, initiating reciprocal induction: the bud induces the mesenchyme to form nephrons (glomeruli, proximal tubules, loop of Henle), while the mesenchyme induces the bud to branch sequentially, forming the renal pelvis, calyces, and collecting ducts. Nephrogenesis is complete by 36 weeks, after which no new nephrons are formed. Key molecular signals include GDNF (promotes bud outgrowth) acting through its receptor RET, and WT1 (crucial for metanephric survival). Faulty interaction between the ureteric bud and metanephros leads to renal dysplasia, characterised histologically by disordered architecture, primitive tubules, cysts, and metaplastic cartilage. Abnormalities of ascent (weeks 6–10) produce horseshoe kidney, pelvic kidney, or crossed fused ectopia, which are often asymptomatic but may present with obstruction or reflux.

### DISTINGUISHING FEATURES OF AN EXCELLENT ANSWER

- Mentioning the specific timing (28 days for ureteric bud, 32 days for penetration)
- Citing GDNF/RET/WT1 as key molecular signals by name
- Distinguishing dysplasia (abnormal architecture) from hypoplasia (small but architecturally normal)
- Relating clinical anomalies to specific weeks of gestation

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Cloacal Division and Genital Tract Development

**Total: 20 marks | BOARD | Time allowed: ~25 minutes**

### CLINICAL SCENARIO

A newborn female is noted to have a single perineal orifice on examination. Prenatal ultrasound had shown bilateral hydronephrosis and a pelvic cystic structure. The diagnosis of persistent cloaca is suspected.

### Question Parts

**(a)** Describe the normal process of cloacal subdivision, including the timing and the structures that result from anterior and posterior compartments. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Explain the role of the SRY gene, anti-Müllerian hormone (AMH), and testosterone in male sexual differentiation, including the timing and cellular sources of each. **[6 marks]**

**(c)** Outline the Meyer-Weigert law and its embryological basis in complete ureteric duplication. **[4 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the embryological origins of bladder exstrophy, comparing the two main proposed mechanisms. **[5 marks]**

---

### EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE

**(a) [5 marks]**

- Cloaca is single cavity comprising primitive hindgut and allantois (4–7 weeks) [1]
- Traditionally ascribed to descent of urorectal septum (Tourneux fold) [1]
- Current understanding: predominantly passive process from spatial realignment and differential dorsoventral growth [1]
- Anterior compartment → urogenital sinus [1]
- Posterior compartment → anorectal canal [1]

**(b) [6 marks]**

- SRY gene on Y chromosome encodes testis-determining factor [1]
- Upregulates SOX9 and other transcription factors → testis differentiation from 6–7 weeks [1]
- AMH (MIS) secreted by Sertoli cells from 7th week → causes regression of paramesonephric ducts [1]
- Testosterone secreted by Leydig cells from 9th week → stabilises mesonephric ducts [1]
- Mesonephric ducts → epididymis, vas deferens, seminal vesicles, ejaculatory ducts [1]
- DHT (via 5α-reductase) virilises external genitalia [1]

**(c) [4 marks]**

- In complete duplication, upper pole ureter drains more caudally (inferomedially) than lower pole ureter [1]
- Embryological basis: upper pole ureteric bud arises more cephalad on mesonephric duct [1]
- As mesonephric duct migrates caudally, it carries upper pole ureter to more distal position [1]
- Lower pole ureter remains anchored on trigone → shorter submucosal tunnel → predisposes to reflux [1]

**(d) [5 marks]**

- Cloacal rupture hypothesis: failure of mesodermal/endodermal/ectodermal integration causes cloacal membrane rupture → exposure of bladder plate [1]
- Wedge-effect hypothesis: overgrowth of cloacal membrane acts as mechanical wedge separating mesodermal components of body wall [1]
- Neither model fully explains "late rupture" or "covered" variants [1]
- Results in open bladder plate, pubic diastasis, epispadiac urethra [1]
- Incidence ~1:50,000; M:F 2:1 [1]

## EMQ 1 — Theme: Embryological Origins of Congenital Anomalies

### OPTIONS

| Letter | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| A | Failure of ureteric bud–metanephros interaction |
| B | Abnormal division of the cloaca (4–7 weeks) |
| C | Failure of testicular descent (AMH/testosterone dependent) |
| D | Incomplete closure of the urethral groove (<15 weeks) |
| E | Abnormal development of the paramesonephric ducts |
| F | Failure of the mesonephric duct to migrate caudally |
| G | Abnormal germ cell migration from the yolk sac |
| H | Premature closure of the neural tube |
| I | Aberrant ureteric bud position on the mesonephric duct |
| J | Failure of the processus vaginalis to close |

### Stems

For each clinical scenario, select the single most appropriate option from the list above:

1. A 3-year-old girl is found to have a bicornuate uterus during investigation of a urinary tract infection. What is the embryological origin of this anomaly?

2. A newborn male has bilateral hydronephrosis, a distended bladder, and oligohydramnios on prenatal ultrasound. Postnatally, MCUG confirms posterior urethral valves. What is the embryological origin of this condition?

3. A 6-month-old girl presents with a right-sided inguinal hernia. At operation, the hernial sac contains a fallopian tube and ovary. What embryological structure has failed to close?

4. A 2-year-old boy with hypospadias is found to have a persistent Müllerian utricle on cystoscopy. What embryological process has failed to complete normally?

5. A DMSA scan in a 4-year-old boy demonstrates a horseshoe kidney. Which embryological event gave rise to this finding?

### ANSWERS & RATIONALE

1. **E** — Bicornuate uterus results from incomplete fusion of the paramesonephric (Müllerian) ducts. This is the most common Müllerian anomaly.

2. **B** — PUV are thought to originate from abnormal interaction between the mesonephric ducts and urogenital sinus around the 7th week of gestation, during cloacal subdivision.

3. **J** — Failure of the processus vaginalis to close after testicular descent can lead to a patent processus and hence an indirect inguinal hernia containing abdominal contents. In females, the round ligament takes a similar path.

4. **D** — The utriculus is a remnant of the paramesonephric duct that normally regresses under the influence of AMH. Its persistence suggests incomplete AMH action or timing mismatch with urethral closure.

5. **A** — Horseshoe kidney results from fusion of the two metanephric masses during ascent (weeks 6–10). The isthmus is trapped under the IMA origin.
