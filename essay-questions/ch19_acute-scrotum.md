## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Acute Scrotum — Differential Diagnosis [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 13-year-old boy presents to the emergency department with a 4-hour history of sudden onset right scrotal pain. He has vomited once. The pain woke him from sleep. On examination, the right testis is swollen, tender, and high-riding. The left testis is normal. The right cremasteric reflex is absent. He is afebrile.

**(a)** List the differential diagnoses of the acute scrotum in a boy of this age and state the most likely in this case with justification. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Explain the anatomy of the bell-clapper deformity and its role in testicular torsion. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Describe the urgent management of testicular torsion, including the surgical technique of scrotal exploration and orchidopexy. **[6 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the factors that determine testicular salvage rates in torsion and the management of a non-viable testis found at exploration. **[6 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [5 marks] — 1 per valid point up to 5:
• Testicular torsion (most likely) — sudden onset, vomiting, absent cremasteric reflex, high-riding testis [1]
• Torsion of appendix testis — gradual onset, older boys, blue dot sign, less systemic upset [1]
• Epididymo-orchitis — gradual onset, dysuria, urethral discharge, fever, PRESERVED cremasteric reflex [1]
• Incarcerated hernia — inguinal swelling, not transilluminable, bilious vomiting [1]
• Idiopathic scrotal oedema — bilateral, diffuse oedema extending to perineum, non-tender [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Bell-clapper deformity: tunica vaginalis inserts too high on the spermatic cord, leaving testis free to rotate within the tunica [1]
• Normally, tunica inserts on lower third of testis — anchors it posteriorly [1]
• In bell-clapper, the testis hangs freely like a bell clapper [1]
• Bilateral in 80% of cases [1]
• Intravaginal torsion: testis twists within the tunica, occluding vessels → ischaemia [1]

**(c)** [6 marks]
• Immediate exploration: 2-hour window is critical — do not delay for imaging [1]
• Midline scrotal raphe incision [1]
• Deliver testis; untwist (medial rotation for left, lateral for right — "open book" rule) [1]
• Soak in warm saline for 10-15 minutes [1]
• If viable: orchidopexy with 3-point fixation (non-absorbable suture) to dartos and septum [1]
• Contralateral orchidopexy mandatory (bell-clapper is bilateral) [1]

**(d)** [6 marks]
• <6 hours: salvage ~90-100% [1]
• 6-12 hours: salvage ~50-70% [1]
• 12-24 hours: salvage ~10-20% [1]
• >24 hours: salvage <5% [1]
• If non-viable: orchiectomy [1]
• Contralateral orchidopexy still mandatory [1]
• Testicular prosthesis discussion for later placement [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This boy has testicular torsion until proven otherwise — the sudden onset, vomiting, absent cremasteric reflex, and high-lying testis are classic. The bell-clapper deformity, present bilaterally in 80%, predisposes to torsion. Immediate scrotal exploration is indicated within the 2-hour salvage window. At exploration, I would untwist the testis, assess viability, and fix bilaterally with non-absorbable sutures. Salvage rate at 4 hours is high (>90%).

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Gives specific salvage rates by time intervals
• Describes "open book" rule for untwisting direction
• Mandates contralateral orchidopexy
• Includes testicular prosthesis discussion

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Torsion of Appendix Testis [Total: 20 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
An 8-year-old boy presents with 24 hours of mild left scrotal pain. He is not vomiting. On examination, there is mild tenderness at the upper pole of the left testis. A small, tender, pea-sized nodule is palpable. The cremasteric reflex is present. The scrotum shows mild erythema but no swelling.

**(a)** What is the most likely diagnosis and why? **[4 marks]**

**(b)** Describe the clinical features that distinguish torsion of the appendix testis from testicular torsion. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Outline the management of torsion of the appendix testis, including the indications for conservative vs surgical treatment. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** A 12-year-old boy presents with a 6-hour history of acute scrotal pain. Ultrasound shows no flow in the right testis. Discuss the role of Doppler ultrasound in the acute scrotum and how it should be used in decision-making. **[6 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [4 marks] — 1 per valid point up to 4:
• Torsion of appendix testis [1]
• Age (8 years old — peak for appendix torsion) [1]
• Gradual onset, mild pain, no vomiting [1]
• Blue dot sign (infarcted appendix visible through scrotal skin at upper pole) [1]
• Palpable tender nodule at upper pole [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Appendix torsion: gradual onset; milder pain; blue dot sign; preserved cremasteric reflex; normal testicular lie [1]
• Testicular torsion: sudden onset; severe pain with vomiting; absent cremasteric reflex; high-riding testis; horizontal lie [1]
• Appendix torsion: less systemic upset; can often walk comfortably [1]
• Age: appendix torsion peaks at 7-12; testicular torsion peaks at 12-18 and neonates [1]
• Both may have scrotal erythema and reactive hydrocoele [1]

**(c)** [5 marks]
• Conservative: rest, scrotal support, NSAIDs — most resolve in 5-10 days [1]
• Surgical excision: if severe pain, diagnostic uncertainty, or parental preference [1]
• If surgery: excise the necrotic appendix through a small scrotal incision [1]
• Contralateral exploration not needed [1]
• Prognosis: excellent — no impact on fertility [1]

**(d)** [6 marks]
• Doppler US: demonstrates testicular blood flow [1]
• Sensitive (95-100%) but operator-dependent and time-consuming [1]
• False positives: flow may be seen in early torsion or partial torsion [1]
• Role: confirm suspicion but must NOT delay surgery [1]
• Clinical judgment overrides US [1]
• If US shows no flow → emergency exploration [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This boy has torsion of the appendix testis — common in this age group, gradual onset, blue dot sign, and preserved cremasteric reflex. I would manage conservatively with NSAIDs and scrotal support, with rapid resolution expected. If the diagnosis were uncertain, Doppler US could help, but clinical examination remains the gold standard. When in doubt, explore — the consequences of missing testicular torsion are catastrophic.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Lists specific age peaks for both conditions
• Discusses the limitations of Doppler US (operator dependence, false negatives)
• Stresses clinical judgment as primary decision tool
• Recommends conservative management confidently

## QUESTION 3 — SAQ: Neonatal Torsion [Total: 8 marks] [FOUNDATION]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 2-day-old term neonate is found to have a firm, painless, discoloured right scrotal mass. The right testis is enlarged and dark. There is no tenderness.

**(a)** What is the most likely diagnosis and what is the mechanism in neonates? **[3 marks]**

**(b)** What is the management of neonatal testicular torsion, and what is the controversy regarding contralateral fixation? **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [3 marks]
• Extravaginal testicular torsion [1]
• Torsion occurs outside the tunica vaginalis (cord twists above the tunica) [1]
• Occurs in utero or perinatal — testis is already non-viable at birth [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Exploration: orchiectomy of non-viable testis + contralateral fixation [1]
• Controversy: some advocate for observation (testis is already lost; anaesthetic risk in neonate) [1]
• Others advocate for contralateral orchidopexy to protect the remaining testis [1]
• Risk of torsion in contralateral testis is low but catastrophic if occurs [1]
• Current practice: contralateral orchidopexy is recommended by most paediatric urologists [1]

## QUESTION 4 — SAQ: Intermittent Torsion [Total: 8 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** Describe the clinical features of intermittent testicular torsion and explain its significance. **[4 marks]**

**(b)** What is the management and why is elective orchidopexy indicated? **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [4 marks]
• Recurrent episodes of sudden, severe scrotal pain that spontaneously resolves [1]
• Episodes last minutes to hours [1]
• Often associated with nausea/vomiting [1]
• On examination between episodes: testis may be horizontally lying (bell-clapper) [1]

**(b)** [4 marks]
• Elective bilateral scrotal exploration and orchidopexy [1]
• Indicated because intermittent torsion carries risk of fulminant torsion [1]
• Almost all have bell-clapper deformity at surgery [1]
• Fixation prevents future torsion [1]

## QUESTION 5 — LCV: Missed Torsion [Total: 35 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~40 minutes

─── STAGE 1: PRESENTATION ───────────────────────────────
A 16-year-old boy presents to A&E with 3 days of right scrotal pain. He initially had severe pain that lasted 12 hours and then settled to a dull ache. The testis is swollen, firm, and non-tender. He has a low-grade fever. The right scrotum is erythematous.

**Q1a.** What is the most likely diagnosis? **[2 marks]**
**Q1b.** Discuss the pathological sequence of events — why did the pain subside? **[3 marks]**

─── STAGE 2: INVESTIGATION ──────────────────────────────
Doppler US shows no flow in the right testis with heterogeneous echotexture. The left testis has normal flow.

**Q2a.** Interpret the US findings. What is the clinical significance of "heterogeneous echotexture"? **[3 marks]**
**Q2b.** What is the management of a missed torsion? **[5 marks]**

─── STAGE 3: SURGERY ────────────────────────────────────
At exploration, the testis is necrotic. You perform a right orchiectomy and left orchidopexy.

**Q3a.** Describe the steps of orchiectomy and how you would discuss the option of a testicular prosthesis. **[6 marks]**
**Q3b.** The family asks if the right testis could have been saved if they came earlier. How do you respond? **[3 marks]**

─── STAGE 4: FOLLOW-UP ──────────────────────────────────
At 6 months, he is well. Left testis volume is 18 mL. He is concerned about fertility and body image.

**Q4a.** What is the expected effect of unilateral orchiectomy on fertility? **[4 marks]**
**Q4b.** What is the recommended follow-up for the remaining testis? **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**FULL MARKING GUIDE:**

**Q1a.** [2 marks]
• Missed testicular torsion (necrotising testis) [2]

**Q1b.** [3 marks]
• Torsion → venous occlusion → ischaemia → pain (first 12 hours) [1]
• Complete arterial occlusion → testicular infarction [1]
• Necrotic testis loses innervation → pain resolves (false reassurance) [1]

**Q2a.** [3 marks]
• No flow = absent perfusion [1]
• Heterogeneous echotexture = infarction, haemorrhage, necrosis [1]
• Irreversible damage — testis is non-viable [1]

**Q2b.** [5 marks]
• Scrotal exploration [1]
• Orchiectomy of necrotic testis [1]
• Contralateral orchidopexy (mandatory) [1]
• Broad-spectrum antibiotics (necrotic testis may be infected) [1]
• Testicular prosthesis discussion: offer for later placement [1]

**Q3a.** [6 marks]
• Scrotal incision, deliver testis [1]
• Confirm non-viability (dark, no bleeding from capsulotomy) [1]
• Ligate and divide cord (suture ligation of vessels) [1]
• Contralateral orchidopexy (dartos pouch, 3-point non-absorbable) [1]
• Prosthesis: discuss silicone prosthesis; can be placed simultaneously or delayed [1]
• Advantages: cosmetic, psychological; disadvantages: infection, migration [1]

**Q3b.** [3 marks]
• Honest but compassionate: the testis was likely not salvageable after 12 hours [1]
• Salvage rates drop to 50% at 6-12 hours and near zero after 24 hours [1]
• Outcome would have been different with earlier presentation [1]

**Q4a.** [4 marks]
• One normal testis sufficient for normal fertility in most men [1]
• Some studies show slightly reduced semen quality after unilateral orchiectomy [1]
• Compensatory hypertrophy of remaining testis (expected 20-30% increase) [1]
• FSH may rise, inhibin B may fall — but fertility usually preserved [1]

**Q4b.** [4 marks]
• Self-examination monthly [1]
• Annual physical examination [1]
• Scrotal US only if symptoms develop [1]
• Semen analysis if fertility becomes a concern [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Explains why pain subsides with necrosis — an important clinical concept
• Describes US findings and their prognostic significance
• Gives compassionate counselling about missed torsion
• Discusses fertility outcomes after orchiectomy

## QUESTION 6 — EMQ: Acute Scrotum — Diagnosis [Total: 10 marks] [BOARD]

**Theme: Acute Scrotum in Children**

**OPTIONS (each option may be used once, more than once, or not at all):**

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| A. Testicular torsion | F. Incarcerated hernia |
| B. Torsion of appendix testis | G. Idiopathic scrotal oedema |
| C. Epididymo-orchitis | H. Testicular tumour |
| D. Hydrocoele | I. Scrotal trauma |
| E. Varicocoele | J. Immune (Henoch-Schönlein) vasculitis |

**For each of the following clinical scenarios, select the single most appropriate diagnosis:**

**1.** A 14-year-old with sudden onset left scrotal pain during football practice. Vomited on the field. Left testis is high-riding, transverse lie, and the cremasteric reflex is absent. **[1 mark]**

**2.** A 10-year-old with 36 hours of mild scrotal discomfort. A blue dot is visible at the upper pole. The testis is in normal position. **[1 mark]**

**3.** A 6-year-old with bilateral scrotal swellings and a palpable purpuric rash on the lower limbs and buttocks. **[1 mark]**

**4.** A 2-year-old with sudden onset scrotal swelling after a nap. The swelling is irreducible and does not transilluminate. The child has bilious vomiting. **[1 mark]**

**5.** A 5-year-old with sudden onset bilateral scrotal swelling with erythema extending to the perineum. The child is well, and the testicles are normal on examination. **[1 mark]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**ANSWERS & RATIONALE:**

**1.** **A** — Testicular torsion. The classic presentation: sudden onset during physical activity, vomiting, high-riding testis with transverse lie, absent cremasteric reflex, and nausea/vomiting. Emergency exploration is indicated.

**2.** **B** — Torsion of appendix testis. The blue dot sign is pathognomonic for torsion of the appendix testis. Gradual onset, mild symptoms, and normal testicular lie confirm the diagnosis. Conservative management is appropriate.

**3.** **J** — Immune (Henoch-Schönlein) vasculitis. Scrotal involvement occurs in ~10-15% of HSP. The palpable purpuric rash on the legs and buttocks with bilateral scrotal swelling is diagnostic. Management is supportive; resolves with the condition.

**4.** **F** — Incarcerated hernia. Sudden scrotal swelling in a 2-year-old with bilious vomiting indicates a strangulated hernia. The irreducibility and lack of transillumination (bowel, not fluid) are key. Emergency reduction ± repair is needed.

**5.** **G** — Idiopathic scrotal oedema. Bilateral scrotal oedema extending to the perineum in a well child with normal testes is characteristic. It is self-limiting; treatment is supportive. The diagnosis is clinical.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Missing HSP as a cause of acute scrotum in a child with purpura
• Exploring for idiopathic scrotal oedema (no surgical indication)
• Failing to recognise strangulated hernia as requiring emergency intervention
• Assuming all acute scrotums are torsion — consider other causes when features differ

• Time-dependent salvage rates are the most frequently tested numerical data in paediatric urology — candidates MUST know the 6/12/24 hour thresholds
• The blue dot sign is pathognomonic for appendix testis torsion but is present in only 50% of cases
• Missed torsion is a common exam scenario — candidates must discuss orchiectomy and contralateral fixation
• Testicular prosthesis is frequently forgotten in counselling
