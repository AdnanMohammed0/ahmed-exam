## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Wilms Tumour — Diagnosis and Staging [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 3-year-old boy presents with a 3-week history of progressive abdominal distension. His mother noticed a firm mass on the right side of his abdomen while bathing him. He is otherwise well with no haematuria, fever, or weight loss. Blood pressure is 110/70. US reveals a 9 cm solid right renal mass with extension into the renal vein.

**(a)** List the clinical features that distinguish a Wilms tumour from a neuroblastoma in a child of this age. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Compare the SIOP and COG staging systems for Wilms tumour and state which approach is used in your practice. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Describe the pre-operative workup and the steps of a radical nephrectomy for Wilms tumour. **[7 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the management of tumour thrombus extending into the IVC. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [5 marks] — 1 per valid point up to 5:
• Wilms: smooth, non-tender mass crossing midline rarely [1]
• Neuroblastoma: firm, irregular, crosses midline, may have systemic symptoms [1]
• Wilms: typically normotensive (can have hypertension); neuroblastoma: catecholamine excess (sweating, flushing, diarrhoea) [1]
• Wilms: no bone pain (neuroblastoma may present with limping) [1]
• Wilms: rarely has opsoclonus-myoclonus (associated with neuroblastoma) [1]
• Imaging: Wilms — intrarenal mass with claw sign; neuroblastoma — extrarenal, displaces kidney, calcification common [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• SIOP (European): pre-operative chemotherapy (vincristine + actinomycin D for 4 weeks) → delayed nephrectomy → histology-based staging [1]
   • Advantages: tumour shrinkage, lower rupture risk, less advanced stage at surgery [1]
• COG (North American): up-front nephrectomy → chemotherapy based on surgical-histological staging [1]
   • Advantages: native tumour tissue for diagnosis, no risk of treating benign lesion [1]
• My practice: SIOP (pre-operative chemotherapy) — standard in Europe, less surgical morbidity [1]

**(c)** [7 marks]
• Pre-op: US with Doppler (tumour extent, IVC thrombus), CT chest/abdomen, FBC, UAE, LDH, urine catecholamines [1]
• Position: supine, roll under flank, transverse abdominal incision (supracostal) [1]
• Enter peritoneal cavity through transverse incision from 9th rib to rectus [1]
• Mobilise colon (right: mobilise ascending colon medially; left: descend colon) [1]
• Identify ureter — ligate and divide [1]
• Identify renal vessels — ligate artery first, then vein (minimises tumour congestion) [1]
• Mobilise kidney — include perirenal fat and Gerota's fascia [1]
• Check for lymph node sampling (hilar, para-aortic, paracaval) [1]
• IVC extension: if thrombus present, obtain proximal and distal control of IVC, cavotomy, thrombectomy [1]

**(d)** [5 marks]
• Assess extent: CT/MRI with vascular protocol + transthoracic echocardiogram [1]
• Level I (infrahepatic): clamp IVC below hepatic veins, open, extract thrombus [1]
• Level II (intrahepatic or pre-atrial): may need cardiopulmonary bypass [1]
• Level III (atrial): cardiac bypass with hypothermic arrest [1]
• SIOP approach: pre-operative chemotherapy may shrink thrombus by 50-70% [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This boy has a right renal mass consistent with Wilms tumour. Under the SIOP protocol, he would receive 4 weeks of vincristine + actinomycin D before delayed nephrectomy. The nephrectomy involves a transverse abdominal incision, early vascular control (artery before vein), en bloc removal of kidney with Gerota's fascia, and lymph node sampling. Given the suspected renal vein extension, pre-operative chemotherapy will likely reduce the thrombus. If thrombus persists, IVC exploration with cross-clamping is required.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Compares SIOP vs COG systematically with rationales
• Describes specific transverse incision approach
• Distinguishes IVC thrombus levels and respective surgical approaches
• Includes pre-operative catecholamines to exclude neuroblastoma

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Bilateral Wilms Tumour [Total: 20 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 2-year-old girl presents with bilateral renal masses. CT shows a 6 cm mass in the right kidney and a 4 cm mass in the left kidney. Both masses appear confined to the kidney. No metastases are identified. She is otherwise well.

**(a)** What is the incidence of bilateral Wilms tumour and its association with predisposing syndromes? **[4 marks]**

**(b)** Outline the management of bilateral Wilms tumour, including the role of neoadjuvant chemotherapy and the principles of nephron-sparing surgery. **[6 marks]**

**(c)** Describe the technique of partial nephrectomy (nephron-sparing surgery) for a polar Wilms tumour. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the long-term follow-up for bilateral Wilms tumour survivors, including chemotherapy-related morbidity and renal function monitoring. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [4 marks] — 1 per valid point up to 4:
• Bilateral Wilms: ~5-8% of all Wilms tumours [1]
• Associated syndromes: WAGR (Wilms, aniridia, GU anomalies, retardation) — WT1 deletion [1]
• Denys-Drash syndrome (WT1 mutation) — Wilms + renal failure + male pseudohermaphroditism [1]
• Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome — WT2 mutation; also associated with hemihypertrophy [1]

**(b)** [6 marks]
• Neoadjuvant chemotherapy first — 4-6 weeks vincristine + actinomycin D ± doxorubicin [1]
• Goal: tumour shrinkage to allow nephron-sparing surgery [1]
• SIOP protocol: response assessment after 4-6 weeks [1]
• Surgical approach: bilateral nephron-sparing surgery or unilateral nephrectomy + contralateral NSS [1]
• Complete tumour excision with negative margins while preserving maximal renal parenchyma [1]
• If NSS not possible → total nephrectomy one side + partial opposite side; may need dialysis [1]

**(c)** [5 marks]
• Identify and isolate tumour with healthy margin of renal parenchyma [1]
• Clamp renal artery and vein; surface cooling with ice slush [1]
• Excise tumour with 5-10 mm normal parenchyma margin [1]
• Oversew collecting system with 5-0 absorbable sutures [1]
• Close capsular defect with haemostatic agents ± pedicled omental flap [1]

**(d)** [5 marks]
• Chemotherapy: doxorubicin → cardiotoxicity (echo surveillance) [1]
• Renal function: eGFR annually + BP + urinalysis for proteinuria [1]
• If eGFR falls to <30 → nephrology referral [1]
• Second malignancy risk: increased due to chemotherapy + radiation (if given) [1]
• Growth and development: endocrine surveillance if abdominal radiation [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
Bilateral Wilms tumour (~5-8%) is managed with neoadjuvant chemotherapy to shrink tumours and permit nephron-sparing surgery. After 4-6 weeks of chemotherapy, the goal is bilateral partial nephrectomy with negative margins. The technique requires vascular clamping, surface cooling, excision with a 5-10 mm margin, and watertight closure of the collecting system. Long-term follow-up includes cardiac monitoring (if doxorubicin given), eGFR, BP monitoring, and surveillance for second malignancies.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Cites specific syndrome associations with WT1/WT2 mutations
• Describes nephron-sparing surgical technique with surface cooling
• Includes doxorubicin cardiotoxicity monitoring
• Discusses second malignancy risk

## QUESTION 3 — SAQ: GU Rhabdomyosarcoma [Total: 8 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 4-year-old boy presents with a painless scrotal mass that does not transilluminate. US shows a solid intra-scrotal mass replacing the testis.

**(a)** What is the differential diagnosis and what tumour markers would you check? **[4 marks]**

**(b)** Describe the appropriate management of a suspicious testicular mass in a child. **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [4 marks]
• Differential: yolk sac tumour (most common in this age), teratoma, rhabdomyosarcoma (paratesticular) [1]
• Tumour markers: α-fetoprotein (AFP) — elevated in yolk sac tumour; β-hCG — elevated in choriocarcinoma [1]
• LDH (non-specific but prognostic) [1]
• Note: AFP is physiologically elevated in infants <6 months — must use age-adjusted reference range [1]

**(b)** [4 marks]
• Radical inguinal orchidectomy (AOR) — high inguinal incision, ligate cord at internal ring [1]
• Do NOT perform scrotal incision (may cause scrotal contamination and lymphatic disruption) [1]
• Send specimen for histology with markers [1]
• CT chest/abdomen/pelvis for staging [1]

## QUESTION 4 — SAQ: Wilms Surveillance [Total: 8 marks] [FOUNDATION]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** Outline the recommended follow-up schedule for a child treated for unilateral Wilms tumour (stage I, favourable histology). **[4 marks]**

**(b)** List three late effects of Wilms tumour treatment. **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [4 marks]
• Abdominal US every 3 months for 2 years, then every 6 months for 2 years, then annually [1]
• Chest X-ray every 3 months for 2 years [1]
• BP and urinalysis at each visit [1]
• Long-term: eGFR, cardiac echo (if doxorubicin given) [1]

**(b)** [4 marks — 1 each]
• Reduced renal function (chronic kidney disease) [1]
• Cardiac toxicity (from doxorubicin) [1]
• Second malignancy (especially if radiation given) [1]
• (Also: scoliosis/pulmonary fibrosis if radiation, growth failure, infertility)

## QUESTION 5 — LCV: Staged Management of Wilms [Total: 35 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~40 minutes

─── STAGE 1: PRESENTATION ───────────────────────────────
A 4-year-old girl with a right-sided abdominal mass. Haemoglobin 90 g/L. CT shows a 10 cm right renal mass with para-aortic lymphadenopathy. Left kidney is normal. No lung metastases.

**Q1a.** What staging investigations are needed? **[3 marks]**
**Q1b.** Under SIOP protocol, what is the treatment plan before surgery? **[3 marks]**

─── STAGE 2: CHEMOTHERAPY RESPONSE ──────────────────────
After 4 weeks of chemotherapy, the tumour has shrunk by 50%. Repeat CT shows a residual 5 cm mass. No metastasis.

**Q2a.** What is the next step? **[2 marks]**
**Q2b.** Describe the radical nephrectomy with lymph node dissection. **[6 marks]**

─── STAGE 3: HISTOLOGY ──────────────────────────────────
Histology shows mixed type Wilms tumour (favourable histology). Lymph nodes are negative. Margins are clear. Staging: stage II (invasion beyond renal capsule but completely excised).

**Q3a.** What post-operative chemotherapy is indicated? **[3 marks]**
**Q3b.** What are the survival outcomes for this stage? **[3 marks]**

─── STAGE 4: LONG-TERM SURVIVOR ─────────────────────────
At 15-year follow-up, she has eGFR 65 mL/min/1.73 m², hypertension requiring monotherapy, and reduced left ventricular function.

**Q4a.** What is the cumulative burden of her Wilms treatment? **[5 marks]**
**Q4b.** Outline a long-term health surveillance plan for adult survivors of Wilms tumour. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**FULL MARKING GUIDE:**

**Q1a.** [3 marks]
• CT chest (lung metastases) [1]
• CT abdomen (vascular involvement, contralateral kidney, lymph nodes) [1]
• FBC (anaemia), UAE, LDH, urine catecholamines [1]

**Q1b.** [3 marks]
• Vincristine + actinomycin D IV weekly for 4 weeks [1]
• Assess response with US/CT after 4 weeks [1]
• Plan delayed nephrectomy [1]

**Q2a.** [2 marks]
• Right radical nephrectomy with lymph node sampling [2]

**Q2b.** [6 marks]
• Transverse abdominal incision [1]
• Mobilise ascending colon medially [1]
• Identify right ureter — ligate and divide [1]
• Identify and ligate right renal artery (between aorta and IVC) [1]
• Ligate right renal vein [1]
• En bloc removal of kidney with Gerota's fascia [1]
• Sample inter-aortocaval and hilar lymph nodes [1]

**Q3a.** [3 marks]
• Vincristine + actinomycin D for 27 weeks (18 weeks if stage I) [1-2]
• No doxorubicin (reserved for stage III-IV or high-risk histology) [1]

**Q3b.** [3 marks]
• Stage II favourable histology: 4-year overall survival ~95% [2]
• Event-free survival: ~85-90% [1]

**Q4a.** [5 marks]
• Nephrectomy → single kidney → eGFR 65 (CKD stage 2) [1]
• Long-term GFR decline risk: 30-40% have CKD by adulthood [1]
• Hypertension: from reduced nephron mass [1]
• Chemotherapy (vincristine): peripheral neuropathy, jaw pain, constipation [1]
• No doxorubicin → no cardiac toxicity in this case (but reduced LV function from other causes?) [1]

**Q4b.** [5 marks]
• Annual BP monitoring [1]
• Annual eGFR and urinalysis [1]
• Cardiac echo every 2-5 years (if doxorubicin given) [1]
• Second malignancy surveillance: annual skin check, health maintenance [1]
• Fertility counselling if she received alkylating agents (she did not, in this case) [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Describes SIOP protocol in detail with chemotherapy specifics (drugs, duration)
• Provides survival statistics by stage
• Includes late effects monitoring as a critical component of survivorship care
• Distinguishes chemotherapy agents and their specific side effects

## QUESTION 6 — EMQ: GU Malignancies [Total: 10 marks] [BOARD]

**Theme: Paediatric Genitourinary Cancers**

**OPTIONS (each option may be used once, more than once, or not at all):**

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| A. Wilms tumour (favourable) | F. Paratesticular rhabdomyosarcoma |
| B. Wilms tumour (anaplastic) | G. Embryonal rhabdomyosarcoma (prostate/bladder) |
| C. Clear cell sarcoma of kidney | H. Yolk sac tumour (testicular) |
| D. Rhabdoid tumour of kidney | I. Teratoma (testicular) |
| E. Neuroblastoma | J. Juvenile granulosa cell tumour |

**For each of the following clinical scenarios, select the single most appropriate diagnosis:**

**1.** A 4-year-old with a right renal mass. Histology shows triphasic morphology (blastema, stroma, epithelium) with no anaplasia. **[1 mark]**

**2.** A 6-month-old with a rapidly growing renal mass, metastatic to the lungs at presentation. Histology shows large cells with rhabdoid morphology. SMARCB1/INI1 deletion present. **[1 mark]**

**3.** A 16-month-old with a testicular mass. AFP elevated at 800 ng/mL (normal age-adjusted), β-hCG normal. **[1 mark]**

**4.** A 3-year-old with a scrotal mass separate from the testis, firm, non-tender. US shows an extra-testicular mass. Histology shows small round blue cells with alveolar pattern. **[1 mark]**

**5.** A 5-year-old with bladder mass and haematuria. US shows a polypoid mass at the bladder base. Biopsy shows embryonal rhabdomyosarcoma. **[1 mark]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**ANSWERS & RATIONALE:**

**1.** **A** — Wilms tumour (favourable histology). The triphasic morphology (blastema, stroma, epithelium) is diagnostic of Wilms tumour. No anaplasia indicates favourable histology, which has an excellent prognosis (~90% survival).

**2.** **D** — Rhabdoid tumour of the kidney. Presents earlier (infants), aggressive, metastatic at presentation. The hallmark is the SMARCB1/INI1 deletion. Rhabdoid cells with abundant eosinophilic cytoplasm and large nucleoli. Very poor prognosis.

**3.** **H** — Yolk sac tumour (testicular). The elevated AFP in a toddler with a testicular mass is diagnostic. Yolk sac tumour is the most common testicular tumour in children <3 years. Treatment: radical inguinal orchidectomy ± chemotherapy.

**4.** **F** — Paratesticular rhabdomyosarcoma. Arises from the spermatic cord/cord structures, NOT from the testis (hence "separate from testis"). The alveolar pattern is a high-risk variant. Treatment: orchidectomy + chemotherapy ± radiation.

**5.** **G** — Embryonal rhabdomyosarcoma (bladder/prostate). Arises from the bladder base or prostate in young children. Presents with haematuria or bladder outlet obstruction. Treatment: chemotherapy + local control (surgery or radiation).

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Confusing clear cell sarcoma with favourable Wilms — both present similarly but have different outcomes
• Not distinguishing between testicular (germ cell) and paratesticular (rhabdomyosarcoma) masses — management differs
• Forgetting that AFP is age-adjusted in infants <6 months
• Rhabdoid tumour is often missed because it's rare but has a characteristic presentation

• The SIOP vs COG controversy is the most tested topic in paediatric GU oncology — candidates must be able to defend their preferred approach
• Rhabdoid tumour of the kidney is a strong discriminator because it is rare and has a specific genetic mutation
• Nephron-sparing surgery for Wilms is increasingly important — candidates should know the technique
• Late effects are now a core part of paediatric oncology training — survivorship care plans are expected knowledge
