## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Ovarian Torsion [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 12-year-old girl presents with acute-onset left lower abdominal pain and vomiting. Pain is colicky and radiates to the left flank. She is afebrile. Abdomen is soft but tender in the left iliac fossa with guarding. Ultrasound shows an enlarged left ovary (8 × 6 × 5 cm) with absent arterial and venous Doppler signals.

**(a)** List the differential diagnoses and state the most likely. **[4 marks]**

**(b)** Discuss the role of Doppler ultrasound and its limitations in diagnosing ovarian torsion. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Describe the surgical management of ovarian torsion, including the importance of ovarian preservation. **[7 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the long-term implications of ovarian torsion and oophorectomy on future fertility. **[6 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [4 marks] — 1 per valid point up to 4:
• Ovarian torsion (most likely) — acute onset, vomiting, adnexal mass on US [1]
• Appendicitis — RLQ > LLQ, fever, migration, anorexia [1]
• Renal colic — haematuria, radiation [1]
• PID — vaginal discharge, fever, sexually active [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Doppler US: absence of arterial/venous flow suggests torsion [1]
• Limitations: Doppler may be present in partial torsion (false negative ~30-50%) [1]
• Dual blood supply (ovarian and uterine arteries) → incomplete occlusion [1]
• Enlarged ovary (>5 cm) is the most sensitive sign [1]
• Ovarian volume asymmetry >20 mL is suspicious [1]

**(c)** [7 marks]
• Emergency diagnostic laparoscopy [1]
• Deliver ovary, untwist pedicle [1]
• Observe for colour change — may take 10-15 minutes [1]
• Preserve ovary even if appears infarcted — oophorectomy is rarely needed [1]
• Oophoropexy (shortening utero-ovarian ligament) if torsion associated with anatomical abnormality [1]
• Cystectomy if underlying ovarian cyst is the trigger (most common in adolescents) [1]
• Bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy not indicated unless bilateral torsion — perform contralateral oophoropexy [1]

**(d)** [6 marks]
• Ovarian conservation results in normal fertility in >90% of cases [1]
• Oophorectomy in adolescent reduces fertility by 50% if contralateral ovary abnormal [1]
• Ovarian torsion recurrence: 2-3% after untwisting alone; 0.5% after oophoropexy [1]
• Impact on menopause: earlier if both ovaries affected [1]
• Hormonal function: preserved if one normal ovary [1]
• Counselling: future reproductive planning, importance of seeking care for recurrent pain [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This child likely has ovarian torsion — confirmed by enlarged >5 cm ovary with absent Doppler signals. Emergency diagnostic laparoscopy is indicated. At surgery, the ovary should be untwisted and preserved regardless of appearance. Oophorectomy is reserved for frankly necrotic, unsalvageable tissue. Oophoropexy should be considered for recurrence prevention. Long-term, preservation of one ovary ensures normal fertility and endocrine function in the vast majority.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Discusses false-negative Doppler rates in ovarian torsion
• Strongly advocates ovarian preservation
• Discusses oophoropexy for recurrence prevention
• Provides fertility outcome data post-torsion

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Mayer-Rokitansky-Küster-Hauser (MRKH) [Total: 20 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 16-year-old girl with primary amenorrhoea. She has normal breast development (Tanner 5) and pubic hair (Tanner 5). She has cyclical abdominal pain. External genitalia are normal with normal labia and clitoris. On examination, there is a blind-ending vaginal pouch (2 cm deep). US shows absent uterus. Ovaries appear normal bilaterally. Karyotype 46XX.

**(a)** What is the likely diagnosis, and what are the associated anomalies? **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Discuss the diagnostic workup and differential diagnoses. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Outline the management options for vaginal agenesis, including vaginal dilation (Frank/Ingram method) and surgical vaginoplasty. **[6 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the psychological and fertility implications of MRKH syndrome. **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [5 marks]
• MRKH (Mayer-Rokitansky-Küster-Hauser) syndrome [1]
• Müllerian agenesis: absent uterus, absent/atretic upper vagina [1]
• Ovaries are normal (functioning) [1]
• Associated anomalies: renal (30-40% — unilateral agenesis, horseshoe kidney, pelvic kidney) [1]
• Skeletal (10-15% — vertebral anomalies, scoliosis) [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Karyotype (to exclude CAIS — 46XY) [1]
• US/MRI pelvis: confirms absent uterus, presence of ovaries; assess renal anatomy [1]
• MRI: best for Müllerian remnant and endometrial activity [1]
• Laparoscopy: if diagnostic doubt or symptomatic endometriosis [1]
• CAIS has similar presentation but karyotype 46XY, absent uterus, but gonads = testes not ovaries [1]

**(c)** [6 marks]
• First-line: vaginal dilation (Frank/Ingram method) — progressive dilation over 6-12 weeks [1]
   • Success: 80-90% achieve functional vaginal length (≥6-7 cm) [1]
   • Advantages: non-surgical, no scar, preserves sensation [1]
• Surgical: Vecchietti procedure (laparoscopic traction device) or McIndoe vaginoplasty (skin graft) [1]
   • Indications: failed dilation, patient preference [1]
   • Success: similar functional outcome but requires commitment to dilation post-op [1]
• Bowel vaginoplasty (sigmoid segment) — for complex cases [1]

**(d)** [4 marks]
• Psychological: diagnosis at adolescence can cause significant distress (body image, femininity, relationships) [1]
• Psychosexual counselling and peer support groups are essential [1]
• Fertility: cannot carry pregnancy (no uterus) but has ovaries → ❌? option to use own oocytes via surrogacy [1]
• Uterine transplantation: experimental option — not yet standard [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This adolescent has MRKH syndrome — Müllerian agenesis with normal ovaries (functioning), normal external genitalia, and 46XX karyotype. Associated anomalies include renal agenesis and vertebral anomalies; renal US/CT is essential. Vaginal dilation is first-line treatment and successful in >80%. Surgery is reserved for failed dilation. Psychological support and peer counselling are mandatory. Fertility options include surrogacy using own oocytes; uterine transplantation is experimental.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Distinguishes MRKH from CAIS (46XX vs 46XY)
• Provides vaginal dilation success rates (>80% for 6-7 cm)
• Discusses surrogacy and uterine transplantation as fertility options
• Emphasises psychological support

## QUESTION 3 — SAQ: Labial Adhesions [Total: 8 marks] [FOUNDATION]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** What are labial adhesions and at what age do they typically present? **[3 marks]**

**(b)** Outline the management options, including the role of topical oestrogen and surgical separation. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [3 marks]
• Fusion of the labia minora in the midline [1]
• Typically presents age 3 months — 6 years [1]
• Usually asymptomatic; may present with pooling of urine [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Asymptomatic: observation — 80% resolve spontaneously by puberty [1]
• Topical oestrogen cream: apply to adhesion line BD for 2-4 weeks [1]
   • Success: ~50-70%, mild side effects (breast budding, pigmentation) [1]
   • Recurrence: ~20-30% after stopping [1]
• Surgical separation: under local or general anaesthesia; gentle blunt separation [1]
   • Indicated: symptomatic (pooling, dysuria, recurrent UTI), failed medical [1]

## QUESTION 4 — SAQ: Imperforate Hymen [Total: 8 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 14-year-old girl presents with cyclical abdominal pain and primary amenorrhoea. She has a bulging bluish membrane at the introitus.

**(a)** What is the diagnosis and the underlying pathophysiology? **[3 marks]**

**(b)** Describe the management. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [3 marks]
• Imperforate hymen [1]
• Failure of hymenal canalisation during embryogenesis [1]
• Accumulation of menstrual blood behind hymen → haematocolpos → cyclical pain [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Examination confirms bulging bluish membrane at introitus [1]
• US: to confirm fluid-filled vagina (haematocolpos) ± haematometra [1]
• Surgery: cruciate incision of hymen under GA ± local anaesthesia [1]
• Excise excess hymenal tissue to prevent re-stenosis [1]
• Suture edges with absorbable suture [1]
