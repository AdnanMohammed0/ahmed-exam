## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Diagnosis and Treatment of PUV [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 2-week-old term male infant presents with poor feeding, lethargy, and a palpable bladder. He has had two febrile UTIs since birth. Serum creatinine is 110 μmol/L. US shows bilateral SFU grade IV hydroureteronephrosis with a thickened bladder wall and echogenic kidneys.

**(a)** What is the most likely diagnosis, and what investigation is required for confirmation? **[4 marks]**

**(b)** Describe the endoscopic technique for primary valve ablation, including the cystoscopic approach, valve morphology, energy source, and landmarks to preserve. **[6 marks]**

**(c)** Post-ablation, serum creatinine decreases to 60 μmol/L but stabilises at 80 μmol/L after 2 weeks. Explain the significance of this "creatinine nadir" and its prognostic value. **[4 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the concept of "valve bladder syndrome" — its pathophysiology, clinical features, and management. **[5 marks]**

**(e)** Outline the long-term surveillance protocol for a boy with PUV through childhood and into adulthood. **[3 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [4 marks]
• Posterior urethral valves (PUV) [1]
• Key signs: palpable bladder, bilateral hydroureteronephrosis, thickened bladder, echogenic kidneys [1]
• MCU (micturating cystourethrogram) for confirmation — shows dilated posterior urethra with abrupt narrowing at the external sphincter, thickened trabeculated bladder, ± VUR [2]
• Also: keyhole sign on US (dilated posterior urethra) [1]

**(b)** [6 marks]
• General anaesthesia, lithotomy position [1]
• Cystoscope: 6-8 Fr with 0° or 30° lens [1]
• Identify valves: leaf-like membranes extending from verumontanum anterolaterally to urethral wall [1]
• Ablation: Bugbee electrode (3Fr) at 15-20W cutting current, or Ho:YAG laser [1]
• Ablate at 5, 7, and 12 o'clock positions [1]
• Preserve: verumontanum (antegrade ejaculation), external sphincter (distal to valves) [1]
• Confirm: unobstructed passage of 8Fr feeding tube and visualise free flow during micturition [1]

**(c)** [4 marks]
• Creatinine nadir = lowest serum creatinine after adequate bladder drainage (catheter + ablation) [1]
• Best single predictor of long-term renal function [1]
• Nadir <50 μmol/L: good prognosis — likely normal renal function through childhood [1]
• Nadir 50-90 μmol/L: moderate — may develop CKD in adolescence [1]
• Nadir >90 μmol/L: poor — high risk of ESRD within 5-10 years [1]

**(d)** [5 marks]
• Valve bladder syndrome: abnormal bladder function persisting after successful valve ablation [1]
• Pathophysiology: polyuria (nephrogenic diabetes insipidus) → high urine output + detrusor hypertrophy + incomplete emptying → high storage/filling pressures [1]
• Clinical features: incontinence, recurrent UTIs, persistent hydronephrosis, high post-void residual [1]
• Management: optimised bladder emptying (CIC ± double voiding), anticholinergics (oxybutynin), α-blockers (tamsulosin) [1]
• In refractory cases: bladder augmentation (enterocystoplasty) ± Mitrofanoff channel [1]

**(e)** [3 marks]
• Renal function: U&E, eGFR every 6-12 months [1]
• Blood pressure monitoring annually [1]
• US annually to assess hydronephrosis and bladder emptying [1]
• Transition to adult nephrology/urology at 16-18 years [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This neonate has PUV, confirmed by MCU showing the characteristic dilated posterior urethra. Primary valve ablation is performed endoscopically — I would use an 8Fr cystoscope and Bugbee electrode at 15W, ablating at 5, 7, and 12 o'clock while preserving the verumontanum. The post-ablation creatinine nadir of 80 μmol/L is moderately elevated and carries a guarded long-term prognosis — he is at risk of developing CKD in adolescence. Valve bladder syndrome is his main long-term management challenge, requiring a multidisciplinary approach of bladder optimisation, urodynamics monitoring, and eventual transition to adult services.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Quotes specific creatinine nadir thresholds with corresponding prognoses
• Describes valve ablation with specific energy settings and positions
• Distinguishes valve bladder syndrome from persistent obstruction
• Includes transition planning to adult services

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Long-term Outcomes in PUV [Total: 20 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 14-year-old boy with a history of PUV ablated in infancy is now in CKD stage 4 (eGFR 25 mL/min/1.73 m²). He has hypertension, proteinuria, and breakthrough UTIs. He wets his pads at night and has urgency. His bladder capacity on urodynamics is 450 mL with high filling pressures (40 cmH₂O at capacity) and detrusor overactivity.

**(a)** Explain the relationship between PUV and progressive CKD, including the concept of "renal dysplasia" versus "obstructive nephropathy." **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Discuss the urodynamic findings in this boy and their implications for his renal function. **[4 marks]**

**(c)** Outline the medical and surgical management options for a hostile bladder in an adolescent with PUV. **[6 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the challenges of transitioning this patient from paediatric to adult urology/nephrology services. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [5 marks]
• Two components: congenital renal dysplasia (primary, due to early in utero obstruction during nephrogenesis) and obstructive nephropathy (secondary, due to ongoing high-pressure bladder) [2]
• Renal dysplasia: irreversible, determined by severity and timing of obstruction in utero [1]
• Obstructive nephropathy: potentially modifiable by optimising bladder dynamics [1]
• The balance between these two components determines the trajectory of renal decline [1]

**(b)** [4 marks]
• High filling pressures (40 cmH₂O) — significantly elevated; normal <10-15 cmH₂O [1]
• Accumulated effect: impaired ureteric drainage → back-pressure → progressive nephron loss [1]
• Detrusor overactivity → incontinence, urgency [1]
• These are modifiable risk factors — improving bladder dynamics may slow CKD progression [1]

**(c)** [6 marks]
• Medical: anticholinergics (oxybutynin 5 mg TDS, solifenacin) to reduce bladder pressures [1]
• α-blockers (tamsulosin) to reduce bladder outlet resistance [1]
• CIC if incomplete emptying — may need Mitrofanoff channel for independence in adolescence [1]
• Bowel management for constipation [1]
• Surgical: onabotulinum toxin A (Botox) intra-detrusor injection — reduces overactivity temporarily [1]
• Bladder augmentation (enterocystoplasty) — for refractory hostile bladder; creates a low-pressure reservoir [1]

**(d)** [5 marks]
• Transition: structured handover between paediatric and adult teams at 16-18 years [1]
• Key challenges: loss to follow-up (common — up to 40%), patient non-compliance in adolescence [1]
• Lack of adult clinicians experienced in congenital urology [1]
• Sexual function/fertility concerns: ejaculatory dysfunction after valve ablation (retrograde ejaculation, anejaculation) [1]
• CKD management: blood pressure control, phosphate binders, vitamin D, erythropoietin; planning for RRT (dialysis/transplantation) [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This boy's CKD reflects both congenital renal dysplasia (nephron deficit from in utero obstruction) and ongoing obstructive nephropathy from a hostile bladder with high filling pressures. His urodynamics demonstrate pressures that are damaging to renal function. Management must address the bladder — optimizing emptying (CIC ± Mitrofanoff), reducing pressures (anticholinergics, Botox), and considering augmentation if conservative measures fail. The transition to adult services is high-risk: many patients disengage during this period. A structured transition programme with joint clinics is essential.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Distinguishes congenital from acquired renal damage
• Recognises that bladder optimisation can slow CKD progression
• Discusses Mitrofanoff channel for adolescent independence
• Addresses sexual/ejaculatory function and fertility counselling

## QUESTION 3 — SAQ: Creatinine Nadir [Total: 8 marks] [FOUNDATION]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** Define "creatinine nadir" in the context of PUV and state the optimal timing for its measurement. **[2 marks]**

**(b)** Stratify the renal prognosis based on the nadir value and describe the corresponding long-term management. **[6 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [2 marks]
• Creatinine nadir: the lowest serum creatinine achieved after adequate bladder drainage (catheterisation + valve ablation) [1]
• Timing: measured at 2-4 weeks post-ablation, after stabilisation of renal function [1]

**(b)** [6 marks]
• Nadir <50 μmol/L: good prognosis — likely normal renal function through adolescence; annual BP and U&E monitoring [2]
• Nadir 50-90 μmol/L: moderate risk — ~30-50% develop CKD by adulthood; regular follow-up, low threshold for managing bladder dynamics [2]
• Nadir >90 μmol/L: poor prognosis — >60% progress to ESRD by adolescence; early nephrology referral, prepare for RRT [2]

## QUESTION 4 — SAQ: Urinary Diversion in PUV [Total: 8 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** State three indications for temporary urinary diversion in a neonate with PUV (other than valve ablation). **[3 marks]**

**(b)** Compare percutaneous vesicostomy (Blocksom) and cutaneous ureterostomy, including the advantages and disadvantages of each. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [3 marks]
• Small premature infant with urethral instrumentation risk [1]
• Urethral stricture/injury from prior instrumentation [1]
• Persistent severe urosepsis despite catheterisation [1]
• (Also: massive hydronephrosis requiring proximal drainage, very poor general condition)

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Vesicostomy: opens bladder dome to anterior abdominal wall — low pressure, reversible, allows bladder cycling [1]
   • Advantages: simple, maintains bladder function, reversible
   • Disadvantages: skin excoriation, stomal stenosis, not ideal for long-term
• Cutaneous ureterostomy: ureter brought to skin — provides direct upper tract drainage [1]
   • Advantages: bypasses VUR, high-pressure system
   • Disadvantages: stomal stenosis, difficult reversal, no bladder cycling, higher complication rate [1]

## QUESTION 5 — LCV: Prenatally Diagnosed PUV [Total: 35 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~40 minutes

─── STAGE 1: PRENATAL ──────────────────────────────────
A 20-week foetus (male) shows bilateral hydroureteronephrosis, a thickened bladder, keyhole sign, and oligohydramnios. The parents are counselled about possible PUV.

**Q1a.** What is the prognostic significance of oligohydramnios detected at 20 weeks? **[3 marks]**
**Q1b.** Discuss the evidence for vesicoamniotic shunting, referencing the PLUTO trial. **[6 marks]**

─── STAGE 2: NEONATAL ─────────────────────────────────
The mother declines intervention. The infant is born at 38 weeks. Birth weight 2.8 kg. He requires immediate respiratory support due to pulmonary hypoplasia. A 6Fr feeding tube is passed per urethra draining 50 mL. Creatinine at 48 hours is 130 μmol/L.

**Q2a.** Outline your immediate management. **[4 marks]**
**Q2b.** Describe the MCU findings you expect and how you would perform valve ablation. **[6 marks]**

─── STAGE 3: POST-ABLATION ────────────────────────────
Creatinine nadir at 4 weeks is 95 μmol/L. At 12 months, creatinine is 85 μmol/L. He has recurrent UTIs despite prophylaxis. MCU shows no residual valves. US shows persistent bilateral hydronephrosis.

**Q3a.** What is the most likely cause of his persistent hydronephrosis and UTIs? **[2 marks]**
**Q3b.** Discuss the role of urodynamics in this child and the management options depending on the findings. **[6 marks]**

─── STAGE 4: ADOLESCENCE ──────────────────────────────
At 16 years, his eGFR is 35 mL/min/1.73 m² (CKD stage 3b). He is preparing for transition to adult services.

**Q4a.** Outline the key components of a successful transition programme. **[4 marks]**
**Q4b.** What are his future renal replacement therapy options and any special considerations for transplantation in PUV? **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**FULL MARKING GUIDE:**

**Q1a.** [3 marks]
• Oligohydramnios at 20 weeks indicates severe early-onset obstruction [1]
• Risk of pulmonary hypoplasia (Potter sequence) — lethal if severe [1]
• Predicts poor renal prognosis — nephrogenesis already disrupted [1]

**Q1b.** [6 marks]
• VAS: drains foetal bladder into amniotic cavity to restore amniotic fluid [1]
• PLUTO trial (2013): RCT comparing VAS to conservative management [1]
• Results: higher perinatal survival with VAS but no difference in renal function at 1 year [1]
• VAS does not prevent renal dysplasia (already occurred) — it addresses pulmonary hypoplasia [1]
• High complication rate: shunt migration, occlusion, preterm labour [1]
• Conclusion: may improve survival but does not improve renal outcome [1]

**Q2a.** [4 marks]
• Respiratory support for pulmonary hypoplasia [1]
• Secure urethral catheter (6Fr feeding tube) on free drainage [1]
• IV fluids, monitoring U&E and output [1]
• Prophylactic antibiotics; wait for respiratory stability before MCU [1]

**Q2b.** [6 marks]
• MCU: dilated posterior urethra, abrupt narrowing at membranous urethra, trabeculated bladder, ± VUR [2]
• Valve ablation after stabilisation and infection-free [1]
• Cystoscope 8Fr, Bugbee electrode 3Fr at 15W [1]
• Ablate at 5, 7, 12 o'clock [1]
• Post-ablation: confirm free flow, leave catheter 24-48 hours [1]

**Q3a.** [2 marks]
• Valve bladder syndrome: abnormal bladder function despite unobstructed urethra [2]

**Q3b.** [6 marks]
• Urodynamics: assesses bladder capacity, compliance, filling pressures, detrusor overactivity [1]
• Findings and management:
   • Low compliance with high filling pressures → anticholinergics, CIC [1]
   • Detrusor overactivity → anticholinergics, Botox [1]
   • Incomplete emptying → CIC ± α-blockers [1]
   • Poor capacity → timed voiding, consider augmentation if refractory [1]
• Repeat US post-optimisation to assess improvement [1]

**Q4a.** [4 marks]
• Joint paediatric-adult clinics for 1-2 years [1]
• Named transition coordinator [1]
• Patient education: disease understanding, medication compliance, follow-up importance [1]
• Fertility and sexual function counselling [1]

**Q4b.** [4 marks]
• Options: haemodialysis, peritoneal dialysis, transplantation [1]
• Transplantation in PUV: higher surgical complication rate (thickened bladder, urological issues) [1]
• Pre-transplant: ensure low-pressure bladder (may need urodynamics, possible augmentation) [1]
• Live-related donor preferred; native nephrectomy if recurrent UTIs/stone disease [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Integrates prenatal and postnatal management across the disease trajectory
• Describes PLUTO trial findings with appropriate nuance (survival benefit, no renal benefit)
• Manages valve bladder syndrome as a distinct long-term entity
• Includes pre-transplant urological assessment

## QUESTION 6 — EMQ: PUV Complications and Associations [Total: 10 marks] [BOARD]

**Theme: Posterior Urethral Valves — Complications**

**OPTIONS (each option may be used once, more than once, or not at all):**

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| A. Valve bladder syndrome | F. Urinary tract stones |
| B. Renal dysplasia | G. VUR (high grade) |
| C. Pulmonary hypoplasia | H. Urethral stricture |
| D. Overflow incontinence | I. Nocturnal enuresis |
| E. CKD/ESRD | J. Epididymo-orchitis |

**For each of the following clinical scenarios, select the single most appropriate complication:**

**1.** A newborn with PUV, oligohydramnios, and respiratory distress despite adequate urethral drainage. **[1 mark]**

**2.** A 10-year-old boy with a history of PUV ablated in infancy, now with eGFR 20 mL/min/1.73 m². **[1 mark]**

**3.** An 8-year-old with PUV, ongoing wetting between voids, incomplete emptying, and recurrent UTIs despite patent urethra on MCU. **[1 mark]**

**4.** A 2-week-old with PUV, elevated creatinine 150 μmol/L that only falls to 120 μmol/L after catheter drainage. **[1 mark]**

**5.** A 6-week-old with PUV, post-valve ablation. MCU shows contrast entering the renal parenchyma on the right. **[1 mark]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**ANSWERS & RATIONALE:**

**1.** **C** — Pulmonary hypoplasia. Oligohydramnios in utero impairs lung development (Potter sequence). Respiratory distress at birth despite adequate urethral drainage indicates lung hypoplasia, not a renal/urological cause.

**2.** **E** — CKD/ESRD. eGFR 20 mL/min/1.73 m² is CKD stage 4-5. This is the cumulative result of congenital dysplasia and obstructive nephropathy from valve bladder syndrome.

**3.** **A** — Valve bladder syndrome. The triad of wetting, incomplete emptying, and recurrent UTIs despite patent urethra is classic valve bladder syndrome. Not simply incontinence from valve ablation.

**4.** **B** — Renal dysplasia. Poor nadir despite adequate drainage reflects congenital renal dysplasia, not ongoing obstruction. The kidneys are primarily dysplastic.

**5.** **G** — VUR (high grade) with intrarenal reflux. Contrast entering the parenchyma indicates high-pressure reflux into the collecting ducts — intrarenal reflux. This predicts scarring and worse renal prognosis.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Confusing valve bladder syndrome with persistent urethral obstruction
• Attributing all renal impairment to the valves themselves without recognising congenital dysplasia
• Not distinguishing pulmonary hypoplasia from a primary urological cause of respiratory distress

**EXAMINER'S NOTES — COMMON SCRIPT PATTERNS TO WATCH FOR:**
• Candidates often forget that the major predictor of long-term outcome is the creatinine NADIR, not the pre-ablation level
• Valve bladder syndrome is frequently misdiagnosed as residual valve obstruction — strong candidates distinguish the two
• The PLUTO trial is a popular question topic — candidates must know that VAS improves survival but NOT renal function
• Transition planning is consistently under-prepared in exam answers
