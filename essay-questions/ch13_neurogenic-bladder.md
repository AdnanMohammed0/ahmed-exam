## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Neonatal Neurogenic Bladder [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A newborn girl with myelomeningocele (L4 level) has just undergone closure of the neural tube defect. You are asked to assess her urinary tract. Prenatal US showed mild bilateral hydronephrosis. Postnatal US confirms bilateral SFU grade II hydronephrosis and a thickened bladder wall.

**(a)** Describe the expected innervation and bladder function in a child with L4-level myelomeningocele. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Outline the initial assessment and investigations you would arrange in the neonatal period. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Define the concept of "safe bladder storage pressures" and explain the rationale for starting clean intermittent catheterisation (CIC) in infancy. **[6 marks]**

**(d)** Describe the technique of CIC in a female infant, including catheter choice, frequency, and how to manage complications. **[6 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [5 marks] — 1 per valid point up to 5:
• L4 level: sacral reflex arc is intact (S2-4) because the lesion is above the sacral cord [1]
• Lower motor neuron component: loss of voluntary control but preserved sacral reflex [1]
• Bladder: detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia — detrusor overactivity with external sphincter contraction simultaneously [1]
• High-pressure storage and voiding: elevated leak point pressure, incomplete emptying [1]
• Upper tracts at risk if pressures not managed [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Renal US: baseline hydronephrosis, parenchymal thickness, bladder wall thickness [1]
• Serum creatinine + electrolytes (creatinine may be high at birth but will fall) [1]
• Video-urodynamics at 1-3 months: assess bladder capacity, compliance, filling pressures, leak point pressure, presence of VUR [1]
• MCU: assess for VUR and bladder outlet [1]
• Baseline DMSA at 3-6 months to assess differential function [1]

**(c)** [6 marks]
• Safe storage pressure: bladder pressure <30-40 cmH₂O during filling [1]
• Above this threshold → impaired ureteric drainage → hydronephrosis → renal scarring [1]
• CIC rationale: empties bladder completely at regular intervals → eliminates high-pressure storage period [1]
• Maintains low pressure throughout filling-emptying cycle [1]
• CIC initiated early (within first months) prevents upper tract deterioration [1]
• Prevents UTIs by reducing residual urine [1]

**(d)** [6 marks]
• Catheter: 6-8Fr Nelaton (straight) catheter, single-use or reusable [1]
• Frequency: every 3-4 hours, 5-6 times/day [1]
• Technique: clean (not sterile) — wash hands, clean urethral meatus with water, lubricate catheter tip [1]
• In girls: labia separated, catheter inserted 3-5 cm until urine flows, then advance 1-2 cm more [1]
• Remove slowly when bladder empty; gentle suprapubic pressure if needed [1]
• Complications: UTIs (treat only if symptomatic), urethral trauma, stricture, haematuria (usually self-limiting) [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This child with L4 myelomeningocele has a neurogenic bladder with detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia — a high-pressure storage system at risk of renal damage. Initial assessment includes US for baseline hydronephrosis and urodynamics at 1-3 months to quantify bladder pressures. CIC should be started in infancy, every 3-4 hours, using a clean technique with a 6Fr Nelaton catheter. The goal is to maintain safe storage pressures below 30-40 cmH₂O to preserve renal function.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Correlates spinal level with specific bladder dysfunction pattern
• Defines safe bladder pressure threshold
• Emphasises early CIC as preventative (not reactive) management
• Describes clean (not sterile) technique — a common source of over-stringency in parents

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Management of Detrusor Overactivity [Total: 20 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 6-year-old boy with myelomeningocele (L3 level) is on CIC 5 times/day with oxybutynin 5 mg TDS. Despite this, he has leak between catheterisations and recurrent febrile UTIs. US shows new bilateral hydronephrosis. Urodynamics show: capacity 150 mL (expected 360 mL), compliance 8 mL/cmH₂O, peak detrusor pressure 60 cmH₂O, leak point pressure 50 cmH₂O.

**(a)** Interpret the urodynamic findings and explain why his current management is insufficient. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Discuss the options for escalating medical management before considering surgery. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Describe the technique and evidence for intradetrusor botulinum toxin A injection in children with neurogenic bladder. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** Outline the surgical options if medical and minimally invasive treatments fail, including the indications for each. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [5 marks] — 1 per valid point up to 5:
• Capacity 150 mL — reduced (normal ~360 mL at age 6: [age in years + 2] × 30) [1]
• Compliance 8 mL/cmH₂O — poor (normal >20) [1]
• Peak detrusor pressure 60 cmH₂O — dangerously elevated [1]
• Leak point pressure 50 cmH₂O — "hostile bladder" [1]
• High pressure + poor compliance + reduced capacity = at risk for upper tract deterioration [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Optimise CIC frequency: increase to 6 times/day [1]
• Consider adding α-blocker (tamsulosin 0.4 mg) to reduce bladder outlet resistance [1]
• Switch from oxybutynin to solifenacin (fewer side effects, longer half-life) [1]
• Anticholinergic dose optimisation: weight-adjusted max dose [1]
• Aggressive constipation management (any faecal loading worsens bladder pressures) [1]

**(c)** [5 marks]
• Botox: onabotulinumtoxinA (Botox) 100-300 units reconstituted in 10-20 mL saline [1]
• Cystoscopic injection: 20-30 injection sites spread across detrusor (avoid trigone — reflux risk) [1]
• Effect: chemodenervation of detrusor → reduces overactivity for 6-9 months [1]
• Efficacy: 60-80% response (reduced pressures, improved compliance, increased capacity) [1]
• Repeat injections annually; efficacy may wane over time [1]

**(d)** [5 marks]
• Augmentation enterocystoplasty: for refractory small capacity/poor compliance (gold standard) [1]
• Mitrofanoff channel: facilitates CIC in children with urethral difficulty (wheelchair, obesity) [1]
• Bladder neck reconstruction/sling: if sphincteric incontinence co-exists [1]
• AUS (artificial urinary sphincter): options limited in children; high revision rate [1]
• Urinary diversion (ileal conduit): last resort; rarely in children [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This boy has a hostile bladder — capacity <50% expected, poor compliance (8), and dangerously high pressures (60 cmH₂O). This is damaging his kidneys. Before surgery, I would escalate: optimise CIC frequency, add an α-blocker, consider switching to solifenacin, and ensure aggressive bowel management. If pressures remain high, intradetrusor Botox is the next step — 300 units injected into 20-30 detrusor sites, avoiding the trigone. If this fails, augmentation ileocystoplasty is the definitive option.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Calculates expected bladder capacity by age ([age+2]×30)
• Quotes specific compliance and pressure thresholds
• Describes Botox injection with specific sites and dose
• Avoids trigone injection (key technical point)

## QUESTION 3 — SAQ: CIC Complications [Total: 8 marks] [FOUNDATION]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** List four complications of long-term CIC in children with neurogenic bladder. **[4 marks]**

**(b)** State how you would manage each complication listed. **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [4 marks — 1 each]
• Symptomatic UTI (febrile) [1]
• Urethral stricture (especially in boys) [1]
• Bladder stones [1]
• Autonomic dysreflexia (in lesions at or above T6) [1]

**(b)** [4 marks]
• UTI: culture-directed antibiotics; treat symptomatic only (asymptomatic bacteriuria does NOT need treatment) [1]
• Stricture: urethral dilatation or endoscopic urethrotomy [1]
• Bladder stones: endoscopic or open cystolithotomy; increase CIC frequency and bladder washouts to prevent [1]
• Autonomic dysreflexia: treat triggering cause (distended bladder, impacted stool), antihypertensives (nifedipine) [1]

## QUESTION 4 — SAQ: Transition in Neurogenic Bladder [Total: 8 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** Outline the key components of a transition programme for an adolescent with neurogenic bladder moving from paediatric to adult urology services. **[4 marks]**

**(b)** Discuss the specific concerns regarding sexual function and fertility in males with myelomeningocele. **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [4 marks]
• Named transition coordinator [1]
• Joint paediatric-adult clinics (1-2 years overlap) [1]
• Self-catheterisation education (move from parent-dependent to independent CIC) [1]
• Patient-held record and education about their condition [1]

**(b)** [4 marks]
• Erectile dysfunction: common in males with myelomeningocele (sacral nerve dysfunction) [1]
• Options: PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil), intracavernosal injections, penile prosthesis [1]
• Ejaculatory dysfunction: may have anejaculation or retrograde ejaculation [1]
• Fertility: surgical sperm retrieval (testicular sperm extraction, TESE) with ICSI [1]

## QUESTION 5 — LCV: Neuropathic Bladder — Failed Medical Management [Total: 35 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~40 minutes

─── STAGE 1: PRESENTATION ───────────────────────────────
A 14-year-old girl with myelomeningocele (L5-S1 level) is referred with deteriorating continence and recurrent UTIs. She has been on CIC + oxybutynin since infancy. Urodynamics show poor compliance (10 mL/cmH₂O), detrusor overactivity, and leak point pressure 45 cmH₂O. Capacity is 200 mL (expected 450 mL).

**Q1a.** What are her current risks and what interventions should be considered? **[4 marks]**
**Q1b.** She asks about becoming dry and independent. Discuss the options. **[5 marks]**

─── STAGE 2: SURGICAL PLANNING ──────────────────────────
You recommend augmentation ileocystoplasty with a Mitrofanoff channel. She has a normal distal appendix on US.

**Q2a.** Describe the pre-operative preparation, including bowel preparation and antibiotic prophylaxis. **[4 marks]**
**Q2b.** Describe the operative steps for augmentation ileocystoplasty. **[6 marks]**

─── STAGE 3: POST-OPERATIVE CARE ────────────────────────
Post-operatively, she develops increasing abdominal distension, fever, and oliguria on day 4.

**Q3a.** What is your differential diagnosis? **[3 marks]**
**Q3b.** A CT shows a small bowel leak. How would you manage this? **[4 marks]**

─── STAGE 4: LONG-TERM FOLLOW-UP ───────────────────────
She recovers well. At 1 year, she is dry on CIC 5 times/day. No further UTIs. She is preparing for transition to adult services.

**Q4a.** Outline her long-term surveillance protocol, including investigations and their frequency. **[5 marks]**
**Q4b.** Discuss the risk of malignancy and metabolic complications in augmented patients. **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**FULL MARKING GUIDE:**

**Q1a.** [4 marks]
• High-pressure bladder → risk of ongoing renal deterioration [1]
• Consider Botox injection as bridge therapy [1]
• Discuss enterocystoplasty with Mitrofanoff [1]
• Assess current CIC regimen, bowel management [1]

**Q1b.** [5 marks]
• Augmentation increases capacity and reduces pressure → improved storage [1]
• Mitrofanoff channel (appendix) provides easy catheter access at umbilicus [1]
• Bladder neck sling/reconstruction if intrinsic sphincter deficiency [1]
• Realistic expectations: 80-90% achieve social dryness, but requires lifelong catheterisation [1]
• Risk of surgical complications and need for revision [1]

**Q2a.** [4 marks]
• Bowel preparation: Movicol or enemas day before surgery [1]
• IV antibiotics: gentamicin + metronidazole (cover bowel flora) [1]
• DVT prophylaxis in older/adolescent patients [1]
• Mark Mitrofanoff site at umbilicus [1]

**Q2b.** [6 marks]
• Harvest 15-20 cm terminal ileum (20 cm from ileocaecal valve) with intact vascular arcade [1]
• Bowel continuity restored with stapled or hand-sewn anastomosis [1]
• Detubularise bowel segment along antimesenteric border [1]
• Clam (bivalve) bladder in coronal midline [1]
• Anastomose bowel to bladder with 3-0/4-0 absorbable suture [1]
• Implant appendix into bladder (Mitrofanoff); bring stoma to umbilicus [1]

**Q3a.** [3 marks]
• Anastomotic leak (bowel or bladder) [1]
• Ileus / small bowel obstruction [1]
• Peritonitis / intra-abdominal collection [1]

**Q3b.** [4 marks]
• IV broad-spectrum antibiotics [1]
• Return to theatre for laparotomy and repair of small bowel leak [1]
• Washout and drainage [1]
• Consider temporary diversion (ostomy) if contamination severe [1]

**Q4a.** [5 marks]
• Renal US and U&E annually [1]
• Cystoscopy with biopsy of augmentation segment starting at 10 years post-op [1]
• Vitamin B12 levels annually (if >30 cm ileum resected) [1]
• Urodynamics if new hydronephrosis or incontinence [1]
• DMSA every 3-5 years to reassess renal function [1]

**Q4b.** [4 marks]
• Malignancy: adenocarcinoma, SCC, TCC; latency 10-20 years; annual surveillance [1]
• Metabolic acidosis (hyperchloraemic): NaHCO₃ supplementation [1]
• B12 deficiency: monitor and supplement [1]
• Stones: regular washouts; treat endoscopic/PCNL [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Provides complete transition-ready long-term surveillance protocol
• Recognises Mitrofanoff as independence-enabling in adolescence
• Discusses malignancy risk with specific latency and surveillance cystoscopy timing
• Manages bowel leak post-augmentation as a recognised complication

## QUESTION 6 — EMQ: Neurogenic Bladder [Total: 10 marks] [BOARD]

**Theme: Spinal Level and Bladder Pattern**

**OPTIONS (each option may be used once, more than once, or not at all):**

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| A. Detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia | F. Acontractile bladder |
| B. Detrusor overactivity alone | G. Normal bladder function |
| C. Sphincter weakness incontinence | H. Autonomic dysreflexia |
| D. Complete lower motor neuron lesion | I. Overflow incontinence |
| E. High-pressure storage | J. Uninhibited sphincter relaxation |

**For each of the following clinical scenarios, select the single most appropriate urodynamic pattern:**

**1.** A newborn with sacral agenesis (absent S2-4). US shows a distended bladder. The bladder expresses easily with gentle suprapubic pressure. **[1 mark]**

**2.** A child with L3 myelomeningocele. Urodynamics show detrusor contraction against a closed sphincter, producing high intravesical pressures. **[1 mark]**

**3.** A 14-year-old boy with T4 complete spinal cord injury. During catheterisation, he develops severe hypertension, headache, bradycardia, and sweating. **[1 mark]**

**4.** A child with an L1 myelomeningocele. Urodynamics show high filling pressures (40 cmH₂O at capacity 80 mL) with a leak point pressure of 55 cmH₂O and bilateral VUR. **[1 mark]**

**5.** A girl with a repaired lipomeningocoele. She has continuous dribbling incontinence. Urodynamics show a low-pressure system with a leak point pressure of 15 cmH₂O. **[1 mark]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**ANSWERS & RATIONALE:**

**1.** **D** — Complete lower motor neuron lesion. Sacral agenesis destroys the sacral micturition centre (S2-4). The result is an areflexic, acontractile bladder with denervated sphincter. The bladder expresses easily because there is no outlet resistance.

**2.** **A** — Detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia. L3-level lesion spares the sacral reflex arc. The detrusor contracts reflexly but the external sphincter simultaneously contracts (dyssynergia), creating high-pressure storage. This is the classic pattern in upper motor neuron lesions affecting the sacral pathways.

**3.** **H** — Autonomic dysreflexia. Spinal cord injury above T6 (splanchnic outflow). Bladder distension triggers massive sympathetic discharge → hypertension, reflex bradycardia, headache. This is a medical emergency requiring immediate catheter drainage and antihypertensives.

**4.** **E** — High-pressure storage. The urodynamic parameters (40 cmH₂O filling pressure, 55 cmH₂O leak point pressure, reduced capacity, bilateral VUR) define a hostile, high-pressure system at imminent risk of renal deterioration.

**5.** **C** — Sphincter weakness incontinence. A leak point pressure of 15 cmH₂O is very low (normal >60 cmH₂O in children). This indicates intrinsic sphincter deficiency — the bladder neck/sphincter cannot maintain closure. Continuous dribbling results.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Assuming all myelomeningoceles produce the same bladder pattern — level matters
• Confusing autonomic dysreflexia with anxiety or sepsis
• Not recognising that a low leak point pressure can be as damaging as high pressure (incontinence, not renal damage)

**EXAMINER'S NOTES — COMMON SCRIPT PATTERNS TO WATCH FOR:**
• Many candidates cannot correlate spinal level with bladder/sphincter pattern — this is essential clinical knowledge
• CIC is often described as "catheterisation" without detailing the technique, catheter size, or frequency
• Botox use in children is increasingly tested — candidates should know dose (100-300 units) and injection pattern
• Transition to adult services is a high-yield topic that many candidates neglect
