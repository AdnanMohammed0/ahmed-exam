## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Initial Management of Bladder Exstrophy [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A term male newborn is born with classic bladder exstrophy. The bladder template appears adequate. The pubic diastasis is 3.5 cm. The child is otherwise well from the resuscitation standpoint.

**(a)** Describe the immediate neonatal management of bladder exstrophy, including the care of the bladder template and the timing of surgical intervention. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Compare the modern staged repair of bladder exstrophy (MSRE) with the complete primary repair of bladder exstrophy (CPRE) approach. **[6 marks]**

**(c)** Describe the role of pelvic osteotomy in bladder exstrophy repair, including the optimal timing and techniques available. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the long-term continence expectations after successful bladder exstrophy closure, including the definition of "social continence" and the factors that influence it. **[6 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [5 marks] — 1 per valid point up to 5:
• Cover bladder with non-adherent membrane (plastic wrap/Saran) to prevent adherence to nappy/diaper [1]
• Umbilical cord clamp — avoid damaging bladder template [1]
• No circumcision (prepuce used for future epispadias repair) [1]
• Orthopaedic assessment for osteotomy [1]
• Timing: primary closure ideally within 72 hours (returns bladder to pelvis before moulding occurs) [1]

**(b)** [6 marks]
• MSRE (Jeffs/Georgeson): staged — bladder closure + pelvic osteotomy (newborn), epispadias repair (6-12 months), bladder neck reconstruction (4-5 years) [1-2]
• CPRE (Mitchell/BG): single-stage complete repair — bladder closure + epispadias repair + bladder neck reconstruction (newborn) [1-2]
• CPRE advantage: potentially fewer anaesthetics, earlier bladder cycling [1]
• MSRE advantage: less surgical stress in neonate, allows assessment of bladder growth before BNR [1]
• CPRE disadvantage: higher complication rate in single stage, longer operating time [1]
• Current practice: MSRE more widely performed; CPRE in select high-volume centres [1]

**(c)** [5 marks]
• Pelvic osteotomy: approximates pubic bones to reduce tension on bladder closure [1]
• Indications: delayed closure (>72 hours), wide diastasis >3-4 cm, redo closure [1]
• Timing: at time of bladder closure [1]
• Techniques: posterior iliac osteotomy (most common), anterior innominate osteotomy [1]
• Post-op: lower limb immobilisation (mermaid/pod spica) for 4-6 weeks [1]

**(d)** [6 marks]
• Social continence: dry at least 3 hours between voids, dry at night, manageable with normal activities [1]
• Factors: bladder capacity at time of BNR (>100 mL predicts better outcome) [1]
• Quality of bladder template (elastic vs fibrotic) [1]
• Pubic diastasis closure quality [1]
• Continence after modern staged repair: ~60-70% achieve social continence; 20-30% need CIC or further surgery [1]
• Dry interval: >3 hours is the benchmark; >4 hours is excellent [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
Neonatal management involves protecting the bladder template with plastic wrap, avoiding circumcision, and planned closure within 72 hours. I favour the modern staged repair (MSRE): primary bladder closure with osteotomy in the newborn period, epispadias repair at 6-12 months, and bladder neck reconstruction at 4-5 years when bladder capacity >100 mL. Pelvic osteotomy is indicated given the 3.5 cm diastasis. Long-term continence depends largely on bladder capacity before BNR — with optimal management, ~70% achieve social continence.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Gives specific numerical thresholds (72 hours, 3-4 cm diastasis, >100 mL capacity)
• Compares MSRE vs CPRE with evidence-based pros/cons
• Defines social continence with specific dry interval
• Recommends against neonatal circumcision

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Failed Bladder Closure [Total: 20 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 2-year-old boy underwent primary bladder closure for exstrophy in the neonatal period. He now presents with a complete dehiscence of the bladder closure. The bladder template is small and fibrotic. The pubic diastasis is 5 cm.

**(a)** List the causes of failed primary bladder closure in exstrophy. **[4 marks]**

**(b)** Outline the surgical options for redo closure, including the role of pelvic osteotomy. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Discuss the ongoing management of the failed bladder exstrophy, including the role of bladder augmentation and urinary diversion. **[6 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the psychological and social impact of repeated surgical interventions on the child and family, and the importance of multidisciplinary care. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [4 marks — 1 each]
• Inadequate pelvic closure (no osteotomy or failed osteotomy) [1]
• Wound infection/dehiscence [1]
• Bladder prolapse through the closure [1]
• Post-operative bladder distension/inadequate drainage [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Delayed redo closure with pelvic osteotomy (posterior iliac) — mandatory [1]
• Osteotomy: bilateral posterior iliac; may need combined with anterior [1]
• Meticulous re-dissection of bladder from abdominal wall [1]
• Bladder template may be inadequate — may need initial growth period before BNR [1]
• Post-op: skeletal traction or spica cast for 4-6 weeks [1]

**(c)** [6 marks]
• Bladder augmentation (enterocystoplasty) — if small fibrotic bladder template [1]
• Bladder neck reconstruction (Young-Dees-Leadbetter or Pippi Salle) — for continence [1]
• CIC if incomplete emptying [1]
• Submucosal injection (Deflux) at bladder neck — adjunctive [1]
• Continent urinary diversion (Mitrofanoff) — if urethral catheterisation not possible [1]
• Urinary diversion (ileal conduit) — last resort [1]

**(d)** [5 marks]
• Multiple operations → missed school, body image concerns, social withdrawal [1]
• Incontinence → social stigma, bullying, reduced self-esteem [1]
• Importance of psychologist, specialist nurse, and child life specialist [1]
• Parental support groups (exstrophy support networks) [1]
• Preparation for transition to adolescent/adult exstrophy clinic [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
Failed closure in exstrophy is devastating but salvageable. Causes include inadequate pelvic closure (most common), infection, and bladder prolapse. Redo closure requires bilateral posterior iliac osteotomy, meticulous re-dissection, and prolonged post-operative immobilisation. If the bladder template is inadequate after redo, augmentation enterocystoplasty may be needed for capacity and compliance, with a Mitrofanoff channel for catheterisation. The psychological impact is profound — multidisciplinary care with psychology and specialist nursing support is essential.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Identifies specific cause of failure (most commonly inadequate osteotomy)
• Describes salvage pathway with specific procedures
• Addresses psychological impact — a key discriminator
• Mentions exstrophy support networks

## QUESTION 3 — SAQ: Epispadias Repair [Total: 8 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 9-month-old boy with a history of bladder exstrophy closure now has a short, dorsally angulated penis with a dorsal meatus at the base of the glans.

**(a)** Classify the epispadias in this child. **[1 mark]**

**(b)** Describe the Cantwell-Ransley (Mitchell technique) for epispadias repair. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** State two complications of epispadias repair. **[2 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [1 mark]
• Penopubic (subcoronal/penile) epispadias — meatus at base of glans (can also use: distal epispadias)

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Dorsal incision circumferentially around glans extending to meatus [1]
• Mobilise urethral plate as a strip [1]
• Mobilise corpora cavernosa dorsally and rotate them medially (ventral) to correct dorsal chordee [1]
• Tubularise urethral plate over a 10Fr catheter [1]
• Glansplasty and skin closure [1]

**(c)** [2 marks — 1 each]
• Urethrocutaneous fistula [1]
• Urethral stricture [1]
• (Also: persistent dorsal chordee, glans dehiscence)

## QUESTION 4 — SAQ: Pelvic Osteotomy [Total: 8 marks] [FOUNDATION]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** Explain the purpose of pelvic osteotomy in bladder exstrophy repair. **[2 marks]**

**(b)** Compare posterior iliac osteotomy with anterior innominate osteotomy in terms of technique, positioning, and immobilisation requirements. **[6 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [2 marks]
• Allows approximation of widened pubic symphysis [1]
• Reduces tension on bladder closure and facilitates abdominal wall closure [1]

**(b)** [6 marks]
• Posterior iliac: patient prone, osteotomy posterior to sacroiliac joint, spica cast post-op [1-2]
• Anterior innominate: supine, osteotomy through ilium above acetabulum, easier positioning but less rotational correction [1-2]
• Posterior: more effective for large diastasis, standard in exstrophy [1]
• Anterior: less commonly used alone in exstrophy; combined with posterior for severe cases [1]

─── STAGE 1: NEWBORN ──────────────────────────────────

**Q1b.** Outline the surgical plan for the first year of life. **[5 marks]**

─── STAGE 2: CHILDHOOD ───────────────────────────────
At age 4, the child has a bladder capacity of 120 mL. He is incontinent despite timed voiding. Cystoscopy confirms an open bladder neck.

**Q2a.** What is the next step in management? **[2 marks]**
**Q2b.** Describe the Young-Dees-Leadbetter bladder neck reconstruction technique. **[6 marks]**

─── STAGE 3: ADOLESCENCE ──────────────────────────────
At 15, he is socially continent (3-hour dry interval) but has recurrent UTIs and epididymitis. He asks about his genital appearance and sexual function.

**Q3a.** List the potential causes of recurrent UTIs and epididymitis in this context. **[3 marks]**
**Q3b.** Discuss the counselling points regarding sexual function, ejaculation, and fertility in men with bladder exstrophy. **[6 marks]**

─── STAGE 4: ADULTHOOD ────────────────────────────────
At 25, he is married and considering fertility.

**Q4a.** Discuss the fertility options for a man with bladder exstrophy. **[4 marks]**
**Q4b.** What are the implications for his offspring regarding risk of bladder exstrophy? **[3 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**FULL MARKING GUIDE:**

**Q1a.** [4 marks]
• Cover bladder with plastic wrap [1]
• No circumcision [1]
• Umbilical cord tie above bladder template [1]
• Photo documentation and referral to specialist centre [1]

**Q1b.** [5 marks]
• Primary bladder closure within 72 hours with pelvic osteotomy [1]
• Spica cast for 4-6 weeks [1]
• Epispadias repair (Cantwell-Ransley) at 6-12 months [1]
• Assess bladder capacity after closure and epispadias repair [1]
• Plan bladder neck reconstruction when capacity >100 mL (usually 4-5 years) [1]

**Q2a.** [2 marks]
• Bladder neck reconstruction (Young-Dees-Leadbetter) — indicated when bladder capacity >100 mL and child is ready for continence [2]

**Q2b.** [6 marks]
• Cystotomy through previous scar [1]
• Triangular mucosal strip from trigone to bladder neck [1]
• Narrowing of bladder neck by lateral incisions [1]
• Tubularisation of bladder neck and proximal urethra over 8Fr catheter [1]
• Reinforce with detrusor muscle wrap [1]
• Bilateral ureteric reimplantation (Cohen) if needed for VUR [1]

**Q3a.** [3 marks]
• VUR (common after BNR) [1]
• Bladder outlet obstruction / incomplete emptying [1]
• Posterior urethral diverticulum [1]

**Q3b.** [6 marks]
• Penile appearance: shorter and wider than normal; dorsal chordee may persist [1]
• Erections: usually normal; occasional dorsal chordee may require further release [1]
• Ejaculation: often normal; anejaculation or retrograde ejaculation possible [1]
• Fertility: generally preserved; may need TESE/ICSI [1]
• Paternity reported in ~50-60% of men with exstrophy [1]
• Sexual satisfaction: most men report satisfactory sexual relationships [1]

**Q4a.** [4 marks]
• Semen analysis first [1]
• If normal: natural conception possible [1]
• If obstructive azoospermia: surgical sperm retrieval (TESE/MESA) [1]
• ICSI for fertilisation [1]

**Q4b.** [3 marks]
• Genetic susceptibility: exstrophy-epispadias complex has a ~1% recurrence risk in offspring (higher than general population by 500x) [1]
• Not a simple Mendelian inheritance pattern [1]
• Offer genetic counselling for future family planning [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Provides a comprehensive lifespan overview from newborn to adult fertility
• Includes specific BNR technique and capacity threshold (>100 mL)
• Discusses sexual function and fertility — often neglected topics
• Recognises the recurrence risk counselling (1% vs background 0.02%)

## QUESTION 6 — EMQ: Exstrophy Spectrum [Total: 10 marks] [BOARD]

**Theme: Bladder Exstrophy-Epispadias Complex**

**OPTIONS (each option may be used once, more than once, or not at all):**

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| A. Classic bladder exstrophy | F. Duplicate exstrophy |
| B. Male epispadias (penopubic) | G. Cloacal exstrophy |
| C. Female epispadias | H. Superior vesical fissure |
| D. Covered exstrophy | I. Exstrophy variant |
| E. Cloacal exstrophy | J. Pseudexstrophy |

**For each of the following clinical scenarios, select the single most appropriate diagnosis:**

**1.** A newborn has an exposed bladder plate, epispadias, and a wide pubic diastasis. The anus is normally placed and there are no other anomalies. **[1 mark]**

**2.** A newborn has two exstrophic bladder halves separated by an exstrophic cecal plate, a blind-ending colon, a hindgut remnant opening onto the cecal plate, and an omphalocoele. **[1 mark]**

**3.** A newborn boy has a dorsally placed urethral meatus at the base of the glans with mild dorsal chordee. The bladder is not exposed. The pubic diastasis is 1 cm. **[1 mark]**

**4.** A newborn has a dense fibrous band replacing the lower abdominal wall and symphysis pubis. The bladder is not exposed but the umbilicus is low-lying. The bladder neck is wide. **[1 mark]**

**5.** A newborn girl has a bifid clitoris and a short urethra that opens onto an exposed area between the pubic rami. The bladder is intact. **[1 mark]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**ANSWERS & RATIONALE:**

**1.** **A** — Classic bladder exstrophy. The triad of exposed bladder, epispadias, and pubic diastasis with a normally placed anus is the definition. No other organ involvement.

**2.** **E** — Cloacal exstrophy (also known as OEIS). More severe than classic: two hemi-bladders, exstrophic cecum, blind-ending colon, omphalocele, and often spinal anomalies. This is the most severe end of the spectrum.

**3.** **B** — Male epispadias (penopubic). Isolated epispadias without bladder exstrophy. Milder form of the exstrophy-epispadias complex. The dorsal meatus and chordee are characteristic.

**4.** **D** — Covered exstrophy. A variant where the bladder has closed but the abdominal wall defect is represented by a fibrous band. Pubic diastasis is present.

**5.** **C** — Female epispadias. In females, epispadias presents as a bifid clitoris with a short, patulous urethra that opens onto exposed tissue between the pubic rami. Incontinence is present.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Confusing cloacal exstrophy with classic bladder exstrophy — the presence of a midline intestinal plate and omphalocoele distinguish cloacal exstrophy
• Not recognising that epispadias is part of the exstrophy spectrum and exists in isolation
• Female epispadias is frequently missed because it is rare and the signs are subtle
