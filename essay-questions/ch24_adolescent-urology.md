## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Transition of Care [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 17-year-old male with a history of posterior urethral valves (abated in infancy, valve bladder syndrome managed with CIC) is approaching transition from paediatric to adult urology. He has CKD stage 3b (eGFR 40 mL/min/1.73 m²), uses CIC 4 times daily, and has erectile dysfunction concerns.

**(a)** Outline the components of a structured transition programme from paediatric to adult urology services. **[6 marks]**

**(b)** Discuss the barriers to successful transition in adolescents with congenital urological conditions. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Explain the pathophysiology of erectile dysfunction in this patient and the management options. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss his fertility potential and the options available. **[6 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [6 marks]
• Named transition coordinator [1]
• Joint paediatric-adult clinics for 1-2 years [1]
• Patient-held record with summary of condition [1]
• Education: disease understanding, self-catheterisation independence, medication compliance [1]
• Sexual health and fertility counselling [1]
• Psychosocial support: body image, independence, relationships [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Loss to follow-up: ~40% of adolescents disengage during transition [1]
• Emotional immaturity / denial of chronic condition [1]
• Lack of adult clinicians experienced in congenital urology [1]
• Poor handover of notes and surgical history [1]
• Cultural: adolescent resistance to "starting over" with new team [1]

**(c)** [5 marks]
• Neurogenic injury: valve ablation near verumontanum may damage nerves for erection [1]
• Vascular: CKD-associated endothelial dysfunction [1]
• Hormonal: CKD → hypogonadism (low testosterone) [1]
• Psychological: anxiety, depression from chronic illness [1]
• Management: PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil/tadalafil), testosterone if deficient, psychosexual counselling [1]

**(d)** [6 marks]
• PUV patients often have normal spermatogenesis (testes are normal) [1]
• Ejaculation may be affected: retrograde ejaculation from bladder neck dysfunction or anejaculation [1]
• Assessment: semen analysis, post-ejaculatory urine (for retrograde) [1]
• Options: α-agonists (pseudoephedrine) for retrograde ejaculation → antegrade [1]
• Sperm retrieval from urine or TESE [1]
• ICSI for fertilisation [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
Transition requires a structured programme with a coordinator, joint clinics, and education. This adolescent faces loss to follow-up (40% risk), erectile dysfunction (neurovascular + CKD-related + psychological), and fertility concerns. PDE5 inhibitors and testosterone replacement are options for ED. Fertility assessment involves semen analysis and post-ejaculatory urine; treatment with α-agonists or TESE/ICSI. A named transition coordinator is essential.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Quotes the 40% loss-to-follow-up rate in transition
• Addresses erectile dysfunction from multiple aetiologies
• Includes α-agonists for retrograde ejaculation (often forgotten)
• Prescribes specific investigation algorithm for fertility

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Sexual Function After Hypospadias [Total: 20 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 19-year-old man who had TIP repair for distal hypospadias in childhood now presents with concerns about penile curvature during erection, a weak stream, and ejaculatory difficulty. He is sexually active but reports that his partner feels discomfort during intercourse.

**(a)** What are the potential long-term complications of childhood hypospadias repair? **[4 marks]**

**(b)** Discuss the assessment of this patient, including investigations. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Outline the management options for residual penile curvature (chordee) in an adult. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the psychological impact of hypospadias on adult sexual function and body image. **[6 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [4 marks] — 1 per valid point:
• Persistent chordee (10-15%) [1]
• Urethrocutaneous fistula (5-10%) [1]
• Meatal stenosis (5-10%) [1]
• Ejaculatory difficulty (6-15%) — due to urethral stricture or pouch [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• History: chordee direction and severity, stream assessment, ejaculation [1]
• Physical exam: meatal position, urethral palpation, curvature with artificial erection [1]
• Uroflowmetry + post-void residual [1]
• Retrograde urethrogram (for stricture/fistula) [1]
• Intracavernosal injection test to assess curvature objectively [1]

**(c)** [5 marks]
• <30° curvature: consider observation or plication (Nesbit/Baskin technique) [1]
• >30°: surgical correction — Nesbit plication (shortens convex side) or tunical plication (16-dot) [1]
• Shortening techniques: safe and effective but cause penile shortening [1]
• Grafting techniques: for complex or severe chordee + short phallus [1]
• Urethroplasty revision: if stricture or fistula contributes to symptoms [1]

**(d)** [6 marks]
• Studies show men with hypospadias are more likely to report: lower sexual satisfaction, avoidance of sexual situations, anxiety about appearance [1]
• Body image dissatisfaction: 20-40% of adults [1]
• Lower urinary tract symptoms add to quality-of-life burden [1]
• Psychological support: referral to psychosexual counselling [1]
• Partner involvement in counselling helpful [1]
• Outcomes improve after corrective surgery — but expectations must be managed [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
Long-term hypospadias complications include chordee, fistula, meatal stenosis, and ejaculatory problems. Assessment requires careful history, physical exam with artificial erection, uroflowmetry, and imaging. For residual chordee >30°, surgical correction (Nesbit plication) is indicated. Psychological impact is significant — body image dissatisfaction affects 20-40%, and psychosexual counselling should be offered.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Quotes specific complication percentages (chordee 10-15%, fistula 5-10%)
• Describes artificial erection test as part of assessment
• Discusses psychological impact and body image
• Recommends partner involvement in counselling

## QUESTION 3 — SAQ: Testicular Prosthesis [Total: 8 marks] [FOUNDATION]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** What are the indications for testicular prosthesis insertion? **[4 marks]**

**(b)** Discuss the timing, technique, complications, and counselling required. **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [4 marks]
• Anorchia (congenital or post-orchidectomy) [1]
• Testicular atrophy after torsion [1]
• Cryptorchidism with orchiectomy [1]
• Patient request for cosmetic reasons [1]

**(b)** [4 marks]
• Timing: deferred until adolescence or after growth complete [1]
• Technique: scrotal incision, dartos pouch [1]
• Complications: infection (1-2%), extrusion, migration, capsule contraction [1]
• Counselling: size options, feel different from native testis, infection risk with future catheterisation [1]

## QUESTION 4 — SAQ: Nephrectomy Counselling [Total: 8 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 20-year-old man with a solitary kidney (contralateral nephrectomy for Wilms at age 3) wants to play rugby and start a family.

**(a)** What advice do you give about contact sports? **[3 marks]**

**(b)** What advice do you give about fertility, fatherhood, and long-term health? **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [3 marks]
• No absolute contraindication to contact sports [1]
• Educate about kidney location and risk of injury [1]
• Wear protective padding; consider avoiding professional-level rugby [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Fertility is usually normal (one testis normally placed) [1]
• eGFR ~70-80% of two-kidney function — should be stable [1]
• Annual BP check, urinalysis, eGFR [1]
• Lifestyle: avoid smoking, maintain healthy weight, avoid NSAIDs [1]
• Offspring: no increased risk of Wilms unless genetic syndrome (WT1) — offer genetic counselling if applicable [1]

• Transition is a high-yield topic — all candidates should have a structured programme
• Sexual function and fertility are often neglected in children's answers — they are core to adolescent urology
• Psychosexual morbidity is as important as technical outcomes
