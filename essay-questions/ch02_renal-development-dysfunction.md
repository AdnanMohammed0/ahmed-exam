## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Neonatal Renal Physiology

**Total: 20 marks | FOUNDATION | Time allowed: ~25 minutes**

### CLINICAL SCENARIO

A 2-day-old term neonate, born at 38 weeks' gestation weighing 3.2 kg, develops hyponatraemia (Na 128 mmol/L) on day 3 of life. Urine output over the preceding 24 hours was 1.5 mL/kg/hour. The infant is receiving intravenous fluids at 100 mL/kg/day of 0.45% NaCl with 10% dextrose. The paediatric team is concerned about fluid balance in the context of immature renal function.

### Question Parts

**(a)** Explain how glomerular filtration rate (GFR) changes from fetal life through the neonatal period, including the factors that limit GFR in the newborn. **[6 marks]**

**(b)** Describe the physiological basis for the impaired urine concentrating ability of the neonatal kidney and its clinical consequences. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Outline the normal changes in water and sodium homeostasis that occur in the first week of life, explaining why this infant is at risk of hyponatraemia. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** State the approximate daily sodium and potassium requirements in a term neonate, and explain why preterm infants have higher sodium requirements. **[4 marks]**

---

### EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE

**(a) [6 marks]**

- Nephrogenesis begins at 5 weeks, is complete by 34–36 weeks; no new nephrons after birth [1]
- GFR at term: ~13 mL/min/1.73 m²; in premature infants: 3.5–6 mL/min/1.73 m² depending on birth weight [1]
- Postnatal GFR doubles by 2 weeks, triples by 4 weeks [1]
- Adult levels reached by 1–2 years [1]
- Factors limiting GFR: low renal blood flow (2.5–4% of cardiac output at birth vs 20–25% in adults) [1]
- Small glomerular surface area, immature glomerular basement membrane [1]
- Max 6 marks

**(b) [5 marks]**

- Maximal urine concentrating ability in neonates: 600–800 mOsm/kg (vs adult 1200+) [1]
- Reduced responsiveness of neonatal kidney to ADH (vasopressin) [1]
- Inability to maintain corticomedullary osmotic gradient due to short tubules [1]
- Neonates produce large volumes of dilute urine: 2–3 mL/kg/hour [1]
- Clinical consequence: easily predisposed to dehydration; limited reserve to handle stress [1]

**(c) [5 marks]**

- At birth, ~75% of body weight is water; ECF ~40% of body weight [1]
- First 24–48 hours: initial oliguria followed by isotonic volume contraction [1]
- Normal 10% loss of birth weight is physiological [1]
- ANP release from increased pulmonary venous return promotes diuresis/natriuresis [1]
- Excessive sodium/water administration before this diuresis causes dilutional hyponatraemia [1]

**(d) [4 marks]**

- Term neonate: sodium 2–3 mmol/kg/day in first week, rising to 3–5 mmol/kg/day after week 1 [1]
- Potassium: 1–2 mmol/kg/day in first week, rising to 2–3 mmol/kg/day thereafter [1]
- Preterm infants have 2–3 times higher Na requirement [1]
- Reason: reduced tubular reabsorption of sodium, additional Na needed for growth [1]

### COMMON ERRORS

- Stating that serum creatinine in the first 48 hours reflects neonatal (rather than maternal) renal function
- Not recognising that the low GFR is normal physiology, not pathology
- Confusing the mechanisms of concentrating vs diluting ability

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Chronic Kidney Disease in Children

**Total: 25 marks | BOARD | Time allowed: ~25 minutes**

### CLINICAL SCENARIO

A 6-year-old boy is referred with poor growth (height below the 3rd centile), polyuria, and polydipsia. He was born at 34 weeks and had a history of bilateral SFU grade 3 hydronephrosis detected antenatally. His serum creatinine is 180 µmol/L, and estimated GFR (Schwartz formula) is 38 mL/min/1.73 m². Blood pressure is 125/80 mmHg (>95th centile for age).

### Question Parts

**(a)** Define CKD staging according to KDIGO, and state this child's CKD stage. **[4 marks]**

**(b)** Outline the metabolic complications of CKD that are likely to be present in this child, and describe their management. **[8 marks]**

**(c)** Discuss the management of growth impairment in children with CKD, including the role of recombinant human growth hormone (rhGH). **[6 marks]**

**(d)** Explain why congenital anomalies of the kidney and urinary tract (CAKUT) are the leading cause of CKD in children, and list other important causes. **[7 marks]**

---

### EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE

**(a) [4 marks]**

- CKD defined as kidney damage >3 months duration [1]
- Stage 1: GFR >90; Stage 2: 60–89; Stage 3a: 45–59; Stage 3b: 30–44; Stage 4: 15–29; Stage 5: <15 [1]
- This child: GFR 38 → Stage 3b [1]
- CKD severity correlates with morbidity/mortality outcomes [1]

**(b) [8 marks]**

**CKD-MBD (renal osteodystrophy):**
- Reduced 1α-hydroxylase → low calcitriol → hypocalcaemia, hyperphosphataemia → secondary hyperparathyroidism [1]
- Management: dietary phosphate restriction, phosphate binders (calcium acetate, sevelamer), alfacalcidol/calcitriol, cinacalcet for refractory cases [1]

**Anaemia:**
- Decreased erythropoietin production; prevalence 73% stage 3, 87% stage 4, >93% stage 5 [1]
- Management: rHuEPO (subcutaneous/IV), iron supplementation (oral/IV), target Hb not >13 g/dL [1]

**Metabolic acidosis:**
- Impaired acid excretion, lower bicarbonate threshold (18–20 mmol/L in neonates) [1]
- Use of sodium bicarbonate to maintain HCO₃ >17 mmol/L — avoid prolonged acidosis (poor feeding, growth impairment) [1]

**Hyperkalaemia:**
- Mainly when GFR <10% of normal [0.5]
- Dietary restriction, calcium resonium/sodium resonium, avoid ACEi if contributing [0.5]

**(c) [6 marks]**

- 35% of children with CKD have height <3rd centile (HtSDS < –1.88) [1]
- Contributing factors: CKD-MBD, polyuria, metabolic acidosis, poor nutrition, anaemia, fluid/electrolyte disorders [1]
- Uraemia causes anorexia/vomiting → reduced caloric intake [1]
- NG/gastrostomy feeding may be needed to maximise caloric intake [1]
- rhGH indicated if height/growth velocity remains low despite optimal nutrition, correction of acidosis, and minimal steroid use [1]
- Protein intake: maintained at 100% RDI for stages 4–5, 100% RDI for dialysis patients [1]
- Max 6 marks

**(d) [7 marks]**

- CAKUT accounts for 49.1% of paediatric CKD [1]
- Spectrum includes: renal agenesis, renal hypoplasia/dysplasia, PUJ obstruction, VUR, PUV [1]
- Higher incidence in males; more common in African Americans and Australian aboriginals [1]
- Prematurity increasingly recognised as risk factor [1]
- Other causes: steroid-resistant nephrotic syndrome (10.4%) [1]
- Chronic glomerulonephritis: lupus nephritis, HSP nephritis, Alport syndrome (8.1%) [1]
- Renal ciliopathies: nephronophthisis (5.3%); rarer: HUS, Wilms tumour [1]
- Max 7 marks

### DISTINGUISHING FEATURES OF AN EXCELLENT ANSWER

- Quoting specific percentages for CAKUT (49.1%) and other aetiologies
- Mentioning the Schwartz formula and its limitations
- Discussing the potential role of premature birth and low nephron number
- Mentioning that GFR decline typically accelerates at puberty

## SAQ 1 — Neonatal Fluid and Electrolyte Management

**Total: 8 marks | FOUNDATION**

**(a)** List three factors that affect the interpretation of serum creatinine as a marker of renal function in neonates. **[3 marks]**

**(b)** State the daily maintenance fluid and electrolyte requirements for a term neonate in the first week of life for: water, sodium, and potassium. **[3 marks]**

**(c)** Explain why preterm infants are at higher risk of hyperkalaemia in the first weeks of life. **[2 marks]**

### MARKING GUIDE

**(a) [3 marks]**

- Maternal creatinine crosses the placenta → neonatal creatinine reflects maternal levels in first 48 hours [1]
- Variable proximal tubular reabsorption of creatinine in immature kidneys [1]
- Creatinine production depends on muscle mass — low in neonates [1]

**(b) [3 marks]**

- Water: ~90–100 mL/kg/day (adjusted for urine output and insensible losses) [1]
- Sodium: 2–3 mmol/kg/day [1]
- Potassium: 1–2 mmol/kg/day [1]

**(c) [2 marks]**

- Reduced renal clearance of potassium (even when corrected for lower GFR) [1]
- Increased intestinal absorption + catabolic state + hypoxia/metabolic acidosis → elevated serum K [1]

### COMMON ERRORS

- Prescribing adult-type fluid volumes for neonates without adjusting for higher body water content
- Using serum creatinine as sole marker of renal function in the first 48 hours of life

### FULL MARKING GUIDE FOR LCV 2

**Q1a. [5 marks]**

**Q1b. [5 marks]**

- Decline in GFR is more rapid at puberty (increasing muscle mass, hyperfiltration injury) [1]

**Q2b. [6 marks]**

**Q3a. [5 marks]**
- Induction: steroids + basiliximab (anti-CD25 monoclonal antibody) or anti-thymocyte globulin [1]

**Q3b. [5 marks]**

- T-cell mediated: diagnosed by renal biopsy (tubulitis, interstitial infiltrate); treated with pulse methylprednisolone ± ATG [1]
- Antibody-mediated: diagnosed by C4d staining on biopsy + donor-specific antibodies (DSA) in blood [1]

- DSA monitoring helps detect subclinical ABMR [1]

### Stems

For each clinical scenario, select the single most appropriate method of assessing renal function:

1. A 2-year-old boy with spina bifida, poor muscle bulk, and a creatinine of 45 µmol/L in whom you suspect the creatinine may not reflect true GFR.

2. A 7-year-old girl undergoing research assessment in a clinical trial who requires the gold standard measurement of GFR.

3. A 5-year-old boy with CKD stage 4 in whom you need an estimate of GFR that accounts for his height and avoids invasive collections.

4. A 15-year-old with a newly diagnosed Wilms tumour who needs assessment of differential renal function to plan nephron-sparing surgery.

5. A 10-year-old girl with recurrent UTIs and suspected PUJ obstruction who needs assessment of both drainage AND differential function.

### ANSWERS & RATIONALE

1. **B** — Cystatin C is a small protein produced from all nucleated cells, freely filtered by the glomeruli, and not affected by muscle mass. Ideal for children with low muscle bulk in whom serum creatinine is unreliable.

2. **C** — Inulin clearance is the gold standard measurement of GFR. It requires an IV bolus and continuous infusion with serum and urine measurements, making it impractical for routine clinical use but ideal for research.

3. **A** — The modified Schwartz formula (eGFR = 36.5 × height (cm) / serum creatinine (µmol/L)) is the most widely used clinical tool. It is simple, non-invasive, and validated for children, although it lacks validation for GFR between 15–75 mL/min/1.73 m².

4. **H** — DMSA provides static images of functioning renal tissue and quantifies differential function. It is ideal for surgical planning to determine how much functioning tissue can be preserved. Normal differential function is 45–55%.

5. **I** — MAG3 renography provides dynamic information: differential function AND drainage curves (O'Reilly classification). It is the investigation of choice for assessing possible PUJ obstruction.

### COMMON ERRORS

- Confusing static (DMSA) with dynamic (MAG3) renography
- Not recognising that creatinine-based estimates are unreliable in low-muscle-mass children
- Recommending inulin clearance for routine clinical use (impractical)
