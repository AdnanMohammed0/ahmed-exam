## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Ultrasound in Paediatric Urology

**Total: 22 marks | FOUNDATION | Time allowed: ~25 minutes**

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 4-week-old male infant is referred following antenatal detection of bilateral hydronephrosis. Postnatal ultrasound shows an AP renal pelvic diameter of 14 mm on the right and 10 mm on the left, with mild calyceal dilatation bilaterally. The bladder wall is not thickened.

**(a)** Describe the normal ultrasound appearances of the paediatric kidney, including normal measurements and echogenicity. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Outline how bladder assessment on ultrasound can provide clues to underlying pathology. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Explain the limitations of ultrasound in the detection of vesicoureteric reflux and renal scarring. **[6 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the role of contrast-enhanced ultrasound (CEUS) in paediatric urology. **[6 marks]**

---

### MARKING GUIDE

**(a) [5 marks]**
- Normal AP renal pelvis diameter: <7 mm normal; >10 mm abnormal [1]
- Normal renal length from age-standardised charts (5th–95th centile) [1]
- Normal parenchyma less echogenic than adjacent liver/spleen [1]
- Corticomedullary differentiation: pyramids slightly lower echogenicity than cortex [1]
- In end-stage CKD: kidneys are echo-bright with loss of corticomedullary differentiation [1]

**(b) [5 marks]**
- Bladder wall thickness: up to 3 mm is normal [1]
- Post-void residual >10% of bladder capacity is abnormal [1]
- Bladder wall thickening suggests outflow obstruction (PUV) or neuropathic bladder [1]
- Trabeculation/sacculation indicates high-pressure voiding [1]
- Ureterocele visible as cystic structure at bladder base when bladder is full [1]

**(c) [6 marks]**
- US is unreliable for detecting VUR — requires MCUG or CEUS [1]
- False-negative rate of 25–50% for grade III VUR [1]
- Poor sensitivity for renal scarring: misses 15–45% of cases [1]
- Operator dependent [1]
- Cannot assess differential renal function [1]
- Nondilated ureters are not visualised [1]

**(d) [6 marks]**
- Microbubble contrast medium (IV or intravesical) [1]
- IV: assesses renal perfusion, tumour vascularity, traumatic haemorrhage [1]
- Intravesical: dynamic assessment of VUR, multiple filling cycles possible [1]
- Can visualise urethra during voiding → detect PUV [1]
- Useful for ambiguous genitalia and cloacal malformations [1]
- Requires high-quality scanner and trained operator [1]

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Dynamic Renography

**Total: 22 marks | BOARD | Time allowed: ~25 minutes**

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 6-month-old girl with antenatally detected left hydronephrosis (AP pelvic diameter 22 mm, calyceal dilatation) undergoes a MAG3 renogram. The differential function is 38% on the left and 62% on the right. The drainage curve shows a rising pattern with delayed response to furosemide.

**(a)** Explain the principles of ⁹⁹ᵐTc-MAG3 renography and how it differs from DTPA. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Draw and label the five O'Reilly drainage curve patterns. **[6 marks]**

**(c)** Interpret the findings in this infant and discuss how they guide management decisions. **[6 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the limitations of renography in children under 2 years of age. **[5 marks]**

---

### MARKING GUIDE

**(a) [5 marks]**
- MAG3 relies on tubular extraction (better extraction efficiency) [1]
- DTPA relies on glomerular filtration (declining use) [1]
- Dynamic renography provides serial gamma camera images [1]
- Generates time-activity (renogram) curves for uptake and clearance [1]
- Differential function calculated at 1–2 minutes post-injection [1]

**(b) [6 marks]**
- Type 1: Normal uptake with prompt washout (normal) [1]
- Type 2: Rising curve, no response to diuretic (obstructed) [1]
- Type 3a: Falls rapidly with diuretic (non-obstructive dilatation) [1]
- Type 3b: Neither falls promptly nor continues to rise (equivocal) [1]
- Type 4: Secondary peak (Homsy's sign) — obstruction precipitated by high flow [1]
- Each drawn correctly (verbal description accepted) [1]

**(c) [6 marks]**
- Left differential function 38% — borderline reduced [1]
- Rising drainage curve suggests impaired drainage; equivocal response to diuretic [1]
- AP diameter 22 mm + calyceal dilatation = SFU grade III–IV [1]
- Consideration: pyeloplasty indicated if function <40% ± worsening drainage [1]
- If asymptomatic and stable → conservative management with serial US and renography [1]
- Deteriorating function or increasing dilatation → pyeloplasty [1]

**(d) [5 marks]**
- Immature renal function in infants <6 months limits reliability [1]
- Reduced GFR → prolonged tracer uptake → poor quality curves [1]
- Full bladder can confound drainage assessment (catheterise if in doubt) [1]
- VUR can cause artefactual "obstructed" pattern (catheterise to exclude) [1]
- Not reliable in dehydrated or poorly hydrated children [1]

- Detecting renal scarring (≥8 weeks post-UTI) [1]

## EMQ 3 — Theme: Imaging Modality Selection

**OPTIONS:**
A. Renal ultrasound (US)
B. MCUG (voiding cystourethrography)
C. DMSA static scintigraphy
D. MAG3 dynamic renography
E. Contrast-enhanced ultrasound (CEUS)
F. MR urography (MRU)
G. CT abdomen/pelvis with contrast
H. Intravenous urography (IVU)
I. Abdominal X-ray (AXR)
J. Retrograde pyelography

**Stems:**
1. An 8-year-old girl with a history of recurrent UTIs has a suspected renal scar. She had a proven febrile UTI 3 months ago. Which investigation is most appropriate to confirm scarring?

2. A 2-week-old male infant with antenatal bilateral hydronephrosis needs urgent assessment for possible posterior urethral valves. Which investigation is diagnostic?

3. A 12-year-old boy presents with acute-onset right loin pain and haematuria. US shows mild hydronephrosis but no definite stone. Which imaging modality is next indicated?

4. A 5-year-old girl with continuous dribbling incontinence is suspected of having a duplex ectopic ureter. US shows a possible upper pole moiety. Which investigation provides the best anatomical delineation?

5. A 3-year-old involved in a high-velocity MVC has gross haematuria and a fall in Hb. Which investigation is the gold standard for staging renal trauma?

**ANSWERS & RATIONALE:**
1. **C** — DMSA is the most sensitive test for detecting renal scarring when performed ≥8 weeks after the acute infection. US has 15–45% false-negative rate for scarring.

2. **B** — MCUG is the only investigation that can definitively diagnose PUV by demonstrating the dilated posterior urethra with an abrupt change in calibre at the valve leaflet.

3. **G** — CT (non-contrast/low-dose stone protocol) is the gold standard for detecting ureteric calculi in children when US is equivocal, with >96% sensitivity.

4. **F** — MR urography provides excellent anatomical delineation of duplication anomalies, ectopic ureters, and poorly functioning upper pole moieties without ionising radiation.

5. **G** — CT with contrast (including delayed images) is the cornerstone for staging renal trauma according to the AAST organ injury scale. It detects lacerations, extravasation, and vascular injury.
