## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Initial Assessment of Anorectal Malformation [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A term male newborn is found to have no anal opening on routine neonatal examination. He has passed meconium through a small perineal opening at the base of the scrotum. He has a patent urethra and is passing urine. Abdominal examination reveals no distension. There is a unilateral right undescended testis.

**(a)** Classify this anorectal malformation using the Peña classification and describe the expected fistula type in a male infant. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** List the VACTERL-associated anomalies you must screen for and state the investigations needed for each. **[6 marks]**

**(c)** Describe the initial surgical management (colostomy), including the recommended timing, type, and site of stoma. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** Outline the urologic evaluation required before definitive repair and the rationale for each component. **[6 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [5 marks] — 1 per valid point up to 5:
• Rectoperineal fistula (low ARM) — perineal opening at base of scrotum [1]
• Peña classification: male ARM with rectoperineal fistula is a "low" type [1]
• Alternative: rectobulbar urethral fistula (if opening into posterior urethra) — but perineal meconium suggests perineal fistula [1]
• Distinguish from "high" ARM (rectobladder neck fistula) where no perineal opening exists [1]
• An undescended testis is part of the ARM association [1]

**(b)** [6 marks]
• V = Vertebral: spinal US/X-ray for sacral anomalies, tethered cord [1]
• A = Anorectal: diagnosed [1]
• C = Cardiac: echocardiogram (VSD, tetralogy of Fallot) [1]
• TE = Tracheoesophageal: consider if history of polyhydramnios, difficulty feeding; chest X-ray for TEF [1]
• R = Renal: renal US (unilateral renal agenesis, VUR, dysplasia) [1]
• L = Limb: examine for radial anomalies, thumb hypoplasia, lower limb defects [1]

**(c)** [5 marks]
• Timing: within 24-48 hours of birth, after initial resuscitation and screening [1]
• Type: divided sigmoid colostomy (two separate stomas: proximal end colostomy, distal mucous fistula) [1]
• Site: left iliac fossa — allows adequate length of distal colon for future pull-through [1]
• Divided stoma: prevents faecal stream from reaching distal segment and entering the urinary tract [1]
• Alternative: loop colostomy (less commonly used — allows faecal contamination of distal segment) [1]

**(d)** [6 marks]
• Renal US: screen for renal agenesis (most common), hydronephrosis, dysplasia [1]
• MCU: rule out VUR and assess bladder function (neuropathic bladder from sacral anomaly) [1]
• DMSA: baseline for function if renal abnormality found [1]
• Spinal US/MRI: screen for tethered cord, sacral dysplasia [1]
• Urodynamics: assess bladder function — especially if sacral anomaly present [1]
• Distal colostogram: contrast study through mucous fistula to define fistula anatomy before definitive repair [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This term male has a low ARM — rectoperineal fistula. The VACTERL screening protocol must be completed: spinal US for sacral/cord anomalies, echocardiogram, renal US and MCU, and examination for limb anomalies. A divided sigmoid colostomy in the left iliac fossa should be performed within 24-48 hours. Urologic evaluation (US, MCU, spinal imaging, and urodynamics) is essential as 30-50% of ARM patients have associated urologic anomalies, and sacral dysplasia predicts neuropathic bladder.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Distinguishes types of colostomy (divided vs loop) and rationale
• Specifies distal colostogram as essential pre-operative investigation
• Orders investigations in sequence (screening → definitive planning)
• Recognises that the undescended testis is part of the ARM association

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Urologic Surveillance in ARM [Total: 20 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 4-year-old girl with a history of ARM (rectovestibular fistula) repaired at birth now presents with recurrent febrile UTIs. She had a previous MCU at birth showing grade II left VUR, managed conservatively. She now has daytime wetting and constipation. Renal US shows moderate left hydronephrosis.

**(a)** Explain the association between ARM and neuropathic bladder, including the role of sacral agenesis. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Outline the urological surveillance protocol for a child with ARM through childhood. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Discuss the relationship between chronic constipation (common after ARM repair) and urinary tract dysfunction. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** Describe the management of the tethered cord syndrome in a child with ARM and recurrent UTIs. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [5 marks] — 1 per valid point up to 5:
• ARM is associated with sacral agenesis/dysgenesis in 30-50% [1]
• Sacral innervation (S2-4) controls bladder and external sphincter [1]
• Absent/reduced sacral segments → neurogenic bladder [1]
• Pattern: acontractile bladder with poor emptying or detrusor overactivity with high pressures [1]
• Risk of VUR, UTIs, and renal deterioration if unrecognised [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Renal US at birth, then annually if abnormalities detected [1]
• MCU at birth (baseline for VUR) [1]
• DMSA at 6-12 months (baseline for function/scars) [1]
• Urodynamics at 3-6 months (if sacral anomaly) or if developing UTIs/incontinence [1]
• Annual BP measurement [1]

**(c)** [5 marks]
• ARM repair often leaves a narrow anal canal and impaired rectal sensation → chronic constipation/fecal soiling [1]
• Rectal distension from stool → compression of bladder neck → impaired bladder emptying [1]
• Faecal loading → detrusor overactivity → urgency/incontinence [1]
• Constipation → increased residual urine → UTI risk [1]
• Management: aggressive bowel regimen (laxatives, enemas, bowel management programme) [1]

**(d)** [5 marks]
• Tethered cord: spinal cord attached to spinal canal by filum terminale or scar [1]
• Diagnosis: spinal US (<6 months) or MRI (older children) [1]
• Symptoms: deteriorating continence, gait changes, leg weakness, foot deformities, new VUR [1]
• Treatment: neurosurgical release of tethered cord [1]
• Timing: early intervention may reverse bladder dysfunction; delayed release may only halt progression [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This child has the classic triad: ARM + sacral anomaly + neuropathic bladder. The recurrent UTIs likely reflect a combination of VUR, bladder dysfunction from the sacral anomaly, and chronic constipation from the ARM repair. The urologic surveillance protocol must include annual US, repeat MCU to reassess VUR, and urodynamics to detect neuropathic bladder. Tethered cord should be excluded by MRI if she has any neurological deterioration. Bowel management is critical — constipation must be aggressively treated to improve bladder emptying.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Links sacral agenesis to specific bladder dysfunction pattern
• Describes the urological surveillance protocol with specific timings
• Recognises tethered cord as a potentially reversible cause of deterioration
• Highlights bowel management as core to urinary health in ARM

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A newborn girl has a single perineal opening. US shows hydrometrocolpos and a pelvic cystic mass.

**(a)** What is the most likely diagnosis? **[1 mark]**

**(b)** Describe the steps of the initial assessment and colostomy in a newborn with cloaca. **[4 marks]**

**(c)** List the three components of the common channel in a cloacal anomaly. **[3 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

**(a)** [1 mark]
• Persistent cloaca

**(b)** [4 marks]

• US/MRI: assess for hydrometrocolpos, renal anomalies [1]
• Colostomy: divided descending colostomy [1]

• Vagina [1]

## QUESTION 5 — LCV: Definitive Repair and Complications [Total: 35 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~40 minutes

─── STAGE 1: PRE-OPERATIVE ASSESSMENT ────────────────────
A 6-month-old male infant with ARM and rectobulbar urethral fistula (sigmoid colostomy at birth) is being prepared for definitive repair (PSARP).

**Q1a.** What pre-operative investigations are required before PSARP? **[4 marks]**
**Q1b.** Describe the PSARP (posterior sagittal anorectoplasty) approach. **[6 marks]**

─── STAGE 2: SURGICAL DETAILS ──────────────────────────
During PSARP, the fistula to the posterior urethra is identified and divided.

**Q2a.** Describe the steps of fistula takedown and rectal mobilisation. **[5 marks]**
**Q2b.** What is the role of the muscle stimulator during PSARP? **[3 marks]**

─── STAGE 3: POST-OPERATIVE COMPLICATIONS ───────────────
At 3 years, the child has constipation, soiling, and recurrent UTIs. He has difficulty passing urine with a poor stream.

**Q3a.** What urologic complications should you consider? **[4 marks]**
**Q3b.** What investigations would you arrange? **[4 marks]**

─── STAGE 4: LONG-TERM OUTCOME ─────────────────────────
Urodynamics shows acontractile bladder. MCU shows no VUR. Spinal MRI shows tethered cord.

**Q4a.** Discuss the management of the tethered cord and its expected effect on bladder function. **[5 marks]**
**Q4b.** Outline the long-term continence and bowel management programme for this child. **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**FULL MARKING GUIDE:**

**Q1a.** [4 marks]
• Distal colostogram (contrast through mucous fistula) — defines fistula anatomy and rectal pouch position [1]
• Renal US — screen for anomalies [1]
• Spinal US — assess sacrum and cord [1]
• Examination under anaesthesia ± cystoscopy — visualise fistula and urethral anatomy [1]

**Q1b.** [6 marks]
• Prone position with pelvis elevated [1]
• Midline incision from coccyx to perineal body [1]
• Muscle stimulator to identify and split the parasagittal fibres and striated sphincter complex [1]
• Identify rectum and dissect circumferentially to the fistula [1]
• Divide fistula from urethra; close urethral defect with absorbable suture [1]
• Place rectum within sphincter complex; close posterior sagittal incision [1]

**Q2a.** [5 marks]
• Identify fistula entering posterior urethra (bulbar region) [1]
• Circumferential dissection of rectum to fistula [1]
• Place stay sutures around fistula [1]
• Divide fistula sharply, leaving a 2-3 mm cuff on urethra [1]
• Close urethral defect with interrupted 6-0 absorbable suture [1]

**Q2b.** [3 marks]
• Electrical muscle stimulator identifies striated sphincter complex [1]
• Allows precise midline division of muscle complex → preserves innervation [1]
• Ensures rectum is placed centrally within the sphincter for optimal continence [1]

**Q3a.** [4 marks]
• Urethral stricture (from fistula closure) [1]
• Neuropathic bladder from tethered cord/sacral agenesis [1]
• Posterior urethral diverticulum from retained fistula [1]
• Persistent VUR [1]

**Q3b.** [4 marks]
• MCU for urethral stricture and VUR [1]
• Urodynamics for bladder function [1]
• Spinal MRI for tethered cord [1]
• Renal US for hydronephrosis [1]

**Q4a.** [5 marks]
• Neurosurgical release of tethered cord [1]
• Timing: as soon as detected — may reverse or halt deterioration [1]
• Effect on bladder: may improve emptying, reduce residual volumes [1]
• Unlikely to fully normalise function; CIC may still be needed [1]
• Post-release: repeat urodynamics to reassess [1]

**Q4b.** [4 marks]
• Bowel management programme: dietary fibre, osmotic laxatives, ± enemas [1]
• Antegrade continence enema (ACE/Malone) if bowel management fails [1]
• CIC for incomplete bladder emptying [1]
• Consider Mitrofanoff channel for independent catheterisation [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Describes distal colostogram as essential pre-PSARP investigation
• Details muscle stimulator role in sphincter identification
• Recognises tethered cord as a potentially treatable cause of bladder deterioration
• Discusses ACE and Mitrofanoff as surgical solutions for bowel and bladder continence

## QUESTION 6 — EMQ: ARM Classification [Total: 10 marks] [BOARD]

**Theme: Anorectal Malformation Types**

**OPTIONS (each option may be used once, more than once, or not at all):**

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| A. Rectoperineal fistula | F. Cloacal anomaly |
| B. Rectovestibular fistula | G. Anal stenosis |
| C. Rectobulbar urethral fistula | H. Covered anus |
| D. Rectoprostatic urethral fistula | I. Anterior ectopic anus |
| E. Rectobladder neck fistula | J. Persistent cloaca |

**For each of the following clinical scenarios, select the single most appropriate ARM type:**

**1.** A newborn girl with no anal opening. Meconium is seen at the vaginal fourchette but separately from the hymen. She has a normal urethra. **[1 mark]**

**2.** A newborn boy with no anal opening. He has meconium in his urine. There is no perineal opening. A catheter passes into the bladder, and the urine is green-tinged. **[1 mark]**

**3.** A newborn girl with a single perineal opening. She has a distended lower abdomen and US shows a fluid-filled vagina (hydrometrocolpos). **[1 mark]**

**4.** A newborn boy with no anal opening. Meconium is noted at the penoscrotal junction. He has a palpable rectal pouch on perineal examination. **[1 mark]**

**5.** A newborn boy with no anal opening. No meconium is seen. He has not passed urine in 24 hours. On attempting to catheterise, the catheter passes into the rectum. **[1 mark]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**ANSWERS & RATIONALE:**

**1.** **B** — Rectovestibular fistula. In girls, the fistula opens into the vestibule (between the hymen and the urethral meatus). This is distinct from a perineal fistula which opens outside the vestibule anterior to the anus. The separate urethral opening helps distinguish from cloaca.

**2.** **E** — Rectobladder neck fistula. Meconium in the urine indicates a fistula between the rectum and the bladder. This is the highest ARM in males (above the levator ani). No perineal opening means the rectum drains entirely into the bladder.

**3.** **J** — Persistent cloaca. A single perineal opening in a girl with hydrometrocolpos is diagnostic. The common channel receives the urinary, genital, and intestinal tracts. Hydrometrocolpos requires urgent drainage.

**4.** **A** — Rectoperineal fistula. In males, a perineal fistula at the penoscrotal junction with a palpable rectal pouch is a low ARM. The rectum has descended through the levator sling.

**5.** **D** — Rectoprostatic urethral fistula (can be E — depends on level). If the catheter passes from urethra into rectum, the communication is at the prostatic level. This is intermediate/high ARM with the fistula entering the prostatic urethra.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Confusing rectovestibular with rectoperineal fistula in girls
• Not recognising that a single perineal opening means cloaca until proven otherwise
• Assuming all boys with ARM have the same type — the level of fistula is critical for surgical planning

**EXAMINER'S NOTES — COMMON SCRIPT PATTERNS TO WATCH FOR:**
• Many candidates forget to screen for VACTERL in the newborn period — this is mandatory
• The classification of ARM is poorly recalled; Peña classification (high/intermediate/low) is the standard
• Urologic surveillance after ARM repair is often neglected in long-term follow-up plans
• Tethered cord is under-recognised as a cause of late bladder deterioration
