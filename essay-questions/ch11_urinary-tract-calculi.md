## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Evaluation of Urolithiasis in Children [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 7-year-old boy presents to the emergency department with acute-onset right flank pain radiating to the groin, associated with nausea and vomiting. Urine dipstick shows haematuria (+++). He has no fever. He has had one similar episode 6 months ago that resolved spontaneously. There is a family history of kidney stones in his father and paternal uncle.

**(a)** List the differential diagnoses for acute flank pain in a child and state how you would differentiate them. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** A non-contrast CT confirms a 7 mm right proximal ureteric calculus. Outline your management options for this stone, including the evidence for each. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** The stone passes spontaneously. Composition analysis shows calcium oxalate monohydrate. Describe the metabolic workup you would arrange for this child to identify the underlying cause. **[6 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the management of hypercalciuria in children, including dietary modifications, pharmacological options, and follow-up. **[6 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [5 marks] — 1 per valid point up to 5:
• Ureteric colic (renal calculus) — sudden onset, radiation to groin, haematuria, vomiting [1]
• UTI/pyelonephritis — fever, dysuria, loin tenderness [1]
• Testicular torsion (if boy) — testicular pain, absent cremasteric reflex [1]
• PUJ obstruction / Dietl's crisis — pain after fluid bolus [1]
• Appendicitis — right iliac fossa pain, fever, anorexia, migration of pain [1]
• CT KUB distinguishes between all of these definitively [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Medical expulsive therapy (MET): tamsulosin 0.4 mg daily for 4 weeks — improves stone passage (alpha-blocker relaxes ureteric smooth muscle) [1]
• Spontaneous passage: stone >5 mm less likely to pass; 7 mm has ~40-50% chance [1]
• ESWL: first-line for proximal ureteric stones <15 mm in children; success ~70-85% [1]
• Ureteroscopy (URS): if ESWL fails or stone impacted; success >90% [1]
• URS risk: ureteric injury, stricture; smaller instruments in children [1]

**(c)** [6 marks]
• 24-hour urine collection: volume, creatinine, calcium, oxalate, urate, citrate, sodium, magnesium [1]
• Spot urine calcium:creatinine ratio (>0.7 mmol/mmol = hypercalciuria) [1]
• Serum: Ca, phosphate, PTH, vitamin D, urate, bicarbonate [1]
• Stone analysis (FTIR spectroscopy) — already done: calcium oxalate monohydrate [1]
• Urine culture to exclude infection-related stones [1]
• Family screening: test parents/siblings for hypercalciuria [1]

**(d)** [6 marks]
• Dietary: increased fluid intake (target 2-3 L/day), reduced sodium, normal calcium intake (DO NOT restrict calcium in children — may worsen bone health) [1]
• Reduced oxalate-rich foods (spinach, chocolate, nuts) if hyperoxaluria [1]
• Pharmacological: thiazide diuretics (e.g., chlortalidone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day) reduce urinary calcium by 30-50% [1]
• Potassium citrate: increases urinary citrate (inhibits stone formation) [1]
• Indications for pharmacotherapy: recurrent stones, severe hypercalciuria, failed dietary measures [1]
• Follow-up: repeat 24-hour urine in 3-6 months, renal US annually, and BP monitoring [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This boy has ureteric colic from a ureteric calculus. With a 7 mm proximal ureteric stone, I would offer ESWL as first-line — it is non-invasive and effective for stones <15 mm. After the stone passes, a full metabolic workup is essential: 24-hour urine, serum calcium/PTH/vitamin D, stone analysis, and family screening. If hypercalciuria is found (likely given family history), I would start with increased fluid intake, normal dietary calcium, and reduced sodium. Thiazides may be added if dietary measures fail. All first-degree relatives should be screened for hypercalciuria.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Distinguishes calcium-restricted from normal-calcium diet (important in children)
• Quotes specific stone passage rates by size
• Recommends family screening beyond just the patient
• Provides specific MET dosing

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Stone Composition and Surgical Management [Total: 20 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 4-year-old girl from Southeast Asia presents with recurrent UTIs and a staghorn calculus filling the entire left renal pelvis and extending into all calyces. Urine culture grows Proteus mirabilis. Serum creatinine is 50 μmol/L.

**(a)** What is the most likely stone composition, and describe the pathophysiology of its formation. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** List the indications for surgical intervention and compare PCNL, ESWL, and open surgery for this stone burden in a 4-year-old child. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Describe the technique of PCNL in children, including positioning, access, tract size, and stone fragmentation. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the importance of complete stone clearance and the metabolic workup specific to infection-related stones. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [5 marks]
• Struvite (magnesium ammonium phosphate) — infection stone [1]
• Produced by urease-producing bacteria (Proteus, Klebsiella, Pseudomonas) [1]
• Urease splits urea → ammonia → alkaline urine → precipitation of MgNH₄PO₄ [1-2]
• Staghorn morphology: fills renal pelvis and extends into calyces [1]
• More common in females and in developing countries [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Indications: staghorn calculus requires active treatment (will not pass, causes obstruction/UTI/parenchymal damage) [1]
• PCNL: gold standard — single-stage clearance, 3-5 mm tract, 70-90% stone-free [1]
• ESWL: not suitable for staghorn — poor clearance, multiple sessions, risk of steinstrasse [1]
• Open surgery (anatrophic nephrolithotomy): historical — rarely needed now; reserved for failed PCNL or complex anatomy [1]
• PCNL is the preferred first-line treatment in children [1]

**(c)** [5 marks]
• Position: prone or supine (Galdakao-modified supine position) [1]
• US or fluoroscopic guidance for puncture [1]
• Tract dilation: serial dilators (Amplatz) or balloon; tract 18-24Fr depending on child size [1]
• Nephroscopy: rigid or flexible through sheath [1]
• Stone fragmentation: pneumatic (Lithoclast), ultrasound, or Ho:YAG laser; retrieval with graspers [1]

**(d)** [5 marks]
• Incomplete clearance → residual fragments → persistent infection → stone regrowth [1]
• Post-operative CT KUB to confirm stone-free status [1]
• Send stone for culture and sensitivity [1]
• Eradicate infection: culture-directed antibiotics for 4-6 weeks [1]
• Metabolic workup: 24-hour urine (may have underlying metabolic abnormality in 30%) [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This is a staghorn calculus from a urease-producing Proteus UTI, making it struvite. PCNL is the definitive treatment — I would position the child prone, access through the lower pole calyx, dilate to 20Fr, and fragment with pneumatic lithoclast. Complete clearance is critical as residual fragments will perpetuate infection. Post-operatively, culture-directed antibiotics and metabolic workup (including 24-hour urine) are essential.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Knows the specific pathophysiology of struvite stones (urease → ammonia → alkaline urine)
• Describes paediatric PCNL with specific tract sizes
• Recognises that metabolic abnormalities coexist in 30% of infection stone formers
• Emphasises complete clearance as a therapeutic goal

## QUESTION 3 — SAQ: Medical Management [Total: 8 marks] [FOUNDATION]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** List four modifiable dietary risk factors for calcium oxalate stones. **[4 marks]**

**(b)** State the prophylactic pharmacological options for recurrent calcium stone formers in children and their mechanisms of action. **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [4 marks — 1 each]
• Low fluid intake (low urine volume) [1]
• High sodium intake [1]
• High animal protein intake [1]
• High oxalate intake (spinach, chocolate, rhubarb, nuts) [1]
• (Also: dietary calcium restriction — which is NOT recommended; low dietary calcium increases oxalate absorption)

**(b)** [4 marks]
• Thiazide diuretics (chlortalidone) — reduce urinary calcium excretion [1]
• Potassium citrate — increases urinary citrate (stone inhibitor) [1]
• Allopurinol — reduces uric acid (for urate/cystine stones) [1]
• Tiopronin — for cystinuria (forms soluble complexes with cystine) [1]

## QUESTION 4 — SAQ: Cystine Stones [Total: 8 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** What is the genetic basis of cystinuria and how does it present in children? **[3 marks]**

**(b)** Outline the management of cystine stones, including medical therapy and role of URS/ESWL. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [3 marks]
• Autosomal recessive (SLC3A1 or SLC7A9 gene mutations) [1]
• Defect in dibasic amino acid transport in renal tubule and small intestine [1]
• Cystine, ornithine, arginine, lysine (COLA) in urine; cystine forms crystals/stones due to low solubility at normal pH [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• High fluid intake (>3 L/day, including at night) [1]
• Urinary alkalinisation (potassium citrate to pH 7.5-8.0) — increases cystine solubility [1]
• Tiopronin (α-mercaptopropionylglycine) 10-20 mg/kg/day — forms more soluble mixed disulphide [1]
• ESWL: less effective for cystine (hard stones); adjunctive [1]
• URS/PCNL: preferred for clearance; cystine stones are difficult to fragment [1]

## QUESTION 5 — LCV: Ureteric Stone Management [Total: 35 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~40 minutes

─── STAGE 1: PRESENTATION ───────────────────────────────
A 12-year-old girl presents with acute right flank pain, vomiting, and microscopic haematuria. CT KUB shows a 10 mm stone at the right vesicoureteric junction (VUJ) with mild hydronephrosis (APD 12 mm). She has normal renal function.

**Q1a.** What is the likelihood of spontaneous passage of a 10 mm distal ureteric stone? **[2 marks]**
**Q1b.** List the treatment options and recommend one with justification. **[5 marks]**

─── STAGE 2: INTERVENTION ───────────────────────────────
You proceed with ureteroscopy (URS) and laser lithotripsy. During the procedure, you note a tight distal ureter with the stone impacted in the intramural tunnel.

**Q2a.** Describe the technique of ureteroscopic stone fragmentation using the Ho:YAG laser, including settings, basket use, and safety considerations. **[6 marks]**
**Q2b.** What are the options if you cannot safely access the stone? **[3 marks]**

─── STAGE 3: COMPLICATION ──────────────────────────────
Post-operatively, the girl develops flank pain and fever (39°C) 24 hours after URS. US shows a perinephric fluid collection and increasing hydronephrosis.

**Q3a.** What is the likely diagnosis and how would you confirm it? **[3 marks]**
**Q3b.** Outline your immediate management. **[5 marks]**

─── STAGE 4: METABOLIC WORKUP ──────────────────────────
After recovery, stone analysis shows calcium oxalate dihydrate. 24-hour urine shows hypercalciuria (8 mg/kg/day), normal oxalate, and low urinary citrate. Serum calcium and PTH are normal.

**Q4a.** What is the diagnosis (specific metabolic abnormality)? **[2 marks]**
**Q4b.** Outline management to prevent recurrence, including dietary advice and pharmacotherapy. **[5 marks]**
**Q4c.** What advice would you give regarding family screening? **[2 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**FULL MARKING GUIDE:**

**Q1a.** [2 marks]
• 10 mm stone has <10% chance of spontaneous passage [1]
• Requires active intervention [1]

**Q1b.** [5 marks]
• URS + laser lithotripsy: first-line for distal ureteric stones >10 mm; >95% stone-free [1]
• ESWL: less effective for distal ureter (bone interference) [1]
• Recommendation: URS — highest single-session stone-free rate, lowest retreatment [1]

**Q2a.** [6 marks]
• Semirigid ureteroscope (6-7.5Fr) or flexible ureteroscope [1]
• Safety wire through working channel to maintain access [1]
• Ho:YAG laser settings: 0.6-1 J, 8-15 Hz; dusting vs fragmentation settings [1-2]
• Fragment with laser; retrieve fragments with tipless nitinol basket (<3 mm fragments) [1]
• Avoid: ureteric avulsion, mucosal injury, thermal injury from laser [1]
• Post-stenting: double-J stent for 1-2 weeks to prevent obstruction from oedema [1]

**Q2b.** [3 marks]
• Balloon dilation of intramural tunnel [1]
• Leave safety wire, place nephrostomy, stage URS 2 weeks later [1]
• Open ureterolithotomy (rarely needed now) [1]

**Q3a.** [3 marks]
• Ureteric perforation with urinoma [1]
• Confirm: non-contrast CT or US shows fluid collection; drain fluid creatinine > serum [1]

**Q3b.** [5 marks]
• IV broad-spectrum antibiotics [1]
• Place percutaneous nephrostomy to divert [1]
• Drain urinoma percutaneously [1]
• Leave ureteric stent across perforation if possible [1]
• Observe for resolution; re-image in 48 hours [1]

**Q4a.** [2 marks]
• Idiopathic hypercalciuria with hypocitraturia [2]

**Q4b.** [5 marks]
• Increased fluid intake to achieve 2-3 L/day urine output [1]
• Reduced sodium intake [1]
• Normal calcium intake (do NOT restrict) [1]
• Potassium citrate 2-4 mEq/kg/day to correct hypocitraturia [1]
• Thiazide (chlortalidone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day) if persistent hypercalciuria [1]

**Q4c.** [2 marks]
• Screen first-degree relatives: spot urine calcium:creatinine ratio [1]
• Hypercalciuria has familial tendency — may be asymptomatic [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Provides Ho:YAG laser settings specific to clinical situation
• Manages ureteric perforation systematically (nephrostomy, stent, antibiotics, drainage)
• Recognises hypocitraturia as a treatable metabolic abnormality
• Recommends family screening for hypercalciuria

## QUESTION 6 — EMQ: Stone Composition by Age [Total: 10 marks] [BOARD]

**Theme: Paediatric Urolithiasis — Age-Related Stone Composition**

**OPTIONS (each option may be used once, more than once, or not at all):**

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| A. Calcium oxalate monohydrate (whewellite) | F. Xanthine |
| B. Calcium oxalate dihydrate (weddellite) | G. Cystine |
| C. Calcium phosphate (apatite/brushite) | H. Drug-induced (e.g., ceftriaxone, indinavir) |
| D. Struvite (magnesium ammonium phosphate) | I. Ammonium urate |
| E. Uric acid | J. Silica |

**For each of the following clinical scenarios, select the single most likely stone composition:**

**1.** A 6-month-old exclusively breastfed infant with recurrent UTIs and a staghorn calculus. Urine grows Proteus. **[1 mark]**

**2.** A 10-year-old boy with a family history of stones, hypercalciuria, and a stone that is extremely hard, brown, and resists fragmentation on ESWL. **[1 mark]**

**3.** A 14-year-old boy with a history of ileostomy for Crohn's disease, passing multiple small radiolucent stones. Urine pH is 5.0. **[1 mark]**

**4.** An 8-year-old girl from a developing country with a history of low-protein diet, passing a brown stone that crumbles easily. Urine pH is 6.5. **[1 mark]**

**5.** A 6-year-old with a radiolucent stone on CT. She has a history of seizures treated with topiramate. Urine pH is 8.0. **[1 mark]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**ANSWERS & RATIONALE:**

**1.** **D** — Struvite. Infection stones from urease-producing organisms (Proteus) are the most common stones in infants. Staghorn morphology and alkaline urine with recurrent UTIs are characteristic.

**2.** **A** — Calcium oxalate monohydrate. COM stones are extremely hard and difficult to fragment with ESWL. They are often associated with hyperoxaluria but can occur with hypercalciuria. Their density makes them resistant to ESWL.

**3.** **E** — Uric acid. Patients with ileostomies lose bicarbonate in stool → metabolic acidosis → acidic urine (pH 5.0) → uric acid precipitation. These stones are radiolucent.

**4.** **I** — Ammonium urate. Seen in developing countries with low-protein, high-cereal diets and chronic dehydration. These stones are associated with hypokalaemia and hypocitraturia.

**5.** **C** — Calcium phosphate (apatite). Topiramate is a carbonic anhydrase inhibitor → metabolic acidosis → alkaline urine (pH 8.0) → promotes calcium phosphate precipitation. These stones are radiolucent on plain X-ray but visible on CT.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Assuming all stones in children are calcium oxalate — composition changes with age, geography, and metabolic context
• Not recognising that uric acid stones occur in children with bowel disease
• Missing drug-induced stones (topiramate is increasingly common in paediatric stone formers)

**EXAMINER'S NOTES — COMMON SCRIPT PATTERNS TO WATCH FOR:**
• Many candidates recommend calcium restriction for hypercalciuria — this is WRONG in children and can cause negative calcium balance and reduced bone density
• ESWL in children is well-tolerated but candidates often underestimate the need for general anaesthesia or multiple sessions
• Ho:YAG laser settings are a good discriminator — strong candidates give specific energy/frequency settings
• Struvite stone recurrence is very high if clearance is incomplete — strong candidates emphasise stone-free rate as the primary outcome
