## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: ARPKD vs ADPKD [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 2-day-old term female neonate is noted to have bilateral flank masses on routine examination. She is normotensive but has mild respiratory distress requiring supplementary oxygen. Renal ultrasound shows symmetrically enlarged, echogenic kidneys (above 95th centile) with poor corticomedullary differentiation. Few small cysts are visible. Liver appears echogenic with bile duct dilatation. Parents are first cousins.

**(a)** What is the most likely diagnosis and what is the mode of inheritance? **[3 marks]**

**(b)** Describe the histological features of ARPKD and explain the genotype-phenotype correlation. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Compare ARPKD with ADPKD in terms of genetics, presentation in childhood, renal imaging features, and systemic manifestations. **[6 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the management of ARPKD presenting in the neonatal period, including the role of respiratory support, antihypertensives, and planning for renal replacement therapy. **[5 marks]**

**(e)** Outline the prenatal counselling points for parents who have had one child with ARPKD regarding recurrence risk and options for future pregnancies. **[3 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [3 marks]
• Autosomal recessive polycystic kidney disease (ARPKD) [1]
• Symmetrically enlarged echogenic kidneys with bile duct dilatation suggests ductal plate malformation [1]
• Autosomal recessive — consanguinity is a risk factor [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Histology: fusiform dilatation of collecting ducts (ectatic collecting tubules) — "sponge-like" [1]
• Renal: tubular dilatation gives the characteristic echogenicity on US [1]
• Liver: ductal plate malformation → congenital hepatic fibrosis → bile duct dilatation [1]
• Genotype: PKHD1 gene mutation on chromosome 6p12 [1]
• Phenotype: severe forms present perinatally (large kidneys, pulmonary hypoplasia); milder forms present later (portal hypertension, varices) [1]

**(c)** [6 marks]
• Genetics: ARPKD = PKHD1 (6p12); ADPKD = PKD1 (16p13.3, 85%) or PKD2 (4q21, 15%) [1]
• ARPKD: autosomal recessive, 25% recurrence risk; ADPKD: autosomal dominant, 50% risk [1]
• ARPKD: presenting in utero/neonatal with bilateral enlarged echogenic kidneys, oligohydramnios → pulmonary hypoplasia [1]
• ADPKD in children: usually asymptomatic, may have hypertension, cysts develop over time (not usually present at birth) [1]
• ARPKD: hepatic fibrosis always present (portal hypertension in older children); ADPKD: no liver fibrosis (but may have liver cysts in adults) [1]
• ARPKD: 30% mortality in first year; ADPKD: rarely progresses to ESRD in childhood [1]

**(d)** [5 marks]
• Respiratory support: CPAP/ventilation for pulmonary hypoplasia; prostacyclin for pulmonary hypertension [1]
• Antihypertensives: ACE inhibitors first-line for hypertension (renin-mediated) [1]
• Growth: nasogastric feeds if failure to thrive (kidneys compress stomach) [1]
• Electrolytes: monitor for hyponatraemia (impaired water excretion), acidosis [1]
• RRT planning: peritoneal dialysis when eGFR <10-15; nephrectomy if massive kidneys prevent respiratory weaning [1]

**(e)** [3 marks]
• Recurrence risk: 25% (autosomal recessive) — offer genetic counselling [1]
• Options: chorionic villus sampling (CVS) for PKHD1 mutation in future pregnancies [1]
• Prenatal US monitoring from 16-18 weeks for large echogenic kidneys and oligohydramnios [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This neonate has ARPKD, confirmed by the triad of bilateral massively enlarged echogenic kidneys, respiratory distress, and hepatic fibrosis on US. ARPKD is autosomal recessive (PKHD1 gene, 25% recurrence). The neonatal form carries a 30% first-year mortality, primarily from respiratory complications. Management involves respiratory support, ACE inhibitors for hypertension, and planning for dialysis if renal function declines. Long-term survivors face portal hypertension from congenital hepatic fibrosis. Genetic counselling for future pregnancies is essential.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Distinguishes ARPKD and ADPKD systematically across multiple domains
• Mentions the genotype-phenotype correlation and ductal plate malformation
• Describes specific neonatal management (CPAP, ACE-i, NG feeds)
• Quotes specific mortality and recurrence figures

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Multicystic Dysplastic Kidney [Total: 20 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 6-week-old infant is referred for follow-up of an antenatally detected right-sided multicystic dysplastic kidney (MCDK). The left kidney appears normal with compensatory hypertrophy on postnatal US. The infant is thriving with no UTIs.

**(a)** Explain the embryological basis of MCDK and describe the typical ultrasound features that distinguish it from other cystic renal diseases. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Outline the natural history of MCDK, including rates of spontaneous involution and the risk of complications such as hypertension and malignancy. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Describe the recommended surveillance protocol for a child with unilateral MCDK, including the rationale for each investigation. **[4 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the controversies surrounding routine follow-up for MCDK, including the arguments for and against lifelong surveillance. **[6 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [5 marks]
• MCDK: ureteric bud atresia at ~8-10 weeks gestation, failure of metanephric induction [1]
• US: multiple non-communicating cysts of varying size, no identifiable renal pelvis, no renal parenchyma [1]
• Absent renal artery on Doppler [1]
• Contrast with: severe hydronephrosis (single communicating dilated pelvis ± cortical rim), ARPKD (bilateral, not cystic in early stage), cystic Wilms (rare, solid components) [1]
• Contralateral kidney: VUR in ~20%, PUJ obstruction in ~5-10% [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Natural history: spontaneous involution (partial or complete) occurs in 50-70% over 5-10 years [1]
• Complete involution most common; residual cysts may calcify [1]
• Hypertension: <1% in childhood (MCDK rarely causes renin-mediated hypertension) [1]
• Malignancy: no proven increased risk above general population (Wilms tumour risk <0.1%) — older series overestimated risk [1]
• Infection: rare unless reflux into MCDK or ureteric stump [1]

**(c)** [4 marks]
• US at 1 year, then at 3 and 5 years to document involution [1]
• Contralateral kidney US to monitor compensatory hypertrophy [1]
• MCU recommended at diagnosis to exclude contralateral VUR [1]
• Annual blood pressure measurements [1]

**(d)** [6 marks]
• Arguments FOR surveillance: risk (albeit low) of hypertension/malignancy, family reassurance, documenting involution [1-2]
• AGAINST surveillance: extremely low complication risk (<1%), cost of serial imaging, radiation exposure (if using DMSA/CT, not US), clinic time and parental anxiety [1-2]
• Current ESPU guidance: discharge after 2-3 normal US showing involution + normal BP; no need for lifelong follow-up [1]
• Exception: contralateral abnormalities may need separate follow-up [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
MCDK results from ureteric bud atresia at ~8-10 weeks, leading to non-functioning dysplastic tissue. Ultrasound shows multiple non-communicating cysts with no parenchyma. The natural history is benign: 50-70% involute spontaneously by age 5, hypertension risk is <1%, and malignancy risk is negligible. I would recommend US at 1, 3, and 5 years, a single MCU to exclude contralateral VUR, and annual BP checks. After involution with normal BP, I would discharge the patient. Prolonged surveillance for risk that is below the general population baseline is not evidence-based.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Quotes specific involution rates and complication risks with numbers
• Articulates the controversy and cites current ESPU guidance for discharge
• Clearly debunks the historical malignancy risk association
• Recommends against lifelong surveillance

## QUESTION 3 — SAQ: Simple Renal Cysts [Total: 8 marks] [FOUNDATION]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** List the Bosniak classification for renal cysts and state the management for each category. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** What is the significance of a simple renal cyst found incidentally on US in a 6-year-old child? **[3 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [5 marks]
• Bosniak I: simple cyst, thin wall, no septation/calcification — no follow-up [1]
• Bosniak II: minimally complicated (few thin septa, fine calcification) — follow-up US at 6-12 months [1]
• Bosniak IIF: more septa, mildly thickened wall — follow-up more frequently (CT at 6 months) [1]
• Bosniak III: thickened walls, solid components — surgical excision (risk of malignancy ~50%) [1]
• Bosniak IV: obvious solid mass/carcinoma — nephrectomy [1]

**(b)** [3 marks]
• Simple renal cysts in children are rare and usually associated with underlying disease (ADPKD, tuberous sclerosis, VHL) [1]
• A truly simple cyst in a child warrants further investigation (CT/MRI) to exclude complex features [1]
• Genetic referral for possible syndromic association [1]

## QUESTION 4 — SAQ: Nephronophthisis [Total: 8 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** What is nephronophthisis, and what are the major clinical features? **[4 marks]**

**(b)** Describe the renal imaging findings and how nephronophthisis presents differently from ARPKD. **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [4 marks]
• Nephronophthisis: autosomal recessive cystic disease; NPHP gene mutations [1]
• Medullary cysts, tubular atrophy, interstitial fibrosis [1]
• Clinical triad: polyuria/polydipsia (nephrogenic DI), growth failure, progressive CKD [1]
• Presentation: age 2-14 years; ESRD by adolescence (juvenile form) [1]

**(b)** [4 marks]
• Nephronophthisis: normal-sized or small kidneys with increased echogenicity; cysts at corticomedullary junction [1]
• ARPKD: massively enlarged kidneys, no discrete cysts early on [1]
• Nephronophthisis: no hepatic involvement (vs ARPKD with congenital hepatic fibrosis) [1]
• Nephronophthisis: extrarenal associations (retinitis pigmentosa in Senior-Løken syndrome, cerebellar ataxia in Joubert syndrome) [1]

## QUESTION 5 — LCV: Syndromic Cystic Disease [Total: 35 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~40 minutes

─── STAGE 1: PRESENTATION ───────────────────────────────
A 4-year-old boy presents with a single episode of visible haematuria. He is otherwise well. US shows a 4 cm complex cystic lesion in the upper pole of the right kidney with septations and thickened walls. You note a hypopigmented patch (ash leaf macule) on his leg.

**Q1a.** What is the differential diagnosis for a complex renal cyst in a child? **[4 marks]**
**Q1b.** What syndromic diagnosis should you suspect, and what additional clinical features would you look for? **[4 marks]**

─── STAGE 2: INVESTIGATION ──────────────────────────────
CT confirms a Bosniak III lesion in the right upper pole. MRI brain shows subependymal nodules and cortical tubers. Family history: the mother has a history of seizures and was diagnosed with "benign renal cysts" at age 20.

**Q2a.** What is the genetic basis of this condition? **[3 marks]**
**Q2b.** Outline the renal manifestations of tuberous sclerosis complex (TSC) and their management. **[6 marks]**

─── STAGE 3: MANAGEMENT ─────────────────────────────────
The boy undergoes a right upper pole partial nephrectomy. Histology confirms angiomyolipoma with epithelioid features (a TSC-associated renal tumour).

**Q3a.** What post-operative surveillance is required for this child? **[4 marks]**
**Q3b.** Discuss the role of mTOR inhibitors (everolimus) in TSC-associated renal disease. **[5 marks]**

─── STAGE 4: FAMILY SCREENING ──────────────────────────
The mother is subsequently diagnosed with TSC. She also has bilateral renal angiomyolipomas; the largest is 5 cm with intralesional aneurysms.

**Q4a.** What is the risk of haemorrhage in angiomyolipomas and when is embolisation indicated? **[3 marks]**
**Q4b.** Discuss the autosomal dominant inheritance of TSC and the implications for other family members. **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**FULL MARKING GUIDE:**

**Q1a.** [4 marks]
• Cystic Wilms tumour [1]
• Cystic partially differentiated nephroblastoma (CPDN) [1]
• Multiocular cystic nephroma [1]
• Complex renal cyst in syndromic context (TSC/VHL) [1]

**Q1b.** [4 marks]
• Tuberous sclerosis (TSC) — suggested by ash leaf macule + complex renal cyst [1]
• Additional features: facial angiofibromas, shagreen patch, seizures, neurodevelopmental delay [1]
• Cardiac rhabdomyoma (may regress) [1]
• Renal angiomyolipomas (most common renal lesion in TSC) [1]

**Q2a.** [3 marks]
• Autosomal dominant [1]
• TSC1 (hamartin, 9q34) — milder phenotype [1]
• TSC2 (tuberin, 16p13.3) — more severe renal involvement [1]

**Q2b.** [6 marks]
• Angiomyolipomas: benign mixed tumours (smooth muscle, fat, vessels) — most common [1]
• Renal cysts: simple or complex [1]
• RCC: slightly increased risk [1]
• Angiomyolipoma management: <3 cm → observation; 3-4 cm → surveillance US ± MRI annually; >4 cm → consider embolisation/mTOR inhibitor [1]
• Risk of spontaneous haemorrhage (Wunderlich syndrome) if >4 cm or intralesional aneurysms >5 mm [1]
• Embolisation: selective arterial embolisation for large/aneurysm-bearing AMLs [1]

**Q3a.** [4 marks]
• US/MRI every 1-2 years for new lesions in remaining kidney [1]
• BP monitoring annually [1]
• MRI brain for TSC surveillance [1]
• Ophthalmology and dermatology referral [1]

**Q3b.** [5 marks]
• mTOR pathway is constitutively active in TSC (loss of TSC1/TSC2 inhibition) [1]
• Everolimus (mTOR inhibitor) reduces angiomyolipoma volume by ~50% in 6-12 months [1]
• EXIST-2 trial demonstrated efficacy for renal AML in TSC [1]
• Indications: AML >3 cm, bilateral/multiple, growing lesions, or haemorrhage risk [1]
• Side effects: stomatitis, rash, fatigue, hyperglycaemia, pneumonitis [1]

**Q4a.** [3 marks]
• AML haemorrhage risk: ~25-50% if >4 cm with aneurysms >5 mm; life-threatening (Wunderlich syndrome) [1]
• Embolisation indicated: AML >4 cm, symptomatic, or aneurysms >5 mm on imaging [1]
• Also: mTOR inhibitors as alternative to embolisation [1]

**Q4b.** [4 marks]
• Autosomal dominant with high penetrance; 50% recurrence risk in offspring [1]
• TSC2 often more severe than TSC1 [1]
• Genetic testing for family members [1]
• Preimplantation genetic diagnosis available [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Recognises ash leaf macule as a clue to TSC
• Discusses mTOR inhibitors with specific trial reference (EXIST-2)
• Gives size thresholds for AML management (3 cm, 4 cm)
• Describes Wunderlich syndrome and indications for embolisation

## QUESTION 6 — EMQ: Cystic Renal Disease [Total: 10 marks] [BOARD]

**Theme: Cystic Renal Disease — Presentation and Diagnosis**

**OPTIONS (each option may be used once, more than once, or not at all):**

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| A. ARPKD | F. Nephronophthisis |
| B. ADPKD | G. Simple renal cyst |
| C. MCDK | H. Cystic partially differentiated nephroblastoma |
| D. Tuberous sclerosis | I. VHL disease-associated cysts |
| E. Multiocular cystic nephroma | J. Acquired cystic disease (dialysis) |

**For each of the following clinical scenarios, select the single most appropriate diagnosis:**

**1.** A 6-week-old girl with bilateral massive flank masses. US shows symmetrically enlarged, echogenic kidneys (>95th centile) with poor corticomedullary differentiation. Liver shows ductal plate malformation. **[1 mark]**

**2.** A 2-week-old with multiple non-communicating cysts replacing the left kidney. The right kidney shows compensatory hypertrophy. Renal artery not visible on Doppler. **[1 mark]**

**3.** A 10-year-old boy with a 5-year history of polyuria, polydipsia, growth failure, and progressive CKD. US shows normal-sized kidneys with increased echogenicity and cysts at the corticomedullary junction. No liver disease. **[1 mark]**

**4.** A 3-year-old with a cystic renal mass that has multiple locales separated by septa. The septa enhance on CT. There are no solid components. No cutaneous or neurological features. **[1 mark]**

**5.** A 6-year-old with hypertension and a 4 cm cystic lesion in the right kidney. She has retinal angiomas. Family history of cerebellar haemangioblastoma in her father. **[1 mark]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**ANSWERS & RATIONALE:**

**1.** **A** — ARPKD. Classic presentation of bilateral massively enlarged echogenic kidneys with hepatic fibrosis in a neonate. This is the perinatal presentation.

**2.** **C** — MCDK. Multiple non-communicating cysts replacing one kidney with contralateral compensatory hypertrophy is pathognomonic. Absent renal artery on Doppler confirms the diagnosis.

**3.** **F** — Nephronophthisis. The clinical triad of polyuria/polydipsia, growth failure, and CKD in a school-age child with corticomedullary cysts and no hepatomegaly is classic.

**4.** **E** — Multiocular cystic nephroma. A septated cystic mass without solid components in a young child. CPDN is the histological counterpart. No extrarenal features.

**5.** **I** — VHL disease. The combination of a complex cystic renal lesion with retinal angioma and family history of cerebellar haemangioblastoma is diagnostic of von Hippel-Lindau disease. VHL gene mutation (3p25).

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Confusing MCDK with hydronephrosis — absent renal artery on Doppler is the key discriminator
• Not recognising nephronophthisis because the kidneys are not enlarged (they are normal/small)
• Missing syndromic associations (TSC/VHL) when evaluating complex renal cysts

**EXAMINER'S NOTES — COMMON SCRIPT PATTERNS TO WATCH FOR:**
• MCDK management has changed — many older candidates still advise serial DMSA scans and lifelong BP monitoring; current guidance is far more conservative with discharge after involution
• The ARPKD/ADPKD comparison is a favourite viva topic — candidates must be systematic across genetics, renal, and liver domains
• TSC-associated renal disease is increasingly tested — mTOR inhibitor management is now expected knowledge
• Nephronophthisis is often forgotten because the kidneys are normal-sized, so it is missed on imaging
