## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Preoperative Assessment of Hypospadias [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 6-month-old boy is referred with hypospadias. On examination, the urethral meatus is located at the midshaft of the penis. There is mild ventral curvature (chordee) on erection induced by saline injection. The prepuce is complete and hooded dorsally. There is no scrotal transposition. Both testes are in the scrotum.

**(a)** Classify this hypospadias by meatal position and describe the assessment under anaesthesia (AUA) that should be performed before surgical repair. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Explain the embryological basis of hypospadias and its association with other genitourinary anomalies. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Outline the surgical options for a midshaft hypospadias with mild chordee, including the advantages and disadvantages of each technique. **[6 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the role of preoperative testosterone stimulation in this child — including the indications, regimens, and potential concerns. **[6 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [5 marks] — 1 per valid point up to 5:
• Midshaft hypospadias: meatus on distal half of penile shaft [1]
• AUA: assess meatal position, size, and quality; degree of chordee (artificial erection → 30° = mild) [1]
• Assess urethral plate quality (width, depth, vascularity) [1]
• Assess glans size and configuration [1]
• Assess prepuce (complete/incomplete hood) and penoscrotal transposition [1]
• Note: chordee <30° typically corrected by degloving alone; >30° may need additional plication or urethral plate division [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Hypospadias: arrested development of urethral folds fusion (8-14 weeks gestation) [1]
• Normal fusion: endodermal urethral folds fuse ventrally from proximally to distally [1]
• Failure of androgen stimulation of the urethral folds → incomplete fusion [1]
• Associated GU anomalies: cryptorchidism (7-10%), inguinal hernia (9-15%), VUR (if proximal hypospadias) [1]
• Proximal hypospadias + cryptorchidism → investigate for DSD (especially if bilateral) [1]

**(c)** [6 marks]
• TIP (Snodgrass) repair: tubularise incised plate — versatile, single-stage, good cosmetic outcome [1]
   • Advantages: good meatal position (slit-like meatus), single stage, low complication [1]
   • Disadvantages: requires healthy urethral plate; fistula risk ~5-10% [1]
• Thiersch-Duplay (Mathieu if distal): local skin flap — good for distal hypospadias [1]
   • Advantages: well-established, high success rate
   • Disadvantages: circular meatus, not for midshaft with chordee
• Two-stage repair (Bracka): if severe chordee requires urethral plate division [1]
   • Advantages: corrects chordee definitively; second stage gives long neourethra
   • Disadvantages: two operations, longer recovery [1]

**(d)** [6 marks]
• Indications: small glans, thin urethral plate, hypoplastic penile shaft [1]
• Regimens: testosterone enanthate 2 mg/kg IM 4-6 weeks before surgery; or topical dihydrotestosterone (DHT) gel [1]
• Benefits: increased penile length, glans circumference, urethral plate width, vascularity [1]
• Concerns: theoretical risk of premature epiphyseal closure — not clinically significant with short courses [1]
• Temporary increase in pubic hair, behavioural changes — resolve after stopping [1]
• Evidence: meta-analyses show reduced complication rates in proximal hypospadias [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This boy has midshaft hypospadias with 30° chordee. AUA is essential — artificial erection confirms chordee and assesses urethral plate quality. Given the mild chordee (<30°), I would proceed with a single-stage TIP repair (Snodgrass). The urethral plate is preserved and tubularised over a catheter. The dorsal hood provides excellent skin cover. I would not use testosterone in this case as the glans appears adequate. Complications include fistula (5-10%), meatal stenosis, and glans dehiscence.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Gives specific chordee threshold (30°) for deciding between degloving alone vs urethral plate division
• Mentions artificial erection evaluation as part of AUA
• Provides specific testosterone regimen (2 mg/kg IM)
• Distinguishes TIP from two-stage based on chordee severity

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Complications of Hypospadias Repair [Total: 20 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 2-year-old boy underwent TIP repair for distal penile hypospadias 8 months ago. He presents with a urethrocutaneous fistula at the corona. The meatus is in a good position and there is no meatal stenosis. Stream is good.

**(a)** List the common complications of hypospadias surgery and the factors contributing to fistula formation. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Describe the assessment and timing of fistula repair. **[4 marks]**

**(c)** Outline the surgical techniques available for fistula closure, including the principles of multilayer repair. **[6 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss strategies to minimise fistula occurrence in hypospadias repair. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [5 marks] — 1 per valid point up to 5:
• Complications: urethrocutaneous fistula, meatal stenosis, glans dehiscence, urethral stricture, persistent chordee, diverticulum [1]
• Fistula risk factors: distal obstruction (meatal stenosis), infection/haematoma, poor vascularity, poor tissue coverage, tension on suture line [1]
• Thin urethral plate, narrow neourethra (<10Fr), excessive cautery [1]
• Surgeon inexperience (steep learning curve) [1]
• Fistula more common with proximal repairs (15-20%) vs distal (5-10%) [1]

**(b)** [4 marks]
• Assess: fistula location, size, number; check for distal stenosis (by calibration); assess with retrograde urethrogram if complex [1]
• Timing: minimum 6 months after primary repair — allows inflammation to resolve [1]
• More conservative for simple fistula vs complex (if multiple/proximal, consider staged repair) [1]
• If >2 attempts failed → consider staged approach or referring to specialist [1]

**(c)** [6 marks]
• Simple closure: excise fistula tract, close urethral mucosa with 7-0 absorbable, then tissue interposition layer, then skin [1]
• Multilayer closure: close urethra, then surround fistula with dartos fascia flap [1]
• Tunnel rotation: rotate fistula through a tunnel into a new skin closure site [1]
• Dorsal dartos flap: mobilise dorsal prepuce dartos to cover the repair (waterproofing layer) [1]
• If chordee present: re-do entire repair [1]
• If fistula too large (>3 mm) or recurrent: consider re-do urethroplasty, not just fistula closure [1]

**(d)** [5 marks]
• Use of dartos barrier flap (most important — reduces fistula by 50%) [1]
• Meticulous haemostasis (avoid bipolar or fine monopolar) [1]
• Tension-free skin closure [1]
• Appropriate catheter size (not too large — ischaemic) [1]
• Avoid distal urethral obstruction — calibrate meatus at end of case [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
Fistula after TIP repair is the most common complication (5-10%). In this case, the coronal fistula with no distal obstruction is favourable — the meatus is good and the stream is normal. I would wait 6 months to allow inflammation to resolve. At surgery, the fistula tract is excised, the urethra is closed in multiple layers (mucosa, dartos interposition, skin), and a dorsal dartos flap is developed as a barrier layer. Dartos interposition reduces fistula recurrence by 50%.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Quotes specific fistula reduction with dartos barrier flap (50%)
• Distinguishes simple fistula repair from re-do urethroplasty based on fistula size
• Discusses the importance of excluding distal obstruction before fistula repair
• Recommends minimum 6-month interval before repair

## QUESTION 3 — SAQ: MAGPI Procedure [Total: 8 marks] [FOUNDATION]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** What does the acronym MAGPI stand for, and what are the indications for its use? **[3 marks]**

**(b)** Describe the steps of the MAGPI procedure and its main limitation. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [3 marks]
• MAGPI = Meatal Advancement and Glanuloplasty Incorporated [1]
• Indications: distal hypospadias with a glandular or coronal meatus [1]
• Contraindication: chordee (MAGPI does not correct chordee) [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Circumferential incision 2 mm proximal to meatus [1]
• Mobilise urethra distally [1]
• Glanuloplasty wings [1]
• Suture glans over advanced meatus [1]
• Limitation: does not lengthen the urethra — only advances the meatus; cannot be used if chordee is present [1]

## QUESTION 4 — SAQ: Two-Stage Repair [Total: 8 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 12-month-old boy with penoscrotal hypospadias and 60° chordee. The urethral plate is short and poorly vascularised.

**(a)** Why would a two-stage repair be preferred in this case? **[3 marks]**

**(b)** Describe the Bracka two-stage urethroplasty technique. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [3 marks]
• >30° chordee requiring urethral plate division — cannot do single-stage TIP [1]
• Poor quality urethral plate (short, thin, fibrotic) — vascularisation needed [1]
• Two-stage allows chordee correction in stage 1 and reliable urethroplasty in stage 2 [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Stage 1: deglove penis, excise urethral plate, correct chordee, buccal mucosal graft (or inner prepuce graft) quilted to ventral shaft [1-2]
• Wait 6 months for graft maturation [1]
• Stage 2: tube the matured graft over catheter to form neourethra, lay down dartos barrier flap, and skin closure [1-2]

## QUESTION 5 — LCV: Complex Proximal Hypospadias [Total: 35 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~40 minutes

─── STAGE 1: PRESENTATION ───────────────────────────────
A 10-month-old boy presents with penoscrotal hypospadias, bilateral cryptorchidism, and a small phallus. The scrotum is bifid.

**Q1a.** What condition must be urgently excluded? **[3 marks]**
**Q1b.** Outline the investigations you would perform to exclude this. **[5 marks]**

─── STAGE 2: RESULTS ────────────────────────────────────
Endocrine workup shows normal 17-OHP, normal androgens, and normal AMH. Karyotype 46XY. He is diagnosed with isolated (non-DSD) proximal hypospadias with cryptorchidism.

**Q2a.** What is your surgical plan for the cryptorchidism and hypospadias, and what is the timing? **[5 marks]**
**Q2b.** Discuss the advantages and disadvantages of using a buccal mucosal graft for urethroplasty in this child. **[5 marks]**

─── STAGE 3: SURGERY ────────────────────────────────────
You perform a two-stage Bracka repair with buccal mucosal graft and bilateral orchidopexy.

**Q3a.** Describe the stage 1 procedure in detail. **[6 marks]**
**Q3b.** What post-operative care is required including catheter management? **[4 marks]**

─── STAGE 4: COMPLICATION AND FOLLOW-UP ────────────────
At 4 years (2 years after stage 2), he develops a recurrent stricture at the proximal anastomosis.

**Q4a.** Discuss the management options for a recurrent urethral stricture after buccal mucosal graft urethroplasty. **[5 marks]**
**Q4b.** Outline the long-term follow-up for this child through to adolescence. **[2 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**FULL MARKING GUIDE:**

**Q1a.** [3 marks]
• DSD (disorder of sex development) — must exclude [1]
• Ambiguous genitalia: proximal hypospadias + bilateral cryptorchidism ± small phallus is a DSD until proven otherwise [1]
• CAH (21-hydroxylase deficiency) is the most common cause [1]

**Q1b.** [5 marks]
• Karyotype (FISH for XY) [1]
• 17-OHP (to exclude CAH) [1]
• Serum electrolytes [1]
• AMH/Müllerian imaging (to assess Müllerian structures) [1]
• hCG stimulation test (to assess Leydig cell function) [1]

**Q2a.** [5 marks]
• Orchidopexy: 6-12 months — Fowler-Stephens (two-stage) if high intra-abdominal [1]
• Hypospadias: two-stage repair [1]
• Stage 1 (12-18 months): chordee correction + graft [1]
• Stage 2 (18-24 months): urethroplasty [1]
• Allows sequential correction with adequate time for graft maturation [1]

**Q2b.** [5 marks]
• Advantages: hairless, abundant tissue, easy to harvest, low contraction rate [1]
• Disadvantages: donor site morbidity (pain, scarring, oromotor dysfunction), long-term stricture risk [1]
• Alternative: preputial graft — if prepuce available [1]
• Buccal more reliable than skin for long-segment urethroplasty [1]
• Can be harvested bilaterally from buccal and lip mucosa [1]

**Q3a.** [6 marks]
• Deglove penis through circumcising incision [1]
• Excise urethral plate and fibrotic chordee tissue [1]
• Artificial erection to confirm chordee correction [1]
• Harvest buccal mucosa (lower lip or cheek) — thin, uniform thickness [1]
• Quilt graft to tunica albuginea of corpora [1]
• Tie-over bolster dressing and urethrostomy at native meatus [1]

**Q3b.** [4 marks]
• Catheter 8-10Fr for 7-10 days [1]
• Antibiotics while catheter in situ [1]
• Graft take assessment at 7 days (remove bolster) [1]
• Wait 6 months before stage 2 [1]

**Q4a.** [5 marks]
• Dilation (temporary, high recurrence) [1]
• Internal urethrotomy (success ~30-50% in children) [1]
• Re-do buccal mucosal graft urethroplasty (preferred) [1]
• Two-stage redo: re-graft and re-tube [1]
• Perineal urethrostomy (salvage diversion) [1]

**Q4b.** [2 marks]
• Uroflowmetry annually [1]
• Assessment of ejaculation in adolescence + fertility counselling [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Immediately recognizes DSD as the first consideration in proximal hypospadias + cryptorchidism
• Describes buccal mucosal graft with specific technical detail
• Mentions alternative grafts (prepuce)
• Includes stricture management options with specific success rates

## QUESTION 6 — EMQ: Hypospadias Repair Selection [Total: 10 marks] [BOARD]

**Theme: Selection of Hypospadias Repair**

**OPTIONS (each option may be used once, more than once, or not at all):**

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| A. MAGPI | F. Bracka two-stage with buccal graft |
| B. TIP (Snodgrass) | G. Koyanagi repair |
| C. Mathieu repair | H. Denis Browne repair |
| D. Thiersch-Duplay | I. Onlay island flap (Duckett) |
| E. Transverse preputial island flap (Duckett) | J. Urethral plate division + TIP |

**For each of the following clinical scenarios, select the single most appropriate repair:**

**1.** A 9-month-old with glandular hypospadias, no chordee, good urethral plate. The meatus is at the tip of the glans but ventrally placed. **[1 mark]**

**2.** A 12-month-old with distal penile hypospadias (coronal meatus). There is 15° chordee on artificial erection. Good quality urethral plate. Well-formed glans. **[1 mark]**

**3.** An 18-month-old with midshaft hypospadias, 45° chordee, poor quality urethral plate (thin and fibrotic). The prepuce is complete. **[1 mark]**

**4.** A 14-month-old with midshaft hypospadias, 30° chordee. Good quality urethral plate, well-formed glans, complete prepuce. **[1 mark]**

**5.** A 2-year-old with previous failed TIP repair, complex urethrocutaneous fistula, and residual chordee. He has had prior circumcision. **[1 mark]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**ANSWERS & RATIONALE:**

**1.** **A** — MAGPI. Glandular hypospadias without chordee is the ideal indication. The MAGPI advances the meatus to the tip and provides excellent cosmetic results. Cannot be used if any chordee is present.

**2.** **B** — TIP (Snodgrass). Coronal hypospadias with mild chordee (<30°) that can be corrected by degloving alone. The urethral plate is of good quality. TIP gives a slit-like meatus and excellent cosmesis. The chordee resolves with degloving.

**3.** **F** — Bracka two-stage with buccal graft. >30° chordee requires urethral plate division. The poor quality plate and need for chordee correction make two-stage repair necessary. Buccal graft is preferred.

**4.** **B** — TIP (Snodgrass). 30° chordee can typically be corrected by degloving + dorsal plication if needed. The urethral plate is good. TIP remains the first-choice for midshaft hypospadias with a healthy urethral plate. If chordee persists after degloving, consider dorsal plication (Nesbit/Baskin).

**5.** **F** — Bracka two-stage with buccal graft. Failed TIP with complex fistula, chordee, and no prepuce leaves buccal mucosal graft urethroplasty as the best option. Two-stage is necessary given the complexity. The absence of prepuce eliminates the option of a preputial graft.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Using MAGPI for any hypospadias with chordee
• Performing TIP when chordee >30° requires urethral plate division
• Not considering two-stage repair for complex recurrent cases
• Using circumferential (preputial) skin grafts in circumcised patients

**EXAMINER'S NOTES — COMMON SCRIPT PATTERNS TO WATCH FOR:**
• Candidates consistently underestimate the importance of chordee assessment under anaesthesia
• The Snodgrass (TIP) repair is the single most tested procedure — candidates must know indications, technique, and complication rates
• Two-stage repairs are under-utilised in candidates' answers (they try to make single-stage work for everything)
• Fistula management after hypospadias repair is frequently oversimplified — candidates must discuss timing, multilayer closure, and dartos interposition
