## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Diagnosis of PUJ Obstruction [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 4-year-old boy is referred with a history of recurrent right-sided abdominal pain associated with nausea. The pain is colicky and worse after drinking large volumes. Urine dipstick is normal. His mother reports that he was investigated for antenatal hydronephrosis that "resolved" by age 1. Ultrasound shows right SFU grade III hydronephrosis with an APD of 25 mm and visible calyceal dilatation. The ureter is not dilated. Left kidney is normal.

**(a)** List the differential diagnoses for this presentation and state the most likely diagnosis with justification. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Outline the investigations you would arrange to confirm the diagnosis and assess functional significance, including the timing and sequence of each. **[6 marks]**

**(c)** A MAG3 renogram shows an obstructed drainage curve (O'Reilly type III-IV) with differential function of 38% on the right. Explain the different O'Reilly drainage curve patterns and the clinical significance of this result. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the concept of "equivocal" drainage on renography and the role of the Whitaker test in this setting. **[3 marks]**

**(e)** State the indications for surgical intervention in PUJ obstruction in children. **[3 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [5 marks]
• PUJ obstruction (most likely) — intermittent pain with fluid bolus (Dietl's crisis), no UTI, APD 25 mm with no ureteric dilatation [1]
• VUR (less likely — ureteric dilatation would be expected; no UTI history) [1]
• Calculi (less likely — no haematuria, no UTI, no flank pain typical) [1]
• Megaureter (no ureteric dilatation on US excludes) [1]
• Renal cyst/MCDK (cystic appearance, not pelvicalyceal dilatation) [1]

**(b)** [6 marks]
• MAG3 renogram (Tc-99m MAG3) — dynamic study: differential function + drainage curves [1]
• Protocol: IV injection, posterior imaging for 20-30 minutes [1]
• Diuretic challenge (frusemide 1 mg/kg IV) at 15-20 minutes to assess drainage [1]
• O'Reilly classification: type I (normal), II (dilated non-obstructed), III (equivocal), IV (obstructed) [1]
• Repeat MAG3 if equivocal — at 3-6 months [1]
• CT urogram if anatomical detail needed (crossing vessels, duplex system) [1]

**(c)** [5 marks]
• Type I (normal): rapid uptake and washout, T½ <10 min [1]
• Type II (dilated non-obstructed): good uptake, slow washout but responds to diuretic, T½ 10-20 min [1]
• Type III (equivocal): intermediate response, T½ 20-30 min [1]
• Type IV (obstructed): good uptake, no significant washout after diuretic, T½ >30 min or rising curve [1]
• Clinical significance: differential 38% indicates moderate impairment — surgical intervention indicated [1]

**(d)** [3 marks]
• Equivocal (type III): T½ 20-30 min, inconclusive between obstruction and stasis [1]
• Management: repeat MAG3 after 3-6 months; some use a second diuretic dose [1]
• Whitaker test: antegrade pressure-perfusion study — pump saline into renal pelvis at 10 mL/min; pressure >20 cmH₂O = obstructed [1]

**(e)** [3 marks]
• Differential function <40% and declining on serial renograms [1]
• Symptomatic (Dietl's crisis, pain affecting quality of life) [1]
• Recurrent UTIs in an obstructed system [1]
• Grade IV hydronephrosis with parenchymal thinning [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
This boy most likely has PUJ obstruction — suggested by intermittent colicky abdominal pain after fluid bolus (Dietl's crisis), SFU III hydronephrosis with no ureteric dilatation, and a history of treated antenatal hydronephrosis. I would arrange a MAG3 renogram with diuretic challenge, which shows obstructed drainage (type IV) and differential function of 38%. This meets the threshold for surgical intervention — pyeloplasty is indicated given the declining function and symptoms.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Names O'Reilly drainage curve types I-IV with specific T½ thresholds
• Mentions Whitaker test as a solution for equivocal renography
• Recognises Dietl's crisis by name as the symptom complex of intermittent PUJ obstruction
• Quotes the 40% function threshold for surgical intervention

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Pyeloplasty Technique [Total: 20 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 6-month-old infant with antenatally detected left SFU grade IV hydronephrosis has a MAG3 renogram confirming PUJ obstruction with 36% differential function on the left. You plan a left Anderson-Hynes dismembered pyeloplasty.

**(a)** Describe the patient positioning, incision, and approach for an open Anderson-Hynes pyeloplasty in a 6-month-old infant. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Outline the key steps of the dismembered pyeloplasty procedure, from mobilisation to anastomosis. **[6 marks]**

**(c)** Discuss the role of a nephrostomy tube and/or ureteric stent following pyeloplasty, including the evidence for stented vs non-stented repairs. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** Compare open, laparoscopic, and robotic pyeloplasty in children, including indications, advantages, and disadvantages of each approach. **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**EXAMINER'S MARKING GUIDE**

**(a)** [5 marks]
• Position: lateral decubitus with flank over kidney rest, table flexed [1]
• Incision: loin incision (12th rib tip to lateral border of rectus) or posterior lumbar incision [1]
• Muscle layers: external oblique, internal oblique, transversus abdominis — split in line of fibres [1]
• Gerota's fascia incised to expose renal pelvis [1]
• In infants: extraperitoneal approach preferred to avoid bowel manipulation [1]

**(b)** [6 marks]
• Mobilise renal pelvis and proximal ureter; identify PUJ [1]
• Place stay sutures proximal and distal to PUJ [1]
• Excise PUJ segment sharply, spatulate lateral aspect of ureter 15-20 mm [1]
• Reduce redundant renal pelvis (if large) — excise excess pelvic wall [1]
• Anastomosis: apex of spatulated ureter to most dependent part of renal pelvis with 6-0/7-0 absorbable sutures [1]
• Running vs interrupted suture: interrupted (absorbable monofilament) preferred in infants to allow growth [1]
• Closure in layers over a drain [1]

**(c)** [5 marks]
• Stent options: 4.7Fr double-J stent; placed antegrade or retrograde [1]
• Tranastomotic stent: externalised through renal parenchyma (nephrostent); removed at 7-10 days [1]
• Evidence: no significant difference in leak or re-stricture rates between stented and non-stented [1]
• Non-stented advantages: avoids second anaesthetic for stent removal, lower infection risk [1]
• Stented advantages: reduced urine leak, ensures drainage if oedema occurs at anastomosis [1]

**(d)** [4 marks]
• Open: gold standard, >95% success, larger incision, longer recovery [1]
• Laparoscopic: reduced postoperative pain, shorter stay, steep learning curve; feasible in older children >1 year [1]
• Robotic: 3D vision, wristed instruments, easier suturing; limited availability/cost; comparable success to open [1]
• In infants <1 year: open approach preferred; robotic gaining popularity but requires expertise [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MODEL ANSWER:**
Open Anderson-Hynes pyeloplasty remains the gold standard. Through a flank approach, the PUJ is excised, the ureter spatulated, and a reduced pelvis anastomosed to the spatulated ureter with 7-0 absorbable sutures. I prefer a non-stented repair in uncomplicated cases to avoid a second anaesthetic, but would place a drain. Laparoscopic and robotic approaches offer faster recovery but require appropriate patient selection and surgical expertise. Success is >95% regardless of approach in experienced hands.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Describes the specific suture material and gauge (6-0/7-0 absorbable)
• Covers the controversy of stented vs non-stented with evidence
• Discusses the age cut-off considerations for minimally invasive approaches
• Mentions nephrostent as a specific alternative

## QUESTION 3 — SAQ: Post-nephrectomy for Non-functioning PUJ [Total: 8 marks] [FOUNDATION]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 2-year-old girl with a right PUJ obstruction has <10% differential function on MAG3 and recurrent UTIs.

**(a)** State three indications for nephrectomy rather than pyeloplasty. **[3 marks]**

**(b)** Describe the long-term follow-up required for a child with a solitary kidney, including specific investigations and lifestyle advice. **[5 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [3 marks — 1 each]
• Differential function <10-15% on DMSA/MAG3 [1]
• Non-functioning kidney with symptoms (pain, UTIs, hypertension) [1]
• Renal dysplasia/thin parenchyma with no potential for functional recovery [1]

**(b)** [5 marks]
• Annual blood pressure monitoring (lifelong) [1]
• Annual renal function (U&E, eGFR) [1]
• Urinalysis for proteinuria [1]
• US to monitor compensatory hypertrophy in the first 2 years [1]
• Avoid contact sports where possible; no specific dietary restriction if function normal [1]

## QUESTION 4 — SAQ: Crossing Vessels [Total: 8 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~10 minutes

**(a)** Explain the relationship between crossing renal vessels and PUJ obstruction in children, including the typical anatomical relationship and presentation. **[4 marks]**

**(b)** Describe how the presence of a crossing vessel alters the surgical approach to pyeloplasty. **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**MARKING GUIDE:**

**(a)** [4 marks]
• Crossing vessel: lower pole accessory renal artery or vein crossing anterior to PUJ [1]
• More common in older children/adolescents (10-15% of cases) [1]
• Mechanism: vessel compresses PUJ, exacerbated by fluid diuresis (intermittent obstruction) [1]
• Presentation: intermittent pain (Dietl's crisis) with normal renography between episodes [1]

**(b)** [4 marks]
• If crossing vessel is identified: vessel is transposed posterior to PUJ during pyeloplasty [1]
• Alternatively: vein ligation if small accessory vessel and segmental supply not critical [1]
• If artery: DO NOT ligate — must preserve [1]
• Preoperative CT angiogram to identify vessel; laparoscopic/robotic approach facilitates identification [1]

## QUESTION 5 — LCV: Complicated Pyeloplasty [Total: 35 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~40 minutes

─── STAGE 1: PRESENTATION ───────────────────────────────
A 3-year-old girl undergoes left Anderson-Hynes pyeloplasty for symptomatic PUJ obstruction. The procedure was uncomplicated. On post-operative day 1, her drain output is 200 mL of clear fluid. On day 2, drain output increases to 350 mL. She has a temperature of 38.2°C.

**Q1a.** List three possible causes for the increased drain output and fever. **[3 marks]**
**Q1b.** State the investigations you would arrange to differentiate between these possibilities. **[4 marks]**

─── STAGE 2: DIAGNOSIS ──────────────────────────────────
Drain fluid creatinine is 800 μmol/L (serum creatinine 35 μmol/L). Urine output from the urethral catheter is low at 0.3 mL/kg/hour.

**Q2a.** What is the most likely diagnosis? **[2 marks]**
**Q2b.** Outline your immediate management plan. **[5 marks]**

─── STAGE 3: SUBSEQUENT COURSE ──────────────────────────
Conservative management (repositioning of catheter, maintaining drainage) results in resolution of the leak over 5 days. She is discharged. At 6 months, a follow-up US shows SFU grade III hydronephrosis (APD 18 mm) with pain on fluid challenge. MAG3 shows differential function 34% with an obstructed curve (type IV).

**Q3a.** Discuss the causes of failed pyeloplasty and how you would investigate this child. **[6 marks]**
**Q3b.** Describe the management options for recurrent PUJ obstruction after failed pyeloplasty, including success rates. **[6 marks]**

─── STAGE 4: MANAGEMENT DECISION ────────────────────────
You decide to proceed with a redo pyeloplasty.

**Q4a.** Outline the specific challenges of a redo pyeloplasty compared to a primary procedure. **[5 marks]**
**Q4b.** Describe the alternative options to redo pyeloplasty that you would discuss with the family. **[4 marks]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**FULL MARKING GUIDE:**

**Q1a.** [3 marks]
• Urine leak from anastomosis [1]
• Urinary tract infection [1]
• Drain is sumping peritoneal fluid/collections [1]

**Q1b.** [4 marks]
• Drain fluid creatinine (compared to serum) — confirms urine if >2x serum [1]
• Urine culture from drain and catheter specimen [1]
• Renal US to assess for urinoma, fluid collection, stent position [1]
• Blood tests: FBC, CRP, U&E [1]

**Q2a.** [2 marks]
• Urine leak from the pyeloplasty anastomosis (drain fluid:serum creatinine ratio >2) [1]
• Catheter may be blocked or malpositioned (low urethral output despite high drain output) [1]

**Q2b.** [5 marks]
• Check and flush urethral catheter; if blocked, change [1]
• Ensure catheter on free drainage [1]
• Consider percutaneous nephrostomy to divert urine and allow anastomosis to heal [1]
• IV antibiotics if febrile [1]
• Monitor drain output and serum creatinine daily [1]

**Q3a.** [6 marks]
• Causes: ischaemic stricture at anastomosis, residual/kinking at PUJ, missed crossing vessel, retained suture material, incomplete resection of adynamic segment [1-2 each]
• Investigation: MAG3 renogram to confirm obstruction [1]
• CT urogram/MR urogram to assess anatomy, length of stricture, and identify crossing vessels [1]
• Antegrade nephrostogram if nephrostomy in situ [1]

**Q3b.** [6 marks]
• Redo open pyeloplasty: 85-95% success; challenging due to scar tissue [2]
• Endopyelotomy: endoscopic incision of stricture (Acucise balloon, Ho:YAG laser); success ~60-75%, lower than redo [1]
• Balloon dilatation: less effective; 40-50% success [1]
• Ureterocalicostomy: ureter anastomosed directly to lower pole calyx — for failed pyeloplasty with significant pelvic reduction [1]
• Nephrectomy: if function <15% and symptomatic [1]

**Q4a.** [5 marks]
• Dense scar tissue makes dissection difficult [1]
• Higher risk of ureteric devascularisation [1]
• Shorter ureteric length for spatulation [1]
• Higher leak/re-stricture rate (5-10% vs 2-5% primary) [1]
• Longer operative time [1]

**Q4b.** [4 marks]
• Endopyelotomy (less invasive, lower success) [1]
• Ureterocalicostomy (if redo not feasible) [1]
• Long-term nephrostomy/stenting (if poor surgical candidate) [1]
• Nephrectomy (if negligible function) [1]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**DISTINGUISHING FEATURES:**
• Distinguishes urine leak from infection using drain fluid creatinine
• Knows specific success rates for redo pyeloplasty vs endopyelotomy
• Mentions ureterocalicostomy as a salvage option
• Includes conservative leak management before reintervention

## QUESTION 6 — EMQ: Upper Tract Obstruction Scenarios [Total: 10 marks] [BOARD]

**Theme: Investigation of Upper Tract Obstruction**

**OPTIONS (each option may be used once, more than once, or not at all):**

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| A. MAG3 renogram + diuretic | F. CT urogram |
| B. DMSA scintigraphy | G. MR urogram |
| C. MCU | H. Whitaker test |
| D. Renal US only | I. Intravenous urogram (IVU) |
| E. US + MCU | J. Laparoscopic exploration |

**For each of the following clinical scenarios, select the single most appropriate investigation:**

**1.** A 6-week-old with antenatal bilateral hydronephrosis (SFU III right, SFU II left). No ureteric dilatation. You need to assess function and drainage. **[1 mark]**

**2.** A 10-year-old with intermittent right flank pain after drinking. Three US and two MAG3 renograms are normal. You suspect a crossing vessel. **[1 mark]**

**3.** An 8-year-old with a failed pyeloplasty, nephrostomy in situ after a leak. You need to decide between redo pyeloplasty and nephrectomy by assessing residual function. **[1 mark]**

**4.** A 3-year-old with right SFU III hydronephrosis (APD 20 mm). A MAG3 renogram shows type II drainage with 45% function. Symptom-free. What investigation for follow-up? **[1 mark]**

**5.** A 5-year-old with SFU IV hydronephrosis and MAG3 type III (equivocal) drainage. The parents are anxious about the diagnosis and request a definitive answer about obstruction. **[1 mark]**

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**ANSWERS & RATIONALE:**

**1. A** — MAG3 renogram with diuretic. This is the gold standard for assessing differential function and drainage in infants with hydronephrosis. It provides both functional and drainage information in a single study.

**2. F** — CT urogram. The intermittent nature with normal renography between episodes suggests a crossing vessel. CT urogram with angiographic phase provides the best anatomical detail of the PUJ and surrounding vasculature.

**3. B** — DMSA scintigraphy. To assess residual function of the left kidney after a failed pyeloplasty. DMSA provides accurate differential function. If function is <15-20%, nephrectomy may be more appropriate than redo surgery.

**4. D** — Renal US only. This child has type II (dilated non-obstructed) drainage with preserved function and no symptoms. Serial US surveillance is appropriate. No need for repeat MAG3 unless US worsens.

**5. H** — Whitaker test. The equivocal renogram (type III) cannot definitively distinguish obstruction from stasis. The Whitaker test (antegrade pressure-perfusion) provides a definitive pressure reading: >20 cmH₂O = obstruction.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**COMMON ERRORS:**
• Ordering MAG3 renogram again when the question asks about crossing vessels (CT is superior)
• Not knowing the role of the Whitaker test for equivocal cases
• Repeating DMSA when US surveillance alone is sufficient for stable, asymptomatic type II drainage

• Candidates often treat all hydronephrosis as PUJ obstruction without considering the broader differential
• The O'Reilly classification is poorly recalled — many candidates know "obstructed curve" but cannot define the T½ thresholds
• Stented vs non-stented debate polarises candidates; strong candidates present both sides
• Redo pyeloplasty challenges are frequently underestimated
