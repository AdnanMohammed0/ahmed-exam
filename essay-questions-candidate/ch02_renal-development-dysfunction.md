## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Neonatal Renal Physiology

**Total: 20 marks | FOUNDATION | Time allowed: ~25 minutes**

### CLINICAL SCENARIO

A 2-day-old term neonate, born at 38 weeks' gestation weighing 3.2 kg, develops hyponatraemia (Na 128 mmol/L) on day 3 of life. Urine output over the preceding 24 hours was 1.5 mL/kg/hour. The infant is receiving intravenous fluids at 100 mL/kg/day of 0.45% NaCl with 10% dextrose. The paediatric team is concerned about fluid balance in the context of immature renal function.

### Question Parts

**(a)** Explain how glomerular filtration rate (GFR) changes from fetal life through the neonatal period, including the factors that limit GFR in the newborn. **[6 marks]**

**(b)** Describe the physiological basis for the impaired urine concentrating ability of the neonatal kidney and its clinical consequences. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Outline the normal changes in water and sodium homeostasis that occur in the first week of life, explaining why this infant is at risk of hyponatraemia. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** State the approximate daily sodium and potassium requirements in a term neonate, and explain why preterm infants have higher sodium requirements. **[4 marks]**

## LCV 2 — Progression to End-Stage Kidney Disease and Transplantation

**Total: 35 marks | DISTINCTION | Time allowed: ~40 minutes**

### STAGE 1 — PRESENTATION

A 14-year-old girl with a history of bilateral high-grade VUR diagnosed at age 2, and multiple febrile UTIs in childhood, is now noted to have declining renal function. Her current GFR is 22 mL/min/1.73 m² (Stage 4 CKD). Serum creatinine is 310 µmol/L. She is hypertensive (140/90 mmHg) despite three agents. She is anaemic (Hb 85 g/L), and her height is on the 5th centile. She feels tired and is struggling to concentrate at school.

**Q1a.** Outline your immediate medical management priorities. **[5 marks]**

**Q1b.** What is the significance of her nadir creatinine, and how does the trajectory of GFR inform prognosis? **[5 marks]**

### STAGE 2 — INVESTIGATION RESULTS

Her GFR continues to decline to 14 mL/min/1.73 m² over 6 months. She develops symptoms of uraemia. Transplant workup is initiated. She has a 38-year-old mother who is willing to donate.

**Q2a.** What are the advantages of a living donor (LD) kidney over deceased donor (DD) transplantation in children? **[4 marks]**

**Q2b.** Outline the pre-transplant workup including vaccination requirements. **[6 marks]**

### STAGE 3 — MANAGEMENT DECISION

She undergoes a pre-emptive LD renal transplant. Induction immunosuppression is given. She is commenced on a maintenance regimen including tacrolimus and mycophenolate mofetil.

**Q3a.** What are the components of induction immunosuppression, and what is the rationale for each? **[5 marks]**

**Q3b.** Discuss the common complications of immunosuppression in paediatric transplant recipients, including the risk of PTLD. **[5 marks]**

### STAGE 4 — COMPLICATION / FOLLOW-UP

Three months post-transplant, she develops a fever and her creatinine rises to 150 µmol/L. Transplant biopsy shows acute T-cell mediated rejection. She is treated with pulse methylprednisolone.

**Q4a.** Distinguish between T-cell mediated and antibody-mediated rejection in terms of mechanism, diagnosis, and management. **[5 marks]**
