## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Diagnosis of PUJ Obstruction [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 4-year-old boy is referred with a history of recurrent right-sided abdominal pain associated with nausea. The pain is colicky and worse after drinking large volumes. Urine dipstick is normal. His mother reports that he was investigated for antenatal hydronephrosis that "resolved" by age 1. Ultrasound shows right SFU grade III hydronephrosis with an APD of 25 mm and visible calyceal dilatation. The ureter is not dilated. Left kidney is normal.

**(a)** List the differential diagnoses for this presentation and state the most likely diagnosis with justification. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Outline the investigations you would arrange to confirm the diagnosis and assess functional significance, including the timing and sequence of each. **[6 marks]**

**(c)** A MAG3 renogram shows an obstructed drainage curve (O'Reilly type III-IV) with differential function of 38% on the right. Explain the different O'Reilly drainage curve patterns and the clinical significance of this result. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the concept of "equivocal" drainage on renography and the role of the Whitaker test in this setting. **[3 marks]**

**(e)** State the indications for surgical intervention in PUJ obstruction in children. **[3 marks]**

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Pyeloplasty Technique [Total: 20 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**

**(a)** Describe the patient positioning, incision, and approach for an open Anderson-Hynes pyeloplasty in a 6-month-old infant. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Outline the key steps of the dismembered pyeloplasty procedure, from mobilisation to anastomosis. **[6 marks]**

**(c)** Discuss the role of a nephrostomy tube and/or ureteric stent following pyeloplasty, including the evidence for stented vs non-stented repairs. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** Compare open, laparoscopic, and robotic pyeloplasty in children, including indications, advantages, and disadvantages of each approach. **[4 marks]**

## QUESTION 5 — LCV: Complicated Pyeloplasty [Total: 35 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~40 minutes

─── STAGE 1: PRESENTATION ───────────────────────────────
A 3-year-old girl undergoes left Anderson-Hynes pyeloplasty for symptomatic PUJ obstruction. The procedure was uncomplicated. On post-operative day 1, her drain output is 200 mL of clear fluid. On day 2, drain output increases to 350 mL. She has a temperature of 38.2°C.

**Q1a.** List three possible causes for the increased drain output and fever. **[3 marks]**
**Q1b.** State the investigations you would arrange to differentiate between these possibilities. **[4 marks]**

─── STAGE 2: DIAGNOSIS ──────────────────────────────────
Drain fluid creatinine is 800 μmol/L (serum creatinine 35 μmol/L). Urine output from the urethral catheter is low at 0.3 mL/kg/hour.

**Q2a.** What is the most likely diagnosis? **[2 marks]**
**Q2b.** Outline your immediate management plan. **[5 marks]**

─── STAGE 3: SUBSEQUENT COURSE ──────────────────────────
Conservative management (repositioning of catheter, maintaining drainage) results in resolution of the leak over 5 days. She is discharged. At 6 months, a follow-up US shows SFU grade III hydronephrosis (APD 18 mm) with pain on fluid challenge. MAG3 shows differential function 34% with an obstructed curve (type IV).

**Q3a.** Discuss the causes of failed pyeloplasty and how you would investigate this child. **[6 marks]**
**Q3b.** Describe the management options for recurrent PUJ obstruction after failed pyeloplasty, including success rates. **[6 marks]**

─── STAGE 4: MANAGEMENT DECISION ────────────────────────
You decide to proceed with a redo pyeloplasty.

**Q4a.** Outline the specific challenges of a redo pyeloplasty compared to a primary procedure. **[5 marks]**
**Q4b.** Describe the alternative options to redo pyeloplasty that you would discuss with the family. **[4 marks]**

**Theme: Investigation of Upper Tract Obstruction**

**OPTIONS (each option may be used once, more than once, or not at all):**

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| A. MAG3 renogram + diuretic | F. CT urogram |
| B. DMSA scintigraphy | G. MR urogram |
| C. MCU | H. Whitaker test |
| D. Renal US only | I. Intravenous urogram (IVU) |
| E. US + MCU | J. Laparoscopic exploration |

**For each of the following clinical scenarios, select the single most appropriate investigation:**

**1.** A 6-week-old with antenatal bilateral hydronephrosis (SFU III right, SFU II left). No ureteric dilatation. You need to assess function and drainage. **[1 mark]**

**2.** A 10-year-old with intermittent right flank pain after drinking. Three US and two MAG3 renograms are normal. You suspect a crossing vessel. **[1 mark]**

**3.** An 8-year-old with a failed pyeloplasty, nephrostomy in situ after a leak. You need to decide between redo pyeloplasty and nephrectomy by assessing residual function. **[1 mark]**

**4.** A 3-year-old with right SFU III hydronephrosis (APD 20 mm). A MAG3 renogram shows type II drainage with 45% function. Symptom-free. What investigation for follow-up? **[1 mark]**

**5.** A 5-year-old with SFU IV hydronephrosis and MAG3 type III (equivocal) drainage. The parents are anxious about the diagnosis and request a definitive answer about obstruction. **[1 mark]**
