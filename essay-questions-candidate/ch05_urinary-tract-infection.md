## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Imaging After Febrile UTI [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
An 18-month-old girl is admitted with her second culture-confirmed febrile urinary tract infection (temperature 39.5°C) within 4 months. She is now afebrile and clinically well after 48 hours of intravenous antibiotics. Urine culture grew E. coli >10⁵ CFU/mL, sensitive to trimethoprim. Renal ultrasound performed during the admission shows no abnormality.

**(a)** Outline the NICE (CG54) imaging guidelines for a child of this age with recurrent febrile UTI, including the rationale for each investigation recommended. **[6 marks]**

**(b)** Explain the technique and timing of DMSA scintigraphy in this context, and describe how you would differentiate acute pyelonephritic changes from established scarring. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** A DMSA performed 6 months later shows a photopenic area in the right upper pole. State the clinical significance of this finding and outline the evidence base for the management of renal scarring in children. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the controversy surrounding the use of prophylactic antibiotics following febrile UTI in children, with specific reference to the RIVUR and PRIVENT trials. **[6 marks]**

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Complicated UTI and Acute Pyelonephritis [Total: 20 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 5-year-old girl presents to the emergency department with a 3-day history of fever (39.8°C), vomiting, and right-sided abdominal pain. She appears unwell and dehydrated. Urine dipstick is strongly positive for leucocytes and nitrites. She has had one previous UTI treated as an outpatient at age 3. She has no known urinary tract abnormalities.

**(a)** List the clinical features that distinguish acute pyelonephritis from cystitis, and justify your admission decision in this case. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Describe your immediate management plan, including choice of antibiotics, fluid management, and the indications for switching from intravenous to oral therapy. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Outline the spectrum of organisms responsible for paediatric UTI according to age group, and discuss the emerging problem of antimicrobial resistance in this context. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** State how you would follow up this child after discharge, including investigations, prophylaxis decisions, and parental counselling regarding recurrence risk. **[5 marks]**

## QUESTION 5 — LCV: Recurrent Pyelonephritis [Total: 35 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~40 minutes

─── STAGE 1: PRESENTATION ───────────────────────────────
A 4-year-old girl is referred to paediatric urology clinic. She has had three culture-confirmed febrile UTIs in the past 12 months. Each episode required hospital admission for IV antibiotics. She is otherwise well between episodes but has chronic constipation (Bristol Stool Chart Type 1-2, bowel opening every 3-4 days) and is described as a "infrequent voider" by her mother.

**Q1a.** List the differential diagnoses you would consider and the key features of her history that narrow the differential. **[5 marks]**

**Q1b.** What questions would you ask to assess bladder bowel dysfunction (BBD) and how would you quantify it? **[5 marks]**

─── STAGE 2: INVESTIGATION RESULTS ──────────────────────
Renal ultrasound shows mild prominence of the right renal pelvis (APD 12 mm) without ureteric dilatation. Post-void bladder residual volume is 35 mL (estimated bladder capacity 250 mL). DMSA scan shows a photopenic area in the right upper pole with reduced differential function to 38%. MCU demonstrates right-sided grade III VUR.

**Q2a.** Interpret these findings and state how they change your management strategy. **[6 marks]**

**Q2b.** Discuss the role of endoscopic treatment for VUR in this child, including success rates, procedural technique, and factors influencing outcome. **[6 marks]**

─── STAGE 3: MANAGEMENT DECISION ────────────────────────
The parents are concerned about repeated infections and request "something definitive." They have heard about the Deflux injection. You discuss the options of conservative management, endoscopic injection, and open ureteric reimplantation.

**Q3a.** Outline the evidence for each option in this clinical scenario, including success rates, risks, and benefits. **[6 marks]**

**Q3b.** Justify your final recommendation and describe how you would counsel the family to reach a shared decision. **[3 marks]**

─── STAGE 4: FOLLOW-UP AND OUTCOME ──────────────────────
The family opts for endoscopic treatment. The child undergoes a successful Deflux injection (HIT technique). At 6-month follow-up, she has had no further UTIs. The residual VUR is grade I. She continues to struggle with constipation and infrequent voiding.

**Q4a.** Outline your ongoing management plan for this child, including the role of urotherapy, bowel management, and surveillance. **[4 marks]**
