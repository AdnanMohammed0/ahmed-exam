## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Antenatal Hydronephrosis Grading and Postnatal Protocol [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 28-year-old primigravida is referred to the foetal medicine unit at 20 weeks' gestation following detection of a bright kidney on the right with an anteroposterior renal pelvis diameter (APD) of 12 mm on routine anomaly scan. The left kidney has an APD of 5 mm. Amniotic fluid volume is normal. The parents are anxious and ask what this means for their baby after birth.

**(a)** Describe the SFU (Society for Fetal Urology) grading system for antenatal hydronephrosis, and classify this foetus using the system. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Explain the ESPU/ESPU antenatal hydronephrosis classification, including the role of APD thresholds and calyceal dilatation in determining severity. **[4 marks]**

**(c)** Outline the postnatal surveillance protocol you would recommend for this infant, including the timing and choice of investigations. **[6 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the natural history of antenatally detected hydronephrosis, including the likelihood of spontaneous resolution versus the need for surgical intervention, stratified by grade. **[5 marks]**

**(e)** Justify the role of prophylactic antibiotics in the neonatal period for infants with antenatal hydronephrosis, referencing current evidence and guideline recommendations. **[2 marks]**

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Counselling in Antenatally Detected Urological Anomalies [Total: 21 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 32-year-old woman at 22 weeks' gestation is referred to the paediatric urology team following detection of a multicystic dysplastic kidney (MCDK) on the left side of her foetus. The right kidney appears normal with adequate amniotic fluid volume. The parents are distressed and are considering termination of pregnancy. They have been told the baby "will have only one kidney."

**(a)** Explain the embryological basis of MCDK, including the genetic and molecular pathways involved. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Outline the typical ultrasound features that distinguish MCDK from severe hydronephrosis and from cystic renal disease. **[4 marks]**

**(c)** Counsel the parents regarding the prognosis for a unilateral MCDK with a normal contralateral kidney, including the expected postnatal management and long-term outcomes. **[6 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the indications for, and controversy surrounding, termination of pregnancy for prenatally diagnosed unilateral renal anomalies. **[4 marks]**

**(e)** State which additional investigations the parents should be offered to exclude associated anomalies, and give your reasons. **[2 marks]**

## QUESTION 5 — LCV: Prenatal Detection of Bilateral Hydronephrosis [Total: 35 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~40 minutes

─── STAGE 1: PRENATAL DETECTION ───────────────────────────────
A 26-year-old woman (G1P0) has a routine anomaly scan at 20 weeks' gestation. The foetus shows bilateral renal pelvis dilatation (APD 10 mm on the right, 8 mm on the left). The bladder appears normal and the amniotic fluid volume is normal. No other anomalies are detected. The parents are referred to you for counselling.

**Q1a.** What is your differential diagnosis for this finding, and how would you refine it using further imaging? List the key sonographic features that discriminate between the possibilities. **[6 marks]**

**Q1b.** Outline the key points you would include in your prenatal counselling for the parents at this stage. **[4 marks]**

─── STAGE 2: LATER PRENATAL COURSE ──────────────────────────
A repeat US at 30 weeks shows progression: right APD 18 mm with mild calyceal dilatation, left APD 14 mm. The bladder wall is thickened (5 mm). The posterior urethra appears dilated with a keyhole sign. Amniotic fluid volume is at the lower limit of normal.

**Q2a.** What is the likely diagnosis, and what additional prognostic information would you seek from the foetal US? **[4 marks]**

**Q2b.** The parents ask about foetal intervention. Discuss the evidence for and against vesicoamniotic shunting, including the specific findings from the PLUTO trial. **[6 marks]**

─── STAGE 3: POSTNATAL PRESENTATION ──────────────────────────
The infant is born at 39 weeks by normal vaginal delivery. Birth weight 3.4 kg. APGARs 8 and 9. The paediatric team performs a postnatal US on day 3, which confirms bilateral SFU Grade IV hydronephrosis, bilateral hydroureters, and a thickened bladder. A catheter is passed easily and 80 mL of urine drained. Serum creatinine at 48 hours is 68 μmol/L.

**Q3a.** What is your immediate management plan for this neonate? **[4 marks]**

**Q3b.** A MCU is performed and confirms PUV. Describe the technique of primary valve ablation, including cystoscopic approach, energy source, and steps to ensure adequate ablation. **[6 marks]**

─── STAGE 4: FOLLOW-UP AND COMPLICATIONS ─────────────────────
At 6 months, the child has had two febrile UTIs despite prophylactic antibiotics. Serum creatinine is steady at 55 μmol/L. Serial US shows persistent bilateral hydroureteronephrosis. Repeat MCU shows no residual valves. MAG3 renogram shows drainage with mild hold-up bilaterally.

**Q4a.** Discuss the concept of "valve bladder syndrome" and its management options. **[5 marks]**

## QUESTION 6 — EMQ: Prenatal Diagnosis of Urinary Tract Anomalies [Total: 10 marks] [BOARD]

**Theme: Evaluation of Antenatal Urological Findings**

**OPTIONS (each option may be used once, more than once, or not at all):**

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| A. Posterior urethral valves | F. Multicystic dysplastic kidney |
| B. PUJ obstruction | G. Autosomal recessive polycystic kidney disease |
| C. UVJ obstruction / primary megaureter | H. Normal variant / transient hydronephrosis |
| D. Duplex kidney with upper moiety obstruction | I. Cloacal anomaly |
| E. Ureterocele | J. Prune belly syndrome (triad syndrome) |

**For each of the following clinical scenarios, select the single most appropriate option from the list above:**

**1.** A 22-week foetus has a left-sided intrarenal cystic mass with multiple non-communicating cysts of varying sizes. The right kidney appears normal. Amniotic fluid volume is normal. The left renal artery cannot be identified on Doppler ultrasound. **[1 mark]**

**2.** A 28-week foetus has bilateral hydroureteronephrosis with a thickened bladder wall and a dilated posterior urethra. The amniotic fluid volume is moderately reduced. **[1 mark]**

**3.** A 24-week foetus has severe oligohydramnios. Both kidneys are symmetrically enlarged (above 95th centile) and markedly echogenic, with poor corticomedullary differentiation but no identifiable cysts at this stage. **[1 mark]**

**4.** A 20-week foetus has right-sided renal pelvis dilatation (APD 14 mm) without ureteric dilatation. The left kidney and bladder are normal. Amniotic fluid is normal. Repeat US at 28 weeks shows improvement (APD 8 mm). **[1 mark]**

**5.** A 32-week foetus has a left-sided duplex system. The upper moiety shows hydroureteronephrosis and there is a thin-walled cystic structure projecting into the bladder. **[1 mark]**

**6.** A 22-week foetus has massive bilateral hydroureteronephrosis with a large, distended, thin-walled bladder. The abdominal musculature appears deficient on US. The infant is male. **[1 mark]**
