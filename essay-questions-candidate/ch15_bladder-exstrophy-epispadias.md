## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Initial Management of Bladder Exstrophy [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A term male newborn is born with classic bladder exstrophy. The bladder template appears adequate. The pubic diastasis is 3.5 cm. The child is otherwise well from the resuscitation standpoint.

**(a)** Describe the immediate neonatal management of bladder exstrophy, including the care of the bladder template and the timing of surgical intervention. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Compare the modern staged repair of bladder exstrophy (MSRE) with the complete primary repair of bladder exstrophy (CPRE) approach. **[6 marks]**

**(c)** Describe the role of pelvic osteotomy in bladder exstrophy repair, including the optimal timing and techniques available. **[5 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the long-term continence expectations after successful bladder exstrophy closure, including the definition of "social continence" and the factors that influence it. **[6 marks]**

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Failed Bladder Closure [Total: 20 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 2-year-old boy underwent primary bladder closure for exstrophy in the neonatal period. He now presents with a complete dehiscence of the bladder closure. The bladder template is small and fibrotic. The pubic diastasis is 5 cm.

**(a)** List the causes of failed primary bladder closure in exstrophy. **[4 marks]**

**(b)** Outline the surgical options for redo closure, including the role of pelvic osteotomy. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Discuss the ongoing management of the failed bladder exstrophy, including the role of bladder augmentation and urinary diversion. **[6 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the psychological and social impact of repeated surgical interventions on the child and family, and the importance of multidisciplinary care. **[5 marks]**

Time allowed: ~40 minutes

─── STAGE 1: NEWBORN ──────────────────────────────────
A term male newborn has classic bladder exstrophy with a 4 cm pubic diastasis and an adequate bladder template.

**Q1b.** Outline the surgical plan for the first year of life. **[5 marks]**

─── STAGE 2: CHILDHOOD ───────────────────────────────

**Q4a.** Discuss the fertility options for a man with bladder exstrophy. **[4 marks]**

## QUESTION 6 — EMQ: Exstrophy Spectrum [Total: 10 marks] [BOARD]

**Theme: Bladder Exstrophy-Epispadias Complex**

**OPTIONS (each option may be used once, more than once, or not at all):**

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| A. Classic bladder exstrophy | F. Duplicate exstrophy |
| B. Male epispadias (penopubic) | G. Cloacal exstrophy |
| C. Female epispadias | H. Superior vesical fissure |
| D. Covered exstrophy | I. Exstrophy variant |
| E. Cloacal exstrophy | J. Pseudexstrophy |

**For each of the following clinical scenarios, select the single most appropriate diagnosis:**

**1.** A newborn has an exposed bladder plate, epispadias, and a wide pubic diastasis. The anus is normally placed and there are no other anomalies. **[1 mark]**

**2.** A newborn has two exstrophic bladder halves separated by an exstrophic cecal plate, a blind-ending colon, a hindgut remnant opening onto the cecal plate, and an omphalocoele. **[1 mark]**

**3.** A newborn boy has a dorsally placed urethral meatus at the base of the glans with mild dorsal chordee. The bladder is not exposed. The pubic diastasis is 1 cm. **[1 mark]**

**4.** A newborn has a dense fibrous band replacing the lower abdominal wall and symphysis pubis. The bladder is not exposed but the umbilicus is low-lying. The bladder neck is wide. **[1 mark]**

**5.** A newborn girl has a bifid clitoris and a short urethra that opens onto an exposed area between the pubic rami. The bladder is intact. **[1 mark]**

**EXAMINER'S NOTES — COMMON SCRIPT PATTERNS TO WATCH FOR:**
• Many candidates cannot name the specific surgical steps of Cantwell-Ransley or Young-Dees-Leadbetter
• The controversy between staged and complete primary repair is a popular viva topic
• Sexual function and fertility counselling are now expected knowledge, not "bonus" topics
• The role of pelvic osteotomy is frequently under-explained — candidates must state WHY it is needed
