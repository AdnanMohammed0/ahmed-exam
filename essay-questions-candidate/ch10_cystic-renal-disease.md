## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: ARPKD vs ADPKD [Total: 22 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 2-day-old term female neonate is noted to have bilateral flank masses on routine examination. She is normotensive but has mild respiratory distress requiring supplementary oxygen. Renal ultrasound shows symmetrically enlarged, echogenic kidneys (above 95th centile) with poor corticomedullary differentiation. Few small cysts are visible. Liver appears echogenic with bile duct dilatation. Parents are first cousins.

**(a)** What is the most likely diagnosis and what is the mode of inheritance? **[3 marks]**

**(b)** Describe the histological features of ARPKD and explain the genotype-phenotype correlation. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Compare ARPKD with ADPKD in terms of genetics, presentation in childhood, renal imaging features, and systemic manifestations. **[6 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the management of ARPKD presenting in the neonatal period, including the role of respiratory support, antihypertensives, and planning for renal replacement therapy. **[5 marks]**

**(e)** Outline the prenatal counselling points for parents who have had one child with ARPKD regarding recurrence risk and options for future pregnancies. **[3 marks]**

## QUESTION 2 — SEQ: Multicystic Dysplastic Kidney [Total: 20 marks] [BOARD]
Time allowed: ~25 minutes

**CLINICAL SCENARIO:**
A 6-week-old infant is referred for follow-up of an antenatally detected right-sided multicystic dysplastic kidney (MCDK). The left kidney appears normal with compensatory hypertrophy on postnatal US. The infant is thriving with no UTIs.

**(a)** Explain the embryological basis of MCDK and describe the typical ultrasound features that distinguish it from other cystic renal diseases. **[5 marks]**

**(b)** Outline the natural history of MCDK, including rates of spontaneous involution and the risk of complications such as hypertension and malignancy. **[5 marks]**

**(c)** Describe the recommended surveillance protocol for a child with unilateral MCDK, including the rationale for each investigation. **[4 marks]**

**(d)** Discuss the controversies surrounding routine follow-up for MCDK, including the arguments for and against lifelong surveillance. **[6 marks]**

## QUESTION 5 — LCV: Syndromic Cystic Disease [Total: 35 marks] [DISTINCTION]
Time allowed: ~40 minutes

─── STAGE 1: PRESENTATION ───────────────────────────────
A 4-year-old boy presents with a single episode of visible haematuria. He is otherwise well. US shows a 4 cm complex cystic lesion in the upper pole of the right kidney with septations and thickened walls. You note a hypopigmented patch (ash leaf macule) on his leg.

**Q1a.** What is the differential diagnosis for a complex renal cyst in a child? **[4 marks]**
**Q1b.** What syndromic diagnosis should you suspect, and what additional clinical features would you look for? **[4 marks]**

─── STAGE 2: INVESTIGATION ──────────────────────────────
CT confirms a Bosniak III lesion in the right upper pole. MRI brain shows subependymal nodules and cortical tubers. Family history: the mother has a history of seizures and was diagnosed with "benign renal cysts" at age 20.

**Q2a.** What is the genetic basis of this condition? **[3 marks]**
**Q2b.** Outline the renal manifestations of tuberous sclerosis complex (TSC) and their management. **[6 marks]**

─── STAGE 3: MANAGEMENT ─────────────────────────────────
The boy undergoes a right upper pole partial nephrectomy. Histology confirms angiomyolipoma with epithelioid features (a TSC-associated renal tumour).

**Q3a.** What post-operative surveillance is required for this child? **[4 marks]**
**Q3b.** Discuss the role of mTOR inhibitors (everolimus) in TSC-associated renal disease. **[5 marks]**

─── STAGE 4: FAMILY SCREENING ──────────────────────────
The mother is subsequently diagnosed with TSC. She also has bilateral renal angiomyolipomas; the largest is 5 cm with intralesional aneurysms.

**Q4a.** What is the risk of haemorrhage in angiomyolipomas and when is embolisation indicated? **[3 marks]**
**Q4b.** Discuss the autosomal dominant inheritance of TSC and the implications for other family members. **[4 marks]**

## QUESTION 6 — EMQ: Cystic Renal Disease [Total: 10 marks] [BOARD]

**Theme: Cystic Renal Disease — Presentation and Diagnosis**

**OPTIONS (each option may be used once, more than once, or not at all):**

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| A. ARPKD | F. Nephronophthisis |
| B. ADPKD | G. Simple renal cyst |
| C. MCDK | H. Cystic partially differentiated nephroblastoma |
| D. Tuberous sclerosis | I. VHL disease-associated cysts |
| E. Multiocular cystic nephroma | J. Acquired cystic disease (dialysis) |

**For each of the following clinical scenarios, select the single most appropriate diagnosis:**

**1.** A 6-week-old girl with bilateral massive flank masses. US shows symmetrically enlarged, echogenic kidneys (>95th centile) with poor corticomedullary differentiation. Liver shows ductal plate malformation. **[1 mark]**

**2.** A 2-week-old with multiple non-communicating cysts replacing the left kidney. The right kidney shows compensatory hypertrophy. Renal artery not visible on Doppler. **[1 mark]**

**3.** A 10-year-old boy with a 5-year history of polyuria, polydipsia, growth failure, and progressive CKD. US shows normal-sized kidneys with increased echogenicity and cysts at the corticomedullary junction. No liver disease. **[1 mark]**

**4.** A 3-year-old with a cystic renal mass that has multiple locales separated by septa. The septa enhance on CT. There are no solid components. No cutaneous or neurological features. **[1 mark]**

**5.** A 6-year-old with hypertension and a 4 cm cystic lesion in the right kidney. She has retinal angiomas. Family history of cerebellar haemangioblastoma in her father. **[1 mark]**
