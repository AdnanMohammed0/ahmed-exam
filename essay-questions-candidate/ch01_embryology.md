## QUESTION 1 — SEQ: Embryology of the Upper Urinary Tract

**Total: 22 marks | BOARD | Time allowed: ~25 minutes**

### CLINICAL SCENARIO

A 3-day-old term male infant is found to have a non-functioning right kidney on postnatal DMSA following antenatal detection of a multicystic dysplastic kidney (MCDK). The contralateral kidney shows compensatory hypertrophy. The parents ask why the kidney failed to develop normally.

### Question Parts

**(a)** Describe the embryological origin of the ureteric bud and metanephric mesenchyme, and explain the process of reciprocal induction that leads to nephrogenesis. **[6 marks]**

**(b)** List the key molecular signals involved in ureteric bud branching and nephrogenesis, naming at least three genes and their roles. **[4 marks]**

**(c)** Explain the embryological basis for the association between ureteric bud anomalies and renal dysplasia, including the histological features characteristic of a dysplastic kidney. **[6 marks]**

**(d)** Outline the embryological events underlying abnormalities of renal ascent and fusion, giving three examples with their approximate timing of origin. **[6 marks]**

## LCV 1 — Embryology and Clinical Consequences

**Total: 35 marks | BOARD | Time allowed: ~40 minutes**

### STAGE 1 — PRESENTATION

A 16-year-old female presents with primary amenorrhoea and cyclic lower abdominal pain of 6 months' duration. She has normal breast development (Tanner stage V) but scant pubic hair. On abdominal examination, there is a tender lower abdominal mass. Examination of the external genitalia reveals a normal clitoris, normal labia, but a blind-ending vagina of approximately 3 cm in depth. No uterus is palpable on rectal examination.

**Q1a.** What is the differential diagnosis? **[5 marks]**

**Q1b.** What embryological defect(s) could explain this presentation? **[5 marks]**

### STAGE 2 — INVESTIGATION RESULTS

Karyotype returns 46XY. Serum testosterone is in the normal adult male range. LH is mildly elevated. AMH is detectable. Renal ultrasound shows bilateral simple renal cysts. MRI pelvis shows absence of the uterus and a short blind-ending vagina. The gonads are located in the inguinal canals.

**Q2a.** What is the most likely diagnosis? Justify your answer. **[5 marks]**

**Q2b.** Explain the embryological mechanisms that explain the presence of breasts, the absence of a uterus, and the presence of testes. **[5 marks]**

### STAGE 3 — MANAGEMENT DECISION

The patient has been raised as female since birth and identifies as female. She is now requesting information about her long-term health, the risk of malignancy in her gonads, and whether she can bear children.

**Q3a.** Discuss the management options regarding the intra-abdominal gonads, including the risk of malignancy and timing of gonadectomy. **[5 marks]**

**Q3b.** Outline the options for vaginal reconstruction and the principles involved in creating a functional vagina. **[5 marks]**

### STAGE 4 — COMPLICATION / FOLLOW-UP

The patient undergoes bilateral gonadectomy at age 17. Histology shows normal testicular tissue with no evidence of malignancy. She begins hormone replacement therapy and decides to proceed with vaginal dilation.

**Q4a.** What are the principles of vaginal dilation therapy, and what success rates can she expect? Describe the surgical alternatives if dilation fails. **[5 marks]**
